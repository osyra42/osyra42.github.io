Urbex Safety

# 🏚️ Urbex Safety So you're going into a building you're not supposed to be in. I'm not going to lecture you about trespassing — if you're reading this, you've already made peace with that part. What I care about is you walking back out in one piece. Abandoned buildings are rotting, dark, full of sharp edges, and sometimes full of *people*. They do not care about you at all. These are the quick notes I'd give a friend before their first crawl — not a textbook, just the stuff that keeps you alive. > 💀 **The only rule that really matters:** if anything feels wrong — the floor, the air, the vibe, a sound you can't place — leave. No photo and no "just one more room" is worth dying somewhere nobody knows you went. --- # 🎒 What to Bring You don't need a tactical loadout. You need to not get cut, not breathe poison, and not get stuck in the dark. ### On your body - **Gloves** — every surface is rusted, splintered, or broken glass. Leather or cut-resistant. - **Real boots** — over the ankle, thick sole. Floors are full of nails and rebar, and a nail through a sneaker ends your day. - **Long sleeves and pants** — fiberglass, rust, and scrapes. Cover skin. - **A respirator or mask** — not optional in old buildings (see below). A fitted P100 if you can swing it, an N95 at the absolute least. - **Eye protection** — cheap safety glasses stop the dust and falling grit. - **A helmet, or at least a hard hat** — low beams and pipes live at exactly skull height in the dark. ### To see — this is the big one - **A headlamp** — hands-free and bright. Get one. - **Two backup lights** — a dead light deep in a dark building is an emergency, not an inconvenience. Bring a spare headlamp *and* a small flashlight, fresh batteries, plus spares. ### To get out and get help - **Phone + power bank** — fully charged, and assume zero signal inside. - **A whistle** — louder than your voice, and it still works when you're hurt and can't shout. - **A person** — the single best safety tool is a second human. More on that below. > 🪢 **Skip the rope work.** Climbing, rappelling, or hanging your weight on anything unless you're trained and using rated gear. Trusting a 40-year-old anchor point is how people die. --- # 🚪 Getting In Without Getting Hurt The opening is where you get cut. Slow down. - **Go through gaps, not glass.** Broken windows are guillotines — clear the frame completely or don't use it. - **Test before you trust.** Anything you grab or step on — rusted metal, rotten wood — assume it'll give way. - **Watch the threshold.** Missing floor right behind a doorway is a classic. Light it before you commit your weight. - **Don't drop into anything you can't climb back out of.** Easy in, impossible out, is a trap you set for yourself. --- # 🧭 Getting Out (Plan This First) Getting in is easy. Getting out fast, in the dark, maybe hurt — that's the part that kills people. - **Know your exit before you go deep.** Two if you can. The way you came in might be blocked on the way back. - **Leave a breadcrumb trail.** Photos of junctions, mental notes, even chalk. Big buildings turn you around fast. - **Don't explore to empty.** Keep light, battery, water, and energy in reserve for the trip back out. - **Tell someone outside.** Where you'll be, when you're back, and who to call if you're not — then actually check in. --- # 👤 When the Building Isn't Empty A lot of "abandoned" buildings aren't. People live in them — some just down on their luck, some using, some not in a good place. Most want nothing to do with you. The real danger is startling someone, cornering them, or getting cornered yourself. - **Make noise on the way in.** Don't creep. Let people hear you coming so nobody gets ambushed — surprise is dangerous for both of you. - **If a space is occupied, it's theirs.** Don't photograph people, don't push past, don't argue. Back out the way you came. - **Never block their exit, never let them block yours.** Keep a clear line to the door and don't explore yourself into a dead end. - **Watch for needles.** Especially around sleeping spots and bathrooms. Don't reach into anything you can't see into — a hidden needle is a real biohazard. - **Go in a group.** Two or three. Alone, with a bad encounter and no witnesses, is the worst case there is. - **Stay calm and keep your distance.** Most situations de-escalate fast if you're non-threatening and already leaving. - A bright handheld light, a loud whistle, and — where it's legal — pepper spray are reasonable. A weapon you're not trained with mostly just gets used on you. --- # ⚠️ The Building Wants to Hurt You Too Quick hits on the stuff that doesn't care how careful you are: - **Floors and stairs rot.** Step near the walls and joists, not the middle, and never trust a single board. - **The air can kill you.** Asbestos and lead dust hide in anything pre-1980s — that's what the respirator is for. Basements, tunnels, and tanks can hold gas that drops you in minutes with no smell. If it's confined and stale, stay out. - **Assume every wire is live.** Backup generators, illegal hookups, restored power — don't touch wiring, especially near water. - **Holes and shafts.** Elevator shafts and missing floor hidden under debris. Light every step; if you can't see the floor, assume there isn't one. - **Mold, droppings, and critters.** Rodent and bird droppings carry things you don't want in your lungs — one more reason for the mask. - **Weather flips the whole game.** Rain floods drains and tunnels fast and makes everything slick. Wind plus rooftops is a no. --- # ✅ Before You Go — The Short Version - Someone outside knows where you are and when you're back - Two lights minimum, plus backups, plus batteries - Gloves, boots, mask, eyes covered - Phone charged, power bank packed - Exits identified before you go deep - Not alone - The second it feels wrong, you leave Be smart, be quick, and be gone before anyone notices. Then come home. --- > ⚖️ *The boring footnote: entering property that isn't yours is trespassing just about everywhere, and "it looked abandoned" is not a legal defense. Forcing entry, taking anything, or wrecking the place turns a slap on the wrist into real charges. Whether you go is your call — this guide is only about not getting hurt.
