# Chapter 6 - The Combine

Transport was a black SUV with tinted windows and a driver who didn't speak. It felt less like a ride and more like an extradition. The coordinates led to a private airstrip where a twin-prop plane, unmarked and utilitarian, swallowed Osyra and two other recruits—a wiry man who kept cracking his knuckles and a woman with a thousand-yard stare who looked at nothing at all. They flew in silence, the drone of the engines a physical pressure in her sensitive ears. She took another pill with a sip of tepid water from a canteen. The chemical calm settled over her like a layer of clear ice.

They landed on a dusty strip carved into dense, evergreen forest. The air was thin, cold, and smelled of pine resin and damp earth. A concrete bunker of a building squatted at the tree line. A sign above the heavy double doors read: **TITAN REACH – ASSESSMENT & ACCLIMATIZATION FACILITY.**

Inside, it was all harsh fluorescence and echoing linoleum. A bored-looking sergeant at a kiosk took her name, scanned her retina, and slapped a plastic wristband on her. "Welcome to the Combine, cadet. Follow the green line. Don't wander. Don't touch anything."

The "green line" was a literal strip of luminous tape on the floor. It led through a maze of corridors. The place hummed with activity, but it was a sterile, purposeful kind—the clatter of gear, the murmur of voices from behind closed doors, the distant thump of what might have been gym equipment.

And everywhere, the stares.

The first cluster of recruits saw her as she was funneled into a larger holding area—a gymnasium converted into a processing hub with rows of temporary cots and locker bays. Maybe two dozen people, all in varying stages of intake, all hard-looking, all sizing each other up.

Her hood was up, but her tail was visible, and the tips of her ears poked through her hair. The murmurs started like a rustle of dry leaves.

"...the hell is that?"
"Check out the tail. No way."
"Costume party's that way, freak."

A big guy with a shaved head and a neck like a tree stump nudged his friend, a lanky redhead. "Look, a Fido. You lose your master, puppy?"

The redhead grinned. "Maybe she's here for the K-9 unit. Wrong door, Furry."

Heat rose up her neck, but it was the old, shameful kind, not the biological one. The pill kept that furnace banked. She kept her eyes on the green line, her face blank. *Don't react. Don't give them the satisfaction.* It was the same rule from every lab, every school hallway, every crowded street.

A staff member in grey fatigues appeared, clipboard in hand. "Osyra. With me. Custom fitting."

She broke from the line, feeling every eye bore into her back as she followed him out a side door. This became the rhythm of her day: brief periods in the common areas, punctuated by constant, solitary summons.

The gear fitting was in a small armory. A taciturn technician measured her with laser calipers, his brow furrowed. "Cranial circumference accounting for auricular structures… cervical spine to lumbar, note the caudal integration point… we'll need a custom helmet, modified body armor for range of motion and tail articulation." He muttered into a headset, "Yeah, send up the prototyping putty. It's the chimera candidate."

Back in the gym, a new group had arrived. The whispers were louder now, more confident.
"Ugly as sin. What kind of sick mod is that?"
"Bet it smells like wet dog."

She found a vacant cot in a corner and sat, pulling her knees to her chest, making herself small. The stares were a physical weight.

Then, a different summons. "Osyra. Medical Bay 3. Vaccine protocol."

In a stark white room, a doctor with a nervous smile consulted a thick digital file. "Ah, yes. Subject presents with a recombinant retro-viral integration. Standard immunoboosters are contraindicated. We'll be administering a tailored series to avoid triggering an autoimmune response against the non-human cellular lines." The series of shots burned, a deep, aching cold that spread from the injection sites.

Each time she returned to the gym, the hostility had crystallized. She was no longer just a curiosity; she was a target. A focus for the universal anxiety of the place. Her difference was a language they all understood, and they spoke it with cruelty.

"Hey, Mutant," the big guy, Stumps, called as she walked back from medical, her arm throbbing. "They giving you your rabies shot? Should be mandatory."

A few chuckles rippled around him. She kept walking, her tail rigid, her ears flat inside her hood.
