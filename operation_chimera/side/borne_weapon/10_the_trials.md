# Chapter 10 - The Trials

The gas chamber was a small, concrete-block building that smelled, even from the outside, like dread and old chemicals. They lined up in their protective masks, the tight rubber seals feeling alien against her face, pressing her ears flat. The DI, Sergeant Hale, stood by the door, his own mask making him look like a giant, unfeeling insect.

"This is CS gas. Tear gas. It will not kill you. It will make you wish you were dead. The point is to trust your gear, and to learn how to clear and seal your mask under stress. You will enter, form a line, and on my command, you will break the seal, remove your mask, state your name and rank, then reseal. Anyone who runs for the door before I say 'go' repeats. Anyone who doesn't complete the exercise repeats. Understood?"

"YES, SERGEANT!"

One by one, they filed into the thick, hazy air inside. Even with the mask on, her eyes began to water. The air had a sharp, metallic taste that seeped through. Then came her turn.

"RENAYA! BREAK SEAL AND REMOVE!"

She pulled the mask from her face.

The world dissolved into instant, agonizing fire. It was like inhaling crushed glass and hornets. Her lungs seized, a raw, scraping cough tearing from her throat. Her eyes flooded, vision blurring into a smear of grey walls and green uniforms. It was exactly as bad as it was for everyone else in the room—no worse, no special weakness the lab had built into her, just the same chemical hell democratically applied. If anything it sat in her sinuses and the back of her throat a little longer, her sharper senses slower to stop screaming about it. But it was the great equalizer: in here, she was just one more body drowning in its own mucus.

"NAME AND RANK!" Hale bellowed from somewhere in the burning fog.

She tried to suck in air to speak, and got a mouthful of fire. "RENAYA—!" she choked out, her voice a strangled rasp before a coughing fit doubled her over. Snot poured from her nose, thick and uncontrollable. All around her, the sounds were the same: retching, gagging, the wet, desperate hacking of fifty people drowning in their own mucus. The mysterious respiratory plague that had haunted the bay was instantly, violently cured. No one was congested anymore; every passage was ruthlessly, painfully clear.

"SEAL YOUR MASK!"

She fumbled the rubber back over her face, her fingers slick with tears and snot, and sucked in the sweet, filtered air. The relief was instantaneous, though the burning lingered for another hour, the way it did for everyone. They stumbled out into the clean, cold daylight, faces raw and shining, all of them united in a new, profound understanding of misery. For a few minutes, no one looked at her. They were too busy trying to remember how to breathe.

***

Night ops came a week later. They were issued night-vision goggles—heavy, bulky units that turned the world into a ghostly green landscape of vague shapes and halos. They practiced moving in squads, communicating in whispers, the gear hissing and whining in their ears.

She tried to use them. She really did. But the amplified light was overwhelming, washing out details, and the limited field of view felt like looking through two foggy soda bottles. It was harder to see with them than without.

During a navigation exercise, moving through a dense patch of woods, she finally lifted the goggles onto her forehead. The world didn't just brighten; it clarified. Her improved night vision rendered the darkness not as an obstacle, but as a different palette. The deep shadows held texture and depth. She could distinguish the grey of a rock from the grey of a root, see the subtle path of crushed leaves, track the heat-shimmer of a recruit's body ten yards away as a faint, rippling disturbance in the cool air. Her pupils, wide and dark, gathered every stray photon. She could hear the lieutenant's whispered commands from two squads over with perfect clarity. She navigated the terrain not by stumbling through a green electronic haze, but by reading the real night as if it were a cloudy twilight.

"Renaya, why are your NODs stowed?" a voice hissed. It was Corporal Vance, one of the training cadre.

"They degrade my natural sight, Corporal. My night vision is sufficient."

A long pause. She could feel his stare in the dark, but he couldn't see her face clearly. "Noted. Keep up. And don't make me regret this."

She didn't make him regret it. By the end of the exercise, she was quietly directing her squad around obstacles she could see and they couldn't, her whispers keeping them from blundering into ditches or low branches. No one thanked her. But when they reassembled, their squad was the only one without a sprained ankle or a face full of spiderwebs. The looks she got were no longer of fear or disgust, but of grim, practical acknowledgment.

***

The PT tests were where things began to shift, almost imperceptibly. The tests were straightforward: a two-mile run, maximum push-ups in two minutes, maximum sit-ups in two minutes, pull-ups. For her, they were laughably easy. Her body, built for endurance and power, performed with a cold efficiency that even she found unsettling.

But as she stood panting lightly after her run, watching others stagger across the finish line, faces purple with strain, something strange happened. She didn't feel smug. She felt… impatient.

"Come on, Miller! You've got fifty yards! Dig! It's just pain, leave it on the road!" The words were out of her mouth before she thought about them. Miller, a wiry guy who was usually one of the mockers, shot her a look of pure shock, then grimaced and found a last, desperate sprint.

On the pull-up bar, she watched Carter fail on his seventh rep, his arms shaking. "Don't let your knees drift, Carter! Lock your core! You've got three more in you, I know you do!" He grunted, heaved, and got an eighth.

It wasn't kindness. It was a brutal sort of pragmatism. They were a cell in an organism. If the cell next to her was weak, it made the whole body weaker. Her own performance was irrelevant if the platoon failed. The DIs had beaten that into them. And for the first time, she internalized it not as a threat, but as a mechanic of survival.

Slowly, tentatively, she became able to lead. Not by appointment, but by default. During ruck marches, when someone's pack strap was digging in, she'd bark the adjustment drill sergeant Hale had taught them. During the endless, mind-numbing parade drills, when the rank would lose its dressing, her height and voice from the flank would call the alignment back. "Dress right, DRESS! Hold your interval, Garcia!"

The most profound comfort was the silence from the DIs. They never blamed her for being different. They only ever blamed the others for *noticing*. "YOU STARING AT HER FEET TO LEARN THE CADENCE, JENKINS? EYES FRONT!" That simple, consistent reinforcement was a balm she hadn't known she needed. Her existence was not the problem. Their lack of focus was.

They did ruck marches that felt like they would never end, the weight of the Mk 13 an old, familiar enemy on her shoulder. They did hand-to-hand combat in the gritty pits, where her reach and speed became assets she learned to control, not just flail. They did parade drills until the movements were autonomic, her tail held in a tight, still curl against her leg. They did a form of camping they called "bivouacking," which was mostly lying in a cold, damp hole in the ground trying not to shiver, listening to the forest with ears that heard every rustle and snap.

She was not becoming one of them. That was never the goal. The red-tape seat on the bus still existed. But the space around her was no longer a vacuum of hostility. It was a space of… function. She was a unique tool in the kit, and the other tools were slowly, grudgingly, learning how to work alongside her. And she, to her own surprise, was starting to care if they could do their jobs.

She was losing her self-conscious mindset. The constant, withering internal narration of *freak, monster, mistake* was being drowned out by the external, immediate demands of the task: *Move faster. Shoot straighter. Carry more. Help them.*

The beast in her blood, the one that had driven her to a stranger on a rooftop, was being channeled. Given a direction. Given a pack, however dysfunctional.

It was a different kind of cage. But for the first time in her life, the walls weren't there just to contain her. They were there to shape her into something that could, perhaps, eventually break out.
