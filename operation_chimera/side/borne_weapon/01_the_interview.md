# Chapter 1 - The Interview

"Hey. My name is Osyra. This is my video application."

She held up a piece of paper. It bleached the screen, a stark rectangle in the dark of her room. The hood of her sweatshirt was up; the light from her monitor glinted off her round glasses and the curve of her cheekbone, leaving the rest of her face in deep shadow.

"I'm told someone put my name forward," she said. "That you already know the broad strokes, and you'd rather hear the rest from me. Fine."

"I was given up as a baby. My parents had nothing—I was one mouth too many. An aunt took me in and raised me until she died, and after that it was foster care, one house after another. Until I was sixteen. Then the man who was supposed to be keeping me sold me to a facility."

She paused for a long moment. She looked down, a sweep of dark hair falling past her glasses, then back into the lens, her expression flat.

"They ran illegal experiments. Hybridization—splicing animal into human and seeing what lived. I was a line item. A body nobody who mattered would come looking for."

"They had charts of my development, my reflexes, my cellular response. Most of the others didn't survive what they were given. I did. When the place burned down I walked out, and I lived in the woods like an animal for the better part of two months—taking what I needed, answering to no one. Long enough to learn I could."

She took a slow, steadying breath, her eyes—a sharp, unnatural red even in the low light—locked on the camera. Her hands, which had been resting on the desk, came up. They grasped the sides of the hood of her brown jacket.

"This is what they made."

In one fluid motion, she pulled the hood back. Her long black hair spilled out. And there, rising from the crown of her head, alert and velvety-black, were a pair of pointed wolf ears. They twitched once, as if adjusting to the cool air of the room.

"Their serum. Chimera 9."

A slight, wry smile touched her lips, devoid of any warmth.

"I believe this covers the 'unique biological modifications' bullet point from your email."

The video ended. The final frame held for a moment: her red eyes staring out from the screen, the dark fur of her wolf ears stark against her black hair. The image blinked out, replaced by two words on a black background: UPLOAD COMPLETE.

She sat back in her chair. The room was silent except for the faint hum of her computer. The glow of the monitor was the only light, painting the edges of her hood, her desk, the empty mug. Five minutes passed. She didn't move.

Then, a soft, digital chime. A new notification slid into the corner of her screen.

She clicked it. The darkness of her room was replaced by the bright white of a new email. The text was crisp, impersonal. She read it, her eyes tracing each clinical line.

FROM: Miranda Vasquez, MD <m.vasquez@titanreach.com>
SUBJECT: RE: Video Application - Verification Required
SENT: Tonight, 21:17

Applicant Osyra,

Your submission has been received and reviewed.

The nature of the presented physiological claims necessitates a preliminary verification to rule out digital or practical artifice before formal consideration can proceed.

You are required to attend a brief, live video conference at 09:00 tomorrow (your local time). You must present yourself exactly as you appeared in your submitted recording—same environment, same attire. You will be asked to perform simple visual confirmations at my direction.

Confirmation of your attendance is required within the hour.

If verification is satisfactory, you will be scheduled for a comprehensive clinical evaluation at our facility. This on-site evaluation serves as the final stage of the interview process.

This is a procedural necessity.

Miranda Vasquez, MD
Medical Screening Director
Titan Reach Consulting

Below her signature was a block of text: a secured Zoom link and an alphanumeric passcode. The characters looked sharp, final. A set of digital keys to a door she had just asked someone to open.

She stared at the words *procedural necessity*. Her tail, curled around the leg of her chair, gave a single, slow twitch. The email offered no greeting, no reaction to what she had shown them. Just the next step. The next test.

Her fingers hovered over the keyboard. The cursor blinked in the reply field. Outside her window, the city lights glowed, indifferent.

She hit reply. She typed a single word.

Confirmed.

She sent it.

The screen dimmed to black again, leaving only the faint, green pulse of the power light on her computer, like a slow, steady heartbeat in the dark.

***

She didn't sleep much. The kind of tired that sits behind the eyes and hums in the bones. Her ears kept tuning into the wrong things—the drip of a tap, the scuttle of something in the walls, the low-frequency thrum of the city that never stops. When she did drift off, it wasn't into dreams, but into a replay of sensations: the cold press of a stethoscope, the bite of a needle, a scientist's voice noting observations in a flat, delighted tone. She woke before her alarm, the grey dawn light seeping around the edges of her blinds. Her hood was still up.

At 08:55, she was back in her chair. The dark room. The computer's glow. She had cleared her desk, leaving only the monitor and the camera. She clicked the link.

The Zoom window opened. It was just her in a small square, and a larger, empty black square labeled 'Dr. Vasquez – Titan Reach.' No picture. No voice. For a full minute, there was nothing. Then her square lit up.

She was not what Osyra had expected. Younger. Sharp features, dark hair pulled into a severe bun, glasses that reflected the light of her own screen so her eyes were invisible. She wore a white lab coat over a black turtleneck. Her background was a blur of sleek, grey office.

"Osyra," she said. Her voice was the same as her email. Cool. Precise. A diagnostic tool. "Confirm your environment is identical to that of your submission."

Osyra panned her camera slowly, showing the dark walls, the empty desk. "It is."

"Good. Posture, please. Sit up straight. Remove your hood."

Her hand hesitated for a fraction of a second at the edge of the fabric. Then she pulled it back. Her hair fell loose. Her ears, freed, pivoted forward, tensing toward the speaker of her laptop. She saw Vasquez's gaze lock onto them on her screen. The doctor didn't comment.

"Shoulders," she commanded. "I need to observe the integration at the trapezius and cervical spine. Rotate them. Forward, then back in a full, slow circle."

It felt absurd. Like a warm-up for a workout video. But Osyra did it. She rolled her shoulders forward, feeling the muscle move under her skin, then back in a wide circle. Her tail, out of frame, flicked once in irritation.

"Again. Slower."

She complied. The silence on the doctor's end was total, except for the faint tap of a keyboard.

"Now, touch your right ear. The outer cartilage. Pinch it gently and pull it forward. Hold it."

A hot flash of humiliation burned in her chest. This was worse than the pointless shoulder rotations. This was a direct, deliberate manipulation of the part of her that was most *not human*. Her hand felt clumsy as she reached up. The fur was soft, the cartilage springy under her fingers. She pulled it forward. It felt grotesque, like showing a wound.

"Hold for five seconds. Release. Now the left."

She did it. Her ears flattened back against her skull of their own accord the moment she let go.

"Eyes," she said. "Look directly into the camera. Without moving your head, track my finger."

A slender finger entered the frame of the doctor's video, moving slowly left, then right, then up, then in a diagonal. Osyra's eyes followed. The red of her irises must have been blazing in the dark-room feed.

"Pupillary response appears normal. Non-standard pigmentation confirmed," Vasquez murmured, more to herself than to Osyra. "Final check. Bare your teeth for the camera. Canines specifically."

Osyra pulled her lips back. A snarl she didn't feel. The points of her teeth caught the light.

A pause. More typing.

"Satisfactory," Dr. Vasquez said finally. The word wasn't praise. It was a stamp. "The preliminary verification is complete. We are authorizing the in-person clinical."

She leaned slightly closer to her camera. "Report to the Titan Reach biomedical facility on 12th Avenue. Can you be there for a full-body physical evaluation at 14:00 today?"

Two o'clock. Today. No time to think. No time to back out.

"It's a comprehensive panel. Blood work, advanced imaging, stress-testing of the modified physiognomy. Consider it a freshening up of your baseline data before field consideration."

Field consideration. The words hung there. Her ticket out. Her leash.

"Is 14:00 acceptable?" she repeated, a hint of steel in the calm.

Osyra's throat was dry. She managed a single, stiff nod.

"Verbal confirmation, please."

"Yes," she said, the word scraping out. "Two is fine."

"Good. An appointment confirmation and pass for the front desk will be sent to this email. Do not be late."

The doctor's video square went black. The meeting ended.

Osyra was left staring at her own reflection in the dark monitor. Her ears were still pinned back. Her shoulders ached from the pointless rotations. The humiliating, precise commands still echoed in the quiet room.

They had just wanted to see if the puppet's strings were attached. Now, they wanted to see how much weight it could hold.

The clock on her screen read 09:07. She had less than five hours until she reported to her next examination.

***

The lobby of the Titan Reach biomedical facility was all muted steel and frosted glass. It smelled of industrial cleaner and recycled air. The woman at the desk scanned Osyra's pass with a bored expression, handed her a stack of forms and a clear plastic cup, and pointed down a hall. "First door on the left. Fill the cup halfway, leave it in the wall cabinet. Then change into the gown in the waiting area. Open in back."

The first room was small and brutally functional. A toilet, a sink, a metal cabinet with a numbered slot. The instructions were laminated. She did as she was told, the ritual humiliating in its banality. In the adjacent waiting area, a curtained alcove held a thin cotton gown. She shed her clothes, her ears flattening at the exposure. The gown did nothing to cover her tail, which she had to thread through the special opening, the tip twitching nervously against her calf.

The main waiting room had a few other people in similar gowns. Two guys, maybe early twenties, sat slouched in plastic chairs. They had the close-cropped hair and wary stillness of military recruits.

"…they say it's just depth perception," one muttered, not looking at the other. "But my cousin went through, he said they're checking for latent psychic resonance. The 'third eye' test, he called it."

"Bull," the other grunted, picking at his thumbnail. "It's micro-expressions. They're weeding out sociopaths. Friend of a friend failed it, got flagged for 'empathy deficiency.'"

Osyra slid into a chair as far from them as possible, pulling her gown tighter. Their conspiracy theories were just nervous chatter, but in this sterile place, they felt like fragments of a stranger, more paranoid truth. A truth that had nothing on hers. She kept her head down, the hood of her hair a poor curtain.

A nurse with a tablet called her name. She didn't blink at the ears. "Osyra. This way."

She led her into a long, brightly lit hallway with multiple doors. This was the processing line. The first stop was a tiny booth where a technician took her blood pressure, then drew vial after vial of blood, the glass filling with dark liquid. "Hydration scan next," she said, handing Osyra a towel for the crook of her arm.

The scan room was cold. She stood on a platform while a ring of light passed over her. "Arms out, please. Hold still." A digital hum, a flicker on a monitor showing a wireframe of her body. The technician's eyes darted from the screen to her head, her tail, back to the screen. He said nothing.

From there, it was the coordination room. A different nurse, younger, with Dr. Vasquez observing from the corner, her arms crossed. "Please walk heel-to-toe along this line." She did. "Now, on your toes only." She rose, her tail extending for perfect balance. "Now your heels." A shuffle. "Good. Now, touch your finger to your nose. Eyes closed." Her finger found its mark unerringly. Her hearing tracked the slight shift of Vasquez's weight on the floor.

"Now," Vasquez said, her voice cutting through the quiet. "Jump. From a standing start. As high as you can."

Osyra bent her knees and pushed off. The ceiling tiles rushed at her face. She came down heavy, knees folding to take the load—a solid, audible *thud* that you felt through the platform. Not graceful. A big frame coming back to earth and the floor knowing about it.

"Ground reaction force must be exceptional," the young nurse breathed.

"Proceed," Vasquez snapped.

It was a blur of small rooms and specific, bizarre commands. A hearing booth where she had to raise a hand at tones so high the technician kept checking his equipment. A vision test where she read lines of letters, then had to identify shapes on a depth-perception panel. "Now track the light without moving your head." The penlight moved; her eyes followed, her ears unconsciously angling toward the motion. She heard Vasquez make a note.

Finally, she was led into a larger examination room. It held an exam table, an ultrasound machine, and a tray of instruments. Dr. Vasquez entered, followed by a younger doctor, Chen, who couldn't stop staring.

"Ms. Osyra," Vasquez said. "We'll begin the musculoskeletal and specific integument assessment."

It was an hour of directed movement. "Full spinal extension. Hold." She arched back. "Plantar flexion to maximum." She rose onto her toes. "Now, rapid directional changes. Follow the laser." For ten minutes, she lunged and pivoted after a red dot, the paper on the floor shredding under her bare feet. Her body performed with a cruel efficiency. Just like the lab's logs. *Page 42: 'Subject demonstrates exceptional lumbar pliability...'*

Chen approached with a handheld scanner. "May I?" he asked, hovering near her ears. She nodded stiffly. He scanned. "Dense cartilage, fully integrated." He produced a clicker. *Click.* Behind her. Her left ear swiveled back instantly. *Click.* To the side. Both ears pinned the location. Humiliation burned hot under her skin.

"Auditory range is significantly extended," Chen reported.

"Eyes," Vasquez commanded. The bright ophthalmoscope light hit her. She stared into it, letting the red in her irises burn. "Pigmentation is structural. Probable superior low-light vision."

"Tail mobility and strength."

This was the worst. She had to lift a small weight with it, swish it through specific arcs, demonstrate its counterbalance during a one-legged stance. The muscles at the base of her spine burned with a familiar shame.

"Fascinating. A fully functional mechanism," Chen murmured to Vasquez, as if she were a piece of engineering.

After what felt like an eternity of pokes, scans, and measurements, Vasquez gave a curt nod. "The physical is concluded. We will now move to the final portion of the evaluation." She glanced at Chen. "Doctor, you may go. Send in Dr. Ellen."

Chen left, and a moment later, a woman in her forties entered. She had a calm, competent air and carried a new tablet. "Hello, Osyra. I'm Dr. Ellen. This part is standard for all applicants, but given your unique physiology, we need to establish a complete reproductive baseline."

The dread was immediate and cold. This was the part their notes had been most feverish about. *Cyclical stability. Hormonal integration.*

She began with a standard physical exam—heart rate, palpation of the abdomen. Her hands were professional, but when she pressed near Osyra's ovaries, a jolt went through her, a deep internal resonance. She paused, her gaze sharpening. "Interesting."

The internal ultrasound was next. The cold gel, the intrusive probe. Osyra stared at a crack in the ceiling tile. On the screen, grey shapes swirled—her own insides, looking alien.

"Uterine structure appears normal," Dr. Ellen said quietly. "Ovarian activity is… significant. Follicular development is advanced."

She removed the probe and sat on a stool, facing her. Her expression was neutral, open. "Osyra, I need you to describe your reproductive cycle for me. In your own words. What do you experience?"

The question was a blade. Osyra had never had to say it out loud. "It's not a period," she started, the distinction clawing its way out. "It runs on a different clock. Once a year, in the fall—same stretch every time. I can mark it on a calendar. And it comes in stages."

"Walk me through them."

She kept her voice flat, like reading a report off a screen. "First I can't settle. Don't sleep. Everything gets too loud, too bright, too much—my own skin feels like it's listening. I get short with people. There's some bleeding, early on, a few days of it." A muscle moved in her jaw. "Then it sharpens into a pull. Toward—" She stopped. Made herself finish. "Toward closeness. Contact. For a few days I want it badly enough that I don't trust myself with it. Then it breaks and I crash—wrung out for a week. After that it's gone for another year."

She nodded, typing. "A seasonal estrous cycle. Photoperiod-triggered—your body reads the shortening daylight and runs the program on schedule. Textbook canid, grafted onto a human frame. The serum rewired your hypothalamic-pituitary axis." She leaned forward slightly. "Predictable, at least. That helps. But that drive window is a profound liability in the field if it isn't managed."

She wasn't judging. She was assessing a flawed asset. "Given Titan Reach's operational parameters, we will provide a regimen of hormonal regulators. Non-negotiable for field readiness. The prescription and a monitoring kit will be couriered to you."

A chemical leash for the biological one. To make her docile, predictable.

"Understood," Osyra said, the word ash.

Dr. Ellen stood. "Your body is operating on their schedule, Osyra. We're just helping you keep time." She gave a professional nod and left.

Dr. Vasquez re-entered, looking from her clipboard to Osyra. Her clinical detachment had hardened into verification. Conquest. "The Chimera 9 expression is valid and fully integrated. You have passed the final interview."

Osyra dressed in the empty curtained alcove, yanking her hoodie on, pulling the hood up the second it cleared her head. The clinic's smell was in her nose, on her skin. No relief. Just a hollow echo.

Passed. She had graduated from the lab to a bigger, better-funded cage. The tests were over.

Somehow, that felt like the worst possible outcome.

***

A week passed. The couriered package arrived: a blister pack of pills and a clinical-grade basal thermometer. She left it on her kitchen counter, untouched. A tiny rebellion. Every itch, every heightened smell, she monitored with a dread that curdled into a perverse defiance. *This is still mine.*

The drive window came two weeks after the exam, right on the calendar she'd never needed to write down. It wasn't a surprise; it was an ambush she'd chosen. The restlessness she'd carried for days sharpened, by dusk, into something with claws—a wire pulled taut, a pull she couldn't sit still under. Her apartment was a torture chamber of amplified sensation—the fridge motor a jackhammer, the scent of her own skin sour and overwhelming. The courier's pills gleamed mockingly from the counter. Taking them felt like letting them win. Not taking them felt like insanity.

The restlessness was a live wire. She couldn't be contained. She fled to the only space left: the roof.

The night air was cool against skin that ran too hot and too aware. The city sprawled below, a map of smells and distant sounds. She paced the gravel, her tail lashing, her ears swiveling at every distant siren, every shout from the streets. The need was a physical pressure, a hollow, pulling throb deep in her core. It was maddening.

That was when she caught his scent.

It cut through the chemical haze of the city—clean soap, fresh linen, and underneath, something warm and vital. Human, but… distinct. Compelling. Her head snapped up. He was at the far end of the roof, leaning against the parapet, looking out at the skyline. Just a silhouette.

Her body moved before her mind could catch up. The need focused, honed in on that scent like a beacon. She was across the roof without deciding to go, the gravel crunching under her boots.

He turned as she approached. He was tall, with kind eyes that widened in surprise, then cautious curiosity as he took her in—the hood, the ears visible in the moonlight, the agitated tail. Most people ran. Or screamed. He just… watched.

His eyes moved from her pinned ears to her glassy, too-bright gaze, and his expression shifted from curiosity to a dawning, careful comprehension. He didn't look at her ears like they were costumes, but like they were… symptoms. "You're… in some kind of trouble," he stated softly. It wasn't a question about what she was, but about the state she was in.

"You shouldn't be up here alone," she said, her voice a rough scrape. It wasn't a warning. It was a confession.

"Neither should you," he replied. His voice was calm. A deep, steady sound that vibrated in her chest. "I'm Leo. I live downstairs."

She stopped a few feet away, close enough that his scent washed over her, spicy and real. The animal part of her screamed to press closer, to rub her cheek against his jacket, to taste the salt on his skin. She locked her knees to stay still.

"Osyra," she managed.

He nodded, his gaze dropping to her twitching ears, then back to her eyes. He didn't flinch from the red. He saw the wound-up thing behind her gaze, the tremor that wasn't from cold. He glanced from her pleading eyes to the city far below, and something in his face softened with decision, not fear.

"What do you need?" he asked, the question simple and devastating.

She didn't have an answer that wasn't primal, instinctual. *Contact. Pressure. You.*

Instead, she reached out, slowly, giving him every chance to pull back. Her fingertips brushed the back of his hand where it rested on the concrete ledge. The spark of contact was electric, a jolt that straightened her spine. His skin was cool. Her whole world narrowed to that point of connection.

He turned his hand, palm up. An invitation. Or a surrender.

She slid her fingers against his, twining them together. His grip was firm, steadying. The simple touch was a floodgate. The frantic, scattered energy in her coalesced, flowed toward that anchor point. The painful edge of the need blunted, not gone, but… channeled. Focused.

They stood there in the dark, hand in hand, not speaking. The city noise faded to a murmur. Her breathing slowed to match his. Her ears, which had been flicking at every sound, relaxed, rotating gently toward the rhythm of his heartbeat. Her tail stilled, curling softly around her own ankle.

It wasn't passion. Not yet. It was something more fundamental. A quieting. A recognition. The beast in her blood, frantic and howling, saw him and… settled.

He lifted their joined hands, his thumb stroking a slow, deliberate line across her knuckles. The gesture was unbearably gentle. Human.

"Is this okay?" he whispered.

She leaned her forehead against his shoulder, hiding her face in the clean linen scent of his shirt. She nodded.

For the first time since the heat began, the cage wasn't pressing in. The door wasn't open, but the lock felt… less heavy. He held her hand in the dark, and for a little while, she was not a subject, or an applicant, or a monster.

She was just a girl on a rooftop, holding onto something warm, waiting for the heat to break.

She went inside. The ghost of Leo's touch on her skin felt different now, under the sterile clarity of the receding heat. Not like a connection, but like a smudge. The Titan Reach pill kit sat on the counter, a stark, clinical answer to the mess she'd just made. *Non-negotiable for field readiness.* What a joke. She couldn't manage a simple interaction on a rooftop.

With a grimace of self-loathing, she punched a pill through the foil. It was a small, white admission of failure. She'd gone up there to escape the frenzy, and what had she done? She'd dragged a random stranger into her chaos. She'd practically glued herself to him, some poor guy just trying to get some air. He'd been kind, which made it worse. He'd seen a girl in obvious distress and had been decent about it. He had no idea the distress was an engineered biological command, that she'd honed in on his scent like a predator. Her skin crawled with the shame of it. Taking the pill wasn't just about control for the future; it was an eraser for tonight.

She swallowed it dry, the bitter chalk taste a fitting punishment.

That night, the heat didn't vanish, but its meaning changed. The raw need was still a persistent thrum in her blood, but the frantic, compulsive narrative was gone. The memory of leaning against him, of twining her fingers with his, played in her head not as a moment of solace, but as a cringe-inducing lapse in judgment. *What was I thinking?* She hadn't been. That was the whole problem. She fell asleep wrapped in a cold blanket of regret, the arousal now just a dull, physiological echo, stripped of all its dangerous illusion.

Morning brought a brutal clarity. The mental fog was gone, burned away by the chemical regulator. With it went the last vestiges of the heat's romantic lie. In the crisp, quiet light of day, she saw the rooftop for what it was: a vulnerable, potentially catastrophic mistake. Leo wasn't a point of quiet humanity; he was a variable. An unknown. A random man who now had a very strange story to tell, if he chose to. Her ears flattened against her skull as she replayed it. The neediness. The trembling. Offering her name like it was a gift, when it was just another piece of data she'd stupidly handed over.

A cold, sharp peace cut through her. This was the clarity she needed. This was how she had to move forward. No more messing with strangers. No more allowing the animal inside to dictate her interactions. The pill gave her the silence to hear her own priorities, and they were singular: survive, get control, get out of this life. Anything else was a liability.

The placid, emotionless days that followed were a relief. She was a closed system again. She left the pill bottle alone, monitoring the steady, artificial calm.

Then, the phone rang.

***

The manufactured quiet was shattered by a single phone call. It didn't come from a blocked number. It came with a title.

"This is Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Thorne, Joint Special Operations Personnel Division," the voice said. It was a voice carved from granite, with the ghost of a southern drawl smoothing its edges. It held no warmth, only absolute authority. "I am contacting you on behalf of our private-sector liaison, Titan Reach Consulting."

Her grip tightened on the phone. She was sitting on her fire escape, but she felt miles away from the rooftop. Her tail lay inert.

"They've forwarded your clinical evaluation package, Ms. Osyra. It's… thorough. Dr. Vasquez's notes indicate you possess a… unique aptitude profile."

She heard the subtext clearly. *A controlled asset. Or a loose cannon.*

"Titan Reach has a proposal. They field operatives. You've passed the medical. Now you face the practical."

The sun felt interrogative.

"They have a dedicated training curriculum at a secure facility. A final evaluation under simulated high-stress conditions. Think of it as a combine for people who hunt things that shouldn't exist. If you pass, a formal job offer is guaranteed. Full benefits, structured pay, a defined role. A purpose."

A purpose. A structure. Walls to replace the cage. Rules to prevent rooftop mistakes.

"And if I fail?" Her voice was flat.

A pause. "Failure in this line of work has permanent consequences. But in the context of the evaluation, it means you walk away. No offer. Your file is closed. Titan Reach considers the matter concluded."

*Concluded.* Either they'd forget her, or they'd ensure she was no longer a variable.

"What does it involve?"

"Need-to-know, delivered on-site. It will assess tactical acumen, threat response, team interoperability, and stress resilience under controlled exposures. All necessary equipment provided. You need only bring yourself."

*And the discipline not to compromise yourself*, she thought, the memory of a stranger's hand sharp in her mind.

"You have forty-eight hours to decide. An NDA will be transmitted. Sign it, and coordinates for transport will follow. Do not sign it, and this conversation never happened."

The line died.

She sat in the fading light. The pills on her counter were proof she couldn't trust her own biology. The rooftop was proof she couldn't trust her judgment when it was compromised. Titan Reach offered a third option: outsource the trust. Surrender to a bigger, sharper system with clearer rules. They wouldn't care about her regrets, only her performance.

The recruiter's call was a lifeline thrown from the very ship that had designed her storm. It was the only play.

She went inside. The NDA glowed on her screen. She didn't hesitate. The shame of her vulnerability with a stranger was a sharper spur than any ambition.

She typed her name.

The screen refreshed. Coordinates. A time: 04:30. **Pack Light.**

She looked at the pills. She took one, ensuring the fog—and the poor decisions it bred—would stay far away. Then she went to find a bag. The only thing she needed to pack was the resolve not to be a mistake again.
