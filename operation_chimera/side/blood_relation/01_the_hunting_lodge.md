# Chapter 1 - The Hunting Lodge

The lodge smelled like cedar and old smoke, the way it always did. The agency had picked it for her on purpose - a hunting lodge at the dead end of a logging road, the kind of place where a tall woman who came and went at strange hours and brought back strange cargo was just a hunter, nothing more. A hunter living in a hunting lodge. Nobody looked twice. That was the entire point.

The walls were cedar, gone honey-dark with age. Taxidermy everywhere - a whitetail buck over the cold fireplace, a brace of pheasants frozen mid-flush, a bobcat on a shelf with glass eyes that caught the light better than the real thing ever had. An antler chandelier hung over the main room, a dozen sheds wired together into something that was supposed to read as rustic luxury and mostly read as a lot of dead animals over your head while you ate. Osyra had not chosen a single piece of it. The agency had dressed the set before she ever moved in. She had only added to it, here and there, when a job left her something worth keeping.

Her own equipment fit the lie without trying. The charge rifle stood in the rack by the door, between a hunting bow and a lever-action that had never been fired. The lead-lined containment boxes stacked in the mudroom looked like gun cases. The breaching tool hung on a pegboard of axes and saws and looked like it belonged to the same family. A stranger walking through would have seen a hunter's home and nothing underneath it. The disguise was the house, and the house was good.

She had come back near dawn. The job was already done and bagged - routine, contained, nothing she'd lose sleep over. The thing she'd pulled out of it sat by the door in a vault box, sealed, quiet now that the lid was shut. She would run it to headquarters in the morning and not think about it again. So she told herself.

It wasn't the box she kept circling back to.

It was the photograph on her nightstand.

She'd found it months ago, on another job - a house at the edge of a town that had thinned out around it, empty for decades, behind cracked glass in a hallway nobody had walked in twenty years. She had taken it off the wall without deciding to, and she hadn't been able to put it down since. It traveled from coat pocket to table to the nightstand by her bed, where she set it most nights before she slept, the way some people set down a watch. She'd told herself a dozen times to log it and let it go. She hadn't.

She picked it up off the nightstand now and stood over it in her coat, contacts already in, looking at it the way she looked at everything she didn't yet understand.

The colors had gone bad with the years - reds drained, blues turned green, the whole thing sitting behind its cracked glass like it had given up trying to be a photograph. But the face hadn't gone with the colors. A young woman, hair parted in the middle, smiling at someone off-frame. The eyes were not red. The ears were ordinary. But the bones underneath were a face Osyra had been looking at in her own mirror her whole life without knowing whose it was.

She did not have a name for the feeling. She had names for very few of them. But somewhere in the night she had decided: today she'd take it to Vanity, and stop pretending she didn't need to know.

Headlights swept the front windows. Gravel under tires. Korval, on time the way he was always on time.

She slid the photograph into the inside pocket of her coat, against her chest, and picked up the box.

Outside, the cold met her face and her ears flattened back under the hat by themselves. She let them. Korval's SUV idled in the turnaround, exhaust ghosting white in the dark.

"Morning," he said as she set the box on the back seat.

"Headquarters." She got in. "I have something to report. And something that isn't a report."

He didn't ask. He never asked until she wanted him to. He pulled out onto the logging road, and the lodge shrank in the side mirror - cedar and dark glass, the antler chandelier a faint warm smudge through the front window where she'd left one lamp burning.

Then the trees took it, and there was only the road, and the box, and the photograph against her chest like a second heartbeat she hadn't asked for.
