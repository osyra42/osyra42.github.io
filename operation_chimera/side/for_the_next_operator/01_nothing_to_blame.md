# Chapter 1 - Nothing to Blame

*Don't criticize. Don't condemn. Blame has never once changed a person - it only teaches them to defend the thing you wanted them to drop. Learn this one early or it will cost you a person while you're busy being right.*

Here's the first one, and it's the hardest, because it goes against everything the job puts in your hands. You will show up to ruined places full of people who did the wrong thing. Froze. Ran. Left someone behind. And every instinct you have is going to want to tell them what they did. Don't. A person with a wall up isn't a person you can move, and you will need to move them, fast, over things you don't want them looking at.

I'll give you the one that taught it to me, and I'm not going to make it clean, because it wasn't, and you're not a recruit I get to protect.

A brood-thing had gotten into a shelter. Not a monster from the woods - one of the wet, patient ones that gets into a crawlspace and lays and waits, and by the time anyone smells it there are already too many. Forty-some people had barricaded themselves in the gymnasium. When I came through the doors the smell told me most of what I'd find and I was still not ready for the sound, which was a room of people who had decided, collectively, in the dark, that the barricade held only as long as it was fed.

They'd been pushing people out. Through a fire door, into the corridor where the thing was working, a few at a time, whenever the noise on the other side of the barricade started up again - because it went quiet for a while after. They'd worked out the exchange. They'd picked. Old ones first, then the ones nobody in the room would speak up for. There was a man running it, a big calm man with a clipboard from the front office, and he had a *list*, and he was keeping order, and the room was letting him because he'd turned the worst thing a person can do into a procedure and procedures let you not be the one deciding.

There was a boy still in the room he'd written down for the next quiet spell. Fourteen, maybe. His mother was holding the door-side of him and the man was explaining to her, gently, reasonably, why it had to be fair, why her boy's number had come up, and she was *listening*, she was starting to nod, that's the part I need you to understand - the barricade had been up two days and she was starting to nod.

Every word in me wanted to go at that man. Wanted to tell the room exactly what they were, put it in the air where they'd have to breathe it. And if I had, they'd have closed around it. Forty people who needed to believe the list was fair would have decided the animal in the doorway was the real threat, and they'd have been right that I was dangerous, and I'd have lost the room and the boy both while I was busy being correct.

So I didn't. I gave the man his procedure back and stepped inside it. Told him I was the relief, that the exchange was over because the count outside was handled, that he'd kept order and now he could stand down and I needed his help getting people to the buses. I let him be the calm man with the clipboard who'd held it together instead of the calm man who'd sorted his neighbors by who'd scream least. I watched him take it - watched relief go across his face, actual relief, that someone had come to tell him he'd done well. He set the boy's paper down on the stack like it was nothing. Like it had always been paper.

We got them out. All of the ones still living, the boy included. It worked. The mercy I didn't spend on that man is the reason a fourteen-year-old walked onto a bus.

I found what was in the corridor after. I made myself look, because someone in that building owed them a witness and the room had already voted not to be one. There were eleven. The thing ate slow and it ate low, started at the soft parts and worked out, so most of them had been alive and aware for the front half of it - you could read which ones had tried to crawl back to the barricade by how far they'd gotten with what they had left, and one had gotten a surprising distance, a woman, on her elbows, because from the knees down there was no longer anything to use and she had kept going anyway on the parts of her that still answered. They'd died in the order the list gave them and you could see the order on the floor, oldest nearest the door where they'd been pushed first, and the quiet the room had learned to time the exchange by was the thing pausing between them, unhurried, because a barricade that feeds itself is in no rush and neither was what it fed. That's not the monster I want you to remember. The monster was the arithmetic. The monster was the nodding. The thing in the corridor was just hungry; it had no idea it was the least guilty animal in the building.

The man gave a statement. Of course he did. He described the operator that arrived - the animal, he called me - and how it "removed him from a leadership role during the crisis" and he wasn't sure it had followed protocol. He was building his defense already, in case anyone ever asked about a list, and the first brick of it was me. He never mentioned the corridor. Neither did the room. Forty people and it simply hadn't happened; there'd been a monster, and an animal the Agency sent, and everyone in the gym had been survivors together.

Don't criticize. Don't condemn. Not because the man deserved to keep his face - he deserved the corridor and a spot near the front of the line for it. Because the boy was in the room, and blame would have cost me the boy. You swallow the true thing and you say the useful thing and you get the living out, and then you go somewhere alone and you deal with what it does to you to have called that man *steady* while eleven people cooled twenty feet away. That last part is yours. I can't teach it. I can only tell you it's coming.
