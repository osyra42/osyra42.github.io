# Chapter 8 - Made to Matter

*Tell someone they matter and they will become more than they were. What they become is not yours to choose.*

There was a man in Ostwick named Ezren who swept the floor of the meeting hall. That was all anyone let him do. He was slow in the way that made people impatient - words came to him late, and his hands shook, and the town had decided a long time ago that he was furniture. When we rolled in on the Ostwick contract they didn't even mention him in the briefing. He was standing in the corner with his broom while the councilmen talked over each other about the thing in the mine.

The thing was a burrower. A blind, wet, segmented cryptid the length of a bus that had come up under the eastern grazing land and was pulling livestock and, by then, three people down into the dark. It hunted by vibration. Sound and footfall. You did not want to be the loudest thing standing on that ground.

I needed the mine layout and none of the councilmen had been down it in twenty years. Ezren had. He'd swept it, back when it still ran. He knew every drift and every collapsed shaft and where the old vent adits let out. Nobody asked him. So I asked him.

I crouched to his eye line - you learn to do that, to stop being six-foot-four at a person you need - and I told him I couldn't do this without him. I meant it as fact, not flattery. He was the only living map. I told him the whole town's safety was going to run through what he remembered, and that made him the most important man in Ostwick that night. I watched it land. I watched twenty years of being furniture come off him like a coat.

He was extraordinary. His words came clean when they mattered. He drew the mine in the dirt from memory, every branch, and he marked the one adit where the burrower's tunnel intersected the old works, and he was right about all of it. We staged the kill there. Drew the thing with a rigged thumper hammering the deck plate, let it come at the noise, and put four charges into the soft segment behind its head when it breached. It died screaming a sound no throat should make and the ground stopped moving under Ostwick for the first time in a week.

They cheered Ezren. For one night the town that had made him furniture carried him on their shoulders. I thought - and this is the part I am setting down for you, so read it slow - I thought I had done a good thing. I thought I had put worth into a man who'd had it starved out of him and that worth would stay good because he had suffered enough to know what it was for. That is the mistake. That is the whole mistake.

I came back through Ostwick eleven months later on another job. Ezren was on the council now. They'd given him a title and a room and a say. And there was a new man in the corner with the broom - a drifter, simple, slower even than Ezren had been - and Ezren had found him, and Ezren was standing over him.

He'd worked out that the drifter would do anything to be allowed to stay. So he made him do things. He made him kneel in front of the council for their laughter. He made him eat off the floor he swept, and when the man was slow to, Ezren put a boot on the back of his neck and held it there and told the room this was how you taught the worthless their place. He'd learned the exact shape of what it was to be made small, and instead of being the one man alive who'd never do it to another, he'd become the best there was at it. He knew precisely where it hurt because he'd been hurt there. The dignity I gave him he'd spent buying himself someone to stand on.

I broke it up. I put Ezren against the wall by his collar and I let him see the ears and the teeth, let him remember what I am, and for a second he was furniture again. Then I let go, because that's not the job, and the job doesn't have a line item for a man's soul.

Here is the lesson, recruit, and it is the coldest one in this book.

Worth is not a gift with strings. When you make someone matter, you hand them a currency and you do not get to say what they buy. You think a person who has been ground down will be gentle with the next one down, because they know. They don't know. Some of them, most of them, they only learned that being on the bottom is unbearable - and the fix for that, the fastest one, is to put someone under you. You didn't give Ezren dignity. You gave him a floor. What he built on it was his.

If you're built like me you'll want to keep score. You'll want the worth you assign to compound into decency out in the world, so the ledger comes out clean and the nights you crouched down to a broken man's eye line meant something. It doesn't compound. It doesn't transfer. You are the only one at that table assigning value, and everyone else is spending it on whatever they want, and some of them want to hurt.

Do it anyway. Give the man the map. Let him be the most important person in the room for the one night it saves lives.

Just don't stay to see what he does with it.
