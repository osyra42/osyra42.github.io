# Chapter 2 - Worth Saying

*Give honest appreciation. People starve their whole lives to feel like they mattered to something. When someone helps you, tell them it counted - and mean it. Just know that the world you're handing them back to didn't hear you say it, and won't care that you did.*

There is a hunger in people you can use, and "use" is the right word, so sit with it. People are starving to be told, by someone whose judgment they trust, that a thing they did mattered. Most go a lifetime and never hear it. You can hand it to them in one true sentence and it will open them like a key. The only rule is that it has to be true. Invent it and they'll smell it. Find the real thing - there's almost always a real thing - and say it plain.

The town was small and it had been wrong about which of its neighbors was the monster, and that is the actual subject of this lesson, so hold onto it.

There was a woman there who'd figured out where a pack was denning. Weeks before anyone. She kept a notebook - brownouts, which nights the dogs wouldn't cross the yard, a smell off the old cannery. Nobody listened to her; she was the kind nobody listens to, worn down to apologizing for the space she stood in. She gave me the notebook with her hands shaking and I read it and it was *good*, good enough that two people walked out of a building that night who wouldn't have, and I told her so. Not "thanks, this helps." I told her the specific thing - that it was better fieldwork than what I get from people who are paid for it, that lives were leaving that town tonight on the strength of her paying attention when no one asked her to. I watched her sit up under it. It was true. That's why it landed.

But the notebook had a name in it. She'd written down, weeks back, that the smell was strongest near the old cannery and that a man named Delph came and went from it at odd hours - not an accusation, just a woman noting a thing near another thing. And a town that had spent a month frightened and looking for somewhere to put the fear had gotten hold of a version of her notebook. And they'd decided Delph.

He wasn't. I'd already put the pack down; I knew exactly what had been living in the cannery and it hadn't been a man. Delph was just a sour old bachelor who kept strange hours because strange hours are what you keep when no one's waiting on you. None of that mattered. By the time I understood what the town had decided, they'd already gone to his house.

I got there and there was a crowd in the yard and there was already not much they hadn't done. I'll give it to you the way I found it, because you need to know what people are before one of them thanks you.

They'd started before they were sure. That's the part. They didn't wait to be right. They'd pulled him out through his own front window - the glass was still in him, in his back and one cheek, when I got there - and they'd used a length of chain from someone's truck, and they had done to his hands the thing a frightened crowd always does to the hands, because a crowd hates hands, hands are what a witch curses you with, so his were broken back on themselves and would not have held a spoon again even if the rest of him had been let live. Someone had gone at his mouth with a boot - to stop the talking, I think, because he'd been telling them the truth and the truth is unbearable when you've already decided - and the teeth were on the ground in front of him like something spilled. He was still alive when they set him going. I need you to hold that. The fire was near the end for them but it was the long middle for him; they lit a man who was still trying to say his own name through a mouth that couldn't shape it anymore, and the sound a person makes then is not screaming, screaming is from before, this was lower and wetter and it came in time with the breathing and it is a sound I did not have a slot for and now do. He cooked from the legs up and the crowd's faces in the firelight were not the faces of monsters. That was the horror of it. They looked *relieved*. They looked like people who had finally been allowed to put something down.

And when I came through the fence, the thing that made them scatter and scream *monster* was me. The animal. Arriving too late to a thing they'd done with their own hands and their own good sense of justice, and being, to them, the only horror in the yard.

The pack in the cannery killed cleanly compared to what those people did to Delph. It killed to eat; it was quick because quick is efficient and nothing in it wanted him to suffer, because nothing in it wanted anything but food. The town wanted him to suffer. The town took its time. I want that difference to sit wrong with you. It's supposed to. The one the world called the monster was the merciful one, and I am the one they built to kill it.

And the woman. Her notebook did it - her careful, ignored, finally-appreciated notebook, the fieldwork I'd praised her for, was the document the town read Delph's death out of. She knew. She came to me after, not the town, *me*, because I was the only one who'd told her the work was good, and she asked me - she needed me to tell her - whether it was her fault. Whether if she'd never written it down. And I stood there with her looking up at me the way she had when I'd praised her, wanting the same key turned the other direction, wanting me to un-say the true thing.

I couldn't. I don't lie to people about the weight they carry; it's the one dignity I've got left to give. So I told her the truth, which was that she'd done nothing but see clearly, and that seeing clearly is not a crime, and that a town of decent people had taken her clear seeing and made it into a murder, and that the fault was theirs, every ounce. And I watched it not help. Because "it's not your fault" doesn't reach a person who is going to hear a man screaming in a yard for the rest of her life. I'd given her one warm hour with the notebook. The same notebook gave her that. The appreciation was real and the ruin was real and they came out of the exact same pages.

Give the appreciation anyway. That woman deserved to hear, once, that she mattered, and almost no one in her life would ever say it, and I don't regret being the one who did. But understand what you're doing when you hand someone the feeling that their eyes are worth something: you're handing it to them inside a world that will take those same eyes and make a weapon of them the second it's frightened. You can't control what the world does with the worth you give people. You can only make sure the worth was true. Some nights that's going to feel like a very thin thing to have gotten right. Get it right anyway.
