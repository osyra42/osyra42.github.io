# Chapter 7 - Talk Yourself Calm

*Let them empty the whole magazine of it. Full attention, no interruption. A frightened person will talk themselves calm if you only hold still and let them.*

The town was called Cinderslope, a mining strip halfway up a dead mountain, and the thing coming down the shaft at night was a burrower - blind, long as a bus, jaws that folded outward like a hand opening. It had already taken nine men off the lower galleries. When we sealed the last adit I found one survivor left alive down there, a shift foreman named Pell, wedged into a supply nook behind a stack of blasting mats, and he would not come out.

You will meet this. The survivor who has gone past screaming into a low steady shaking, who has stopped being a person and become a held breath. You cannot pull them. Pull them and they lock harder. So you do the one thing that costs you nothing and everything.

You listen.

I sat down in the muck across from Pell with my back to the rock, which put my rifle down and my face at his level, and I did not say a word about the burrower or the exit or the time. I let the silence sit until he filled it. And he filled it. He told me about the men. He told me which one had screamed and which one hadn't. He told me he'd pushed a boy named Dorsey out of the way and into the thing's path to buy himself the nook, and he'd been holding that in his chest for six hours like a swallowed coal. He told me about his wife. He told me the thing his father said to him at eleven that he had never told anyone. I did not interrupt. I did not fix. I nodded when he needed a wall to throw the words against, and I kept my ears - the ones they built into me, the ones that pick a heartbeat out of static - trained on his, and I felt it come down. Ninety beats. Eighty. His hands stopped. When a person is heard, truly heard, the body believes it is safe, and the body climbs down. It is the closest thing to a miracle I own. It works every single time.

He walked out on his own feet. I did not carry him. He walked, because he'd talked himself back into a man who could walk, and I let him think it was his own doing, because it was.

Learn that. It is real. It is the best of what I can teach you.

Now the rest.

Pell testified four months later. Not against me by name - against the program, against the units, in the county inquest they held over the nine dead and the sealed galleries and the question of who authorized fielding a Chimeran asset near a civilian workforce. And Pell stood up under oath and told them what the monster had been like. He described it. The height of it, the eyes, the way it moved in the dark, the reach of it, the sound of its breathing when it sat too close. He described me. Every word he gave them was a word he'd given me first, in the dark, when I was the only warm thing between him and the burrower - and now he arranged them so the jury would picture the thing that came down the shaft and the thing that sat across from him and could not tell which was which. That was the point. He needed them not to be able to tell. Because if the operator was a monster too, then Dorsey didn't die because Pell pushed him. Dorsey died in a place monsters were allowed to be.

He used the intimacy. That is the exact instrument. Everything he unburdened to me because I held still and let him - the boy he pushed, the coal in his chest - he needed that gone from his own record, so he welded it onto mine. The attention I gave him became the material. I heard him back into a man, and he spent the man I made talking me into a beast.

Understand what that costs, and understand it precisely, because this is the part no one warns you about.

Attention is a gift with no return built into it. You give it and give it. The frightened survivor talks and steadies; the debrief with Vanity comes and I recount it flat and she writes it down; the next town, the next nook, the next held breath. I have listened to more people talk themselves calm than I could name for you. And in twelve years not one of them ever turned it around and asked me what I was carrying down that shaft. Not one asked what I heard in the dark with those ears while they slept off the sedative. You will absorb every person you save. You will hold the coal for them until they can set it down. And when it is your chest full of coals, there is no one across the muck sitting still and letting you empty it, because the thing they see when they look at you is not a person who could be full. It is a wall to throw words against. It is a monster with good ears.

So do it anyway. Sit down in the muck, put the rifle out of your hands, and let them talk themselves calm. It is the truest kindness the job allows and it will save them and it will be used against you and both of those are the same act.

Just know going in that the account only runs one direction. You are the one who assigns it worth. No one at that table will ever assign it back.

I thought, driving out of Cinderslope, that if I'd just let the burrower reach the surface and take the town whole, they'd have needed each other too much to build a case against the thing that saved them.

That's not our job.
