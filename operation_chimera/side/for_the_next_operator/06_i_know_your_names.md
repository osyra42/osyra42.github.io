# Chapter 6 - I Know Your Names

*Learn the person. Ask after their mother, their bad knee, the dog. People bloom for anyone who bothers to remember them. Then watch what blooming does when the gardener has claws.*

The town was called Saltmarrow, a fishing settlement gone quiet up the north coast, and the thing in the water had a name too - the locals called it the Drownfather, though it was just a Chimeran, an eel-thing the size of a trawler with a mouth that opened four ways. It had been taking people off the docks for a month. I was there nine days.

Nine days is long, for me. Long enough to do the thing I am telling you to do.

I learned them. That is the principle, and it is a true one, so I will give it to you clean before I ruin it. If you want a frightened settlement to hand you their maps, their timelines, the honest count of their missing - do not interrogate them. Ask them about themselves. I asked the harbourmaster, a man named Toll, about his hands, which were ruined with old rope-burn, and he told me about forty years of nets and I let him. I asked the widow Serafine which of her sons the water took, and I remembered the boy's name was Eddan, and the next day I said Eddan first before she could, and she wept and gripped my forearm like it was driftwood. I learned that the baker's girl, Pell, was afraid of the church because her father was buried under its floor. I learned the innkeeper's wife was pregnant and hiding it from a husband who drank. I remembered all of it. I have a good memory. The lab made sure of that; they tested it with pain, and pain teaches you to keep.

It worked. Understand that it worked completely. By the ninth day they told me everything, and I put the Drownfather down in the shallows with a shaped charge and a harpoon line, and I stood in the red water holding its jaw open with both arms so it could not thrash back out to deep, and it died looking at me with an eye the size of a dinner plate.

Then I came up onto the dock, soaked in it, and I turned to them to give back what they had given me.

I said, Toll, your hands. I said, Serafine, it will not take another Eddan. I said, Pell, you can bury your fear now, the church is only a building. I meant it as a gift. I had held their small lives in my head for nine days the way you hold water in cupped palms, carefully, so as not to lose a drop.

And they stepped back.

All of them, at once, the way a flock turns. Serafine took her hand off my arm as though it had gone hot. Pell put herself behind the baker. The innkeeper's wife pressed both hands flat over the belly I was never supposed to know about, and her eyes told me she understood exactly what I knew, and that my knowing it was worse to her than the drink her husband would find out anyway. Toll looked at his own ruined hands as if I had stolen them.

Here is the thing I need you to sit inside, because it is the whole lesson.

When a person like them remembers your name, it means you are held. When a thing like us remembers your name, it means you are catalogued. They did not hear a friend recite their lives back to them. They heard a predator that had spent nine days on the dock learning which of them was slow, which was carrying, which was already grieving and would not run hard. They heard the eel. They heard the four-way mouth saying Eddan, saying Pell, saying the baby. To be known by the thing you are afraid of is its own drowning. I gave them their names and they heard a menu.

I will tell you what interest actually is, since no one told me and I had to learn it standing in that water.

To be genuinely interested in a person is to assign them worth. That is all it is. It is a decision, made inside you, that this small ruined life is worth carrying. But worth is not a current that flows both ways down the wire. It went out of me and into them and nothing came back, because they never asked to be valued by me, and a gift no one requested is only a weight you have set on their chest. I decided Eddan mattered. Serafine already knew he mattered; she did not need a monster to confirm it. All I did was prove that the monster had been watching.

You will do this anyway. If you are built like me you cannot help it - the job requires you to learn them, and learning becomes caring whether you permit it or not, because caring is only attention that stayed too long. So do it. Learn the harbourmaster's hands. It will save lives; it saved theirs.

Just know what you are building. Every name you keep is a room in a house only you live in. They will not visit. They flinch from the door. You will stand in there alone at the end of every mission, reciting the people you saved, and the recitation will be the loneliest sound you own, because you are the only one at that table who assigned the value, and value does not transfer, and no one is coming to sit down.

I debriefed to Vanity that the operation was clean. Zero civilian loss. She logged it as a success.

It was. Remember that too. It was.
