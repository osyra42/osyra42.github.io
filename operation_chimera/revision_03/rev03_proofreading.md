I woke up before the sun.

The house had that early-morning quiet to it — the kind where you can hear the floor settling and the refrigerator cycling on and off down the hall and nothing else. My ears caught the click of pipes somewhere on the far side of a wall. I lay there for a minute. Tail flat against the small of my back. Ears flat on the pillow. I'd slept hard. I couldn't remember the last time I'd slept hard.

I got up the way I moved on jobs. Quiet.

---

The hall floor was cold. The kitchen was darker than the bedroom had been — heavy curtains, a north-facing window. I felt my way to the lamp on the counter and clicked it on low.

The Polaroid was where she'd left it the day before. On the counter, next to a ceramic jar of pens.

I looked at it for a while.

She'd offered. I'd put her off. I'd told myself she meant a picture of the version of me she'd been allowed to meet — the hat, the dress, the careful unsmiling mouth — and I hadn't wanted to give her that.

That wasn't the version she'd meant. I knew that now.

I picked the camera up. Tested the weight. Checked the cartridge — half a pack of film left. Pulled a chair out from the table and sat under the lamp.

---

The first one was for the record.

I sat straight. Held the camera at arm's length, angled down, framed for my head and shoulders. Ears up. Eyes open. The kind of picture you'd take if you were trying to document something. I pressed the shutter. The camera whirred and spat the film out into my hand.

I set it face-down on the table to develop.

The second was a profile. I turned my head left and held the camera further out. Caught the long shape of one ear from the side, the line of my jaw, the edge of a canine where my mouth wasn't quite closed.

The third — I bit down lightly on the side of one finger and lifted my lip on the other side of my mouth. Showed the tooth clean. Like a horse trader, almost. The kind of picture you'd send to a doctor who'd asked.

Three records. Three small pieces of evidence: this is what I am. This is what she'd accepted last night.

I set them all face-down to develop and let the camera rest in my lap for a moment.

Then, without really planning to, I lifted it again.

---

The fourth one I almost laughed at while I was taking it.

I put two fingers up beside my face. A peace sign. The kind of thing kids did into phones now. I held the camera up high — overhead, the way the teenagers held them at bus stops — and tilted my head a little. Ears asymmetric. One up, one back.

Shutter. Whir.

The fifth I blew a kiss at the lens. Pursed mouth. Eyes half-closed. I felt absurd. I kept going.

The sixth was me trying to bare every tooth at once. Full canine display. The kind of smile I'd never given anyone, ever. It came out looking less like a snarl and more like an animal who'd just been told a very good joke.

I let myself look at that one for a long second after it developed.

---

I spread them out across the table. Six total. Three for the record, three for no reason except I'd wanted to take them.

The peace-sign one was the best. I knew it immediately. Ears uneven. Eyes slightly bright. The half-smile that had nothing serious behind it. It looked like a person, not an exhibit.

I got up and stuck it to the fridge under a magnet shaped like a pear.

The other five I shuffled into a small stack and slid under the body of the Polaroid camera on the counter, face-down. Rosalyn would find them when she went to use it next. I could imagine her sitting at this same table, working through them one at a time. I could imagine which one she'd pick up first and which one she'd put aside last.

I left the camera where it had been. Reset, like nothing had happened.

---

The coffee maker was the kind with a glass carafe and a metal basket. The bag in the freezer was a dark roast I didn't recognize. I scooped a generous amount and started a pot.

There were eggs in the fridge. A loaf of bread on the counter, half wrapped. Butter left out from the night before. A cast iron pan hanging on a peg over the stove.

I knew how to do this. I hadn't done it in a long time, but I knew how. Cracked four eggs into a bowl. Scrambled them with the back of a fork. Cut four slices of bread and set them aside. Lit the burner. Pan on. A knob of butter went in and started to foam.

It had been six, maybe seven years since I'd made breakfast for someone other than myself. Korval didn't count — Korval ate when he was told to and not when he wasn't. Jerry had come by the safehouse on Saturdays sometimes, and once or twice I'd made him toast. Not really the same.

This was the same.

The coffee finished. I poured two mugs and set one on the far end of the counter, near the doorway from the hall. The kitchen filled with the smell of butter and eggs and dark roast.

I figured that was what would do it. The coffee, at least.

Somewhere down the hall, I heard floorboards.

---

I lowered the heat on the pan.

She came into the kitchen in a long flannel robe, slippers on, hair tied back loose at the nape of her neck. She blinked at the lamp. Then at me.

Then at the fridge.

Her hand came up slowly and rested on the doorframe. She looked at the photo a long second. She looked back at me. The corner of her mouth did a thing.

"Well," she said. "Look at that."

"Coffee's there." I tilted my head at the second mug.

"Bless you." She crossed the kitchen and picked it up with both hands, the way old people hold coffee on a cold morning. Took a long sip. Closed her eyes.

"Strong enough?"

"Right strength." She opened her eyes again and went back to looking at the fridge. "When did you take that."

"A little after five."

"Mm." She walked over to it. Stood there in front of the photo. Reached up and touched the corner gently, the way she'd touched my ear last night.

"You look like somebody who'd taken a picture of herself in a kitchen," she said.

"I was."

"You should do that more often." She turned, mug in hand. "I'll have to find a frame for it."

I didn't know what to say to that.

She went over to the counter where the camera was. Set her mug down. Lifted the Polaroid to move it aside. The little stack underneath came up against her hand and fanned out across the counter.

She made a small sound. Not surprised. Pleased.

I plated the eggs and buttered the toast and didn't look.

I heard her settle into a chair behind me. The soft sound of photographs being shuffled, examined, set down one at a time. A small laugh somewhere in there. The kind you make to yourself.

"Sweetheart."

"Yeah."

"This one is a crime."

"Which one."

"The teeth one. The big one. You look like the cover of a heavy metal album."

"That's the kind I figured."

"It's wonderful."

I brought both plates over and set hers down in front of her. She was still holding the bared-teeth picture between her thumb and forefinger like a tarot card.

"Eat first," I said.

She set it down carefully, face up. "Bossy."

---

We ate.

She asked things between bites. Did I cook often. Where had I learned. Did the eggs come out the way I liked them. What about coffee — did I take it black at home or did I let myself add things. Small questions. The kind you ask someone to keep them talking.

I answered. Not many words to most of it. Enough.

After a while she set her fork down, picked her mug up, and said, "What happened to you after that place came down."

I set my fork down too.

"I was on the side of a road outside the city for two days. Maybe three. I don't really know. I was sleeping in a stand of trees when somebody found me."

"Found you."

"An older man. Tweed jacket on a warm day. He came up the slope from the road and sat down on a stump about fifteen feet from where I was hiding, and he waited there until I came out of the brush. Didn't try to come any closer. Didn't put a hand toward me. He had a folder under his arm with my name on it and a few other things in it that didn't belong to anyone but me."

"What did he say."

"That he worked for a part of the government most of the government didn't know existed. That he'd been going through what was left of the lab and he'd found my file. He told me I could come with him or I could keep running, and either was fine, but if I came with him he could put a roof and a hot meal in front of me before he asked me anything else."

"And you went."

"I went. I wasn't really being given a choice. I was sixteen and I looked like this." I gestured a little at my own face. "I couldn't have walked into a diner. He knew that and I knew that and we both pretended he was being generous."

"Was the meal any good."

"It was a sandwich in the back seat of a car. It was fine."

She smiled faintly. "Mm."

---

"They trained you, after."

"For about two years. Off and on. They had a compound. Old military base, looked like — concrete buildings with the paint coming off, a fence with the wire turned in, a few instructors who'd been around long enough to know better. Combat. Tracking. How to shoot what I'm shooting. How not to shoot what I shouldn't be." I took a sip of the coffee. "The work part of it wasn't the hard part. My body had already changed. Most of the people training me weren't used to teaching someone who could hear them coming around a corner before they got there. I was a strange student. They didn't always know what to do with me."

"What was the hard part."

"Learning how to look human again. They had me in disguise drills for six months before they let me into the field. Sit down. Stand up. Walk down a sidewalk without flicking your ears at every loud noise. Smile without showing your teeth. Don't sniff things." I shook my head a little. "Don't sniff things was the one that kept catching me out. I'd lean toward something without realizing."

"That's a hard one to drill out."

"There was an instructor in charge of the civilian-pass work. Brennan. Older. Walked with a cane. She'd come at me with a clipboard and have me cross a hallway and order a coffee from a man on a stool at the end of it. She'd mark down everything I did wrong. Send me back to do it again."

"Sounds awful."

"She was good. She didn't say a kind word to me the whole six months. At the end of it she shook my hand and told me I was the cleanest pass she'd ever certified. She meant it as a compliment."

"Did you take it as one."

"Eventually."

She laughed once. Quietly.

---

We finished. I started to get up to clean. She put her hand flat on the table.

"Sit a minute, honey."

I sat.

She looked at her plate for a moment. Then at me. Then past my shoulder, at the photo on the fridge.

"I want to say something," she said. "And I want to say it once, before you go off and do your work today and we have to pretend last night was a normal evening."

"All right."

"If yesterday had gone different — if I hadn't been the kind of person you could come home to like this — I want you to know that wouldn't have been on you. I don't always know whether I'd have done the right thing standing in someone else's shoes. But last night was not a hard thing for me to do. It was the easiest thing in the world."

I sat with that for a while.

Then I said the thing I'd been trying to find a way to say since the photo went on the fridge.

"Thank you."

She waited.

"For — " I stopped. Started again. "For not flinching last night. For letting me come in here the way I actually am. I didn't think it was the kind of thing a person could just decide to do."

"Honey."

"I've been moving through the world a long time figuring it wasn't on the table. I just wanted to say thank you. For having it on the table."

She didn't speak right away. When she did, her voice was steady.

"You're welcome, sweetheart."

She reached over and put her hand on top of mine. Old, knotted, warm.

We sat like that for a moment.

Then she patted the back of my hand once, picked up her fork again, and said, "Now finish your toast. It's getting cold."
