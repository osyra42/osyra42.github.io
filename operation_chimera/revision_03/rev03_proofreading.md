I woke up before the sun.

The house had that early-morning quiet to it — the kind where you can hear the floor settling and the refrigerator cycling on and off down the hall and nothing else. My ears caught the click of pipes somewhere on the far side of a wall. I lay there for a minute. Tail flat against the small of my back. Ears flat on the pillow. I'd slept hard. I couldn't remember the last time I'd slept hard.

I got up the way I moved on jobs. Quiet.

---

The hall floor was cold. The kitchen was darker than the bedroom had been — heavy curtains, a north-facing window. I felt my way to the lamp on the counter and clicked it on low.

The Polaroid was where she'd left it the day before. On the counter, next to a ceramic jar of pens.

I looked at it for a while.

She'd offered. I'd put her off. I'd told myself she meant a picture of the version of me she'd been allowed to meet — the hat, the dress, the careful unsmiling mouth — and I hadn't wanted to give her that.

That wasn't the version she'd meant. I knew that now.

I picked the camera up. Tested the weight. Checked the cartridge — half a pack of film left. Pulled a chair out from the table and sat under the lamp.

---

The first one was for the record.

I sat straight. Held the camera at arm's length, angled down, framed for my head and shoulders. Ears up. Eyes open. The kind of picture you'd take if you were trying to document something. I pressed the shutter. The camera whirred and spat the film out into my hand.

I set it face-down on the table to develop.

The second was a profile. I turned my head left and held the camera further out. Caught the long shape of one ear from the side, the line of my jaw, the edge of a canine where my mouth wasn't quite closed.

The third — I bit down lightly on the side of one finger and lifted my lip on the other side of my mouth. Showed the tooth clean. Like a horse trader, almost. The kind of picture you'd send to a doctor who'd asked.

Three records. Three small pieces of evidence: this is what I am. This is what she'd accepted last night.

I set them all face-down to develop and let the camera rest in my lap for a moment.

Then, without really planning to, I lifted it again.

---

The fourth one I almost laughed at while I was taking it.

I put two fingers up beside my face. A peace sign. The kind of thing kids did into phones now. I held the camera up high — overhead, the way the teenagers held them at bus stops — and tilted my head a little. Ears asymmetric. One up, one back.

Shutter. Whir.

The fifth I blew a kiss at the lens. Pursed mouth. Eyes half-closed. I felt absurd. I kept going.

The sixth was me trying to bare every tooth at once. Full canine display. The kind of smile I'd never given anyone, ever. It came out looking less like a snarl and more like an animal who'd just been told a very good joke.

I let myself look at that one for a long second after it developed.

---

I spread them out across the table. Six total. Three for the record, three for no reason except I'd wanted to take them.

The peace-sign one was the best. I knew it immediately. Ears uneven. Eyes slightly bright. The half-smile that had nothing serious behind it. It looked like a person, not an exhibit.

I got up and stuck it to the fridge under a magnet shaped like a pear.

The other five I shuffled into a small stack and slid under the body of the Polaroid camera on the counter, face-down. Rosalyn would find them when she went to use it next. I could imagine her sitting at this same table, working through them one at a time. I could imagine which one she'd pick up first and which one she'd put aside last.

I left the camera where it had been. Reset, like nothing had happened.

---

The coffee maker was the kind with a glass carafe and a metal basket. The bag in the freezer was a dark roast I didn't recognize. I scooped a generous amount and started a pot.

There were eggs in the fridge. A loaf of bread on the counter, half wrapped. Butter left out from the night before. A cast iron pan hanging on a peg over the stove.

I knew how to do this. I hadn't done it in a long time, but I knew how. Cracked four eggs into a bowl. Scrambled them with the back of a fork. Cut four slices of bread and set them aside. Lit the burner. Pan on. A knob of butter went in and started to foam.

It had been six, maybe seven years since I'd made breakfast for someone other than myself. Korval didn't count — Korval ate when he was told to and not when he wasn't. Jerry had come by the safehouse on Saturdays sometimes, and once or twice I'd made him toast. Not really the same.

This was the same.

The coffee finished. I poured two mugs and set one on the far end of the counter, near the doorway from the hall. The kitchen filled with the smell of butter and eggs and dark roast.

I figured that was what would do it. The coffee, at least.

Somewhere down the hall, I heard floorboards.
