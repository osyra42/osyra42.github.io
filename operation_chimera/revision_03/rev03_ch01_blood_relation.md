I was nervous. There were so many things that could go wrong.

I thought this while looking out the window as my chauffeur drove me to my destination. Up front, behind the wheel, was Korval—a tough, Russian ex-military guy. He was primarily my chauffeur and bodyguard. He always followed me everywhere. Not that I needed it. I was usually the dangerous one.

I had recently gotten word that the agency found one of my biological family members and gave me the opportunity to visit. I took it. My grandmother: Rosalyn Renaya. Despite my protests—I didn't need a bodyguard just to blend in as family—my boss insisted and would not back down.

"How much further until we get there?" I wondered out loud.

"About thirty minutes," Korval answered in his thick Russian accent. Then he added, "Why are you so impatient? You're always calm."

A strange sensation washed over me. I'd never wanted to be close to family before. Maybe this was a new experience. I had nothing to say back. No reply. I stared out the window as the metropolis turned into rural neighborhoods.

"Okay, we're just about there," Korval said. "Make sure you dress appropriately."

For a moment, I'd nearly forgotten that my grandmother didn't know about my altered form. I climbed into the back seat and changed my jeans for a dress. Put a hat on, added contacts. I felt like a completely different person. I couldn't tell which version of me was real. The one where I'm Chimeran? Or the one where I was human?

---

I was Chimera now. I wasn't always Chimera. This came from a lab. I was given up for adoption as an infant. My aunt took me in and raised me until she passed. After that I went to foster care — they're the ones who sold me to the medical facility. I was sixteen. They experimented on me. They experimented on many other kids. Most of them didn't make it. I did.

I became a Chimera—part wolf, part human. You couldn't classify me as human or animal. While they were running their tests, there was a breach. Someone blew up part of the building. The facilities shut down, and I got free. They wrote it off as a total loss.

But then came the problem: how to get work? How to get money when I looked like this? I was discovered by someone sifting through the paperwork in the building. They recognized me, found me, and offered me a job. Provide my capabilities—which had proven adequate. They wanted me to hunt down and fix things. Deal with anomalous entities and situations. The pay was good. I got to be the secret they didn't point and laugh at.

I had wanted to go outside occasionally, so I learned how to conceal my form:

- Color contacts to hide my red eyes
- Big hats to hide my ears
- Long dresses to hide my tail

Didn't need to smile much. You couldn't see my canines anyway.

People I came into contact with just thought I was angry. That was good enough. They left me alone.

After a while lost in thought, I was suddenly snapped back to reality. Korval said we had about fifteen minutes until we arrived. This was my sign to get ready—prepare any way I saw fit. Double-check my dress was set just right. Hat hiding my ears. No smiling. Standard procedure.

It was a relatively warm day. The sun was shining, and I looked like a bronze beauty.

---

The house was a bit covered in ivy and just looked a little overgrown. A small stone path led to the front door. When we parked, an old lady walked out.

"Osyra. You're Rosalyn, right? My grandmother?"

"Ah yes, I got a phone call saying that you would be around soon."

"That must have been my boss."

"Lieutenant something. I can't remember his name."

"He worries too much."

"Well, that means he cares about you, honey. We can talk about it inside. Does your driver want to come in too?"

I side-eyed Korval. "No. He prefers the quiet."

Korval said nothing. He just nodded slightly, then walked to the front of the car and sat on the hood. His sunglasses reflecting.

"He'll head to a hotel when it gets dark. Back in the morning," I explained.

"All right then, sweetheart." She turned around and walked inside.

I looked back to Korval for just a second, nodded my head, and walked inside following her.

Once inside, I chose a comfortable seat close to the door. I didn't want to wander too far into the home without an invitation. The seat was almost too comfortable. The age had made it relax into the exact shape of a body. I looked around the room. The light was not blinding. It was also not dull. It was relaxing. The wood trims on the walls, the furniture, everything felt like it was a snapshot back in time. The smell in there was of newspaper and sunshine.

Somehow I felt like this was a memory in the back of my mind. Something I missed. Something I needed. For today, the plan was to sleep here tonight and go on the next mission tomorrow. Korval and I had agreed to meet up in the morning, and he would transport me to the next location.

"What do you do for work, dear?"

"Can't say much. It's military."

"Oh, I understand. I don't want to get you in any trouble," Rosalyn said softly.

"Combat unit. Mostly eliminating dangerous animals."

"What a world we live in. They come up with new jobs every day, don't they?" She smiled. "As long as they're paying you well and treating you right."

"They are," I said.

"How about some pictures? So I can remember you once this day's over." Rosalyn had been wandering around this whole time. She finally reached onto a counter and picked up a Polaroid.

I felt my face go red. I didn't take pictures. This was completely embarrassing. I couldn't deny her. That'd be cruel. But to accept was just so damn awkward. I'd have to consider this. Did she take a picture of me? Should she take a picture of the person I pretended to be? "Later," I told her.

"Of course, honey. You must be tired."

Just then there was a knock on the door. "Now who could that be?" Rosalyn said, turning toward the sound.

It was Korval. What could he possibly want, I wondered. I got up and walked over.

"Captain Renaya, I have to inform you—headquarters has assigned you a new agent. They'll be taking notes on your operations for records. Commander says their name is Vanity."

I stared at him.

"You don't need to do anything on your end. They've already swapped out the agent. Contact them through normal means. Chain of command says we still have to inform you that there was a change."

"What about Corporal Ryan?" I asked.

"He's been reassigned. There's nothing we can do," Korval said.

"Bullshit. I don't want some rookie I have to break in."

His name was Jerry Ryan. It took me a couple of months to explain the way I'd like my notes. Aggression, nihilism, and all. At first I didn't even want to take notes at all. This was the compromise between me and corporate. And then he took on a few more people. I think he had four people under him. He was taking the notes for them. What a paper pusher. But honestly, I did really enjoy his company. It was gone now. Such was life.

I finally spoke again. "Do you know where they're taking him?"

"All I was told was standard transfer," Korval said. "Anyway, that's all I have to report, Captain. I'm out." He did a short bow, turned, got in the car, and left. Like there was nothing else to do in the world. Stiff, mechanical.

I shut the door and turned around to face Rosalyn, but she wasn't there. She seemed to be in the kitchen. I wandered back to the comfortable chair. Sat down. It was relaxing. It took the edge off. I couldn't have explained the military secrets if I had to. I kept these thoughts to myself.

For a moment I was alone with my thoughts. I had nothing to think about except what this new recruiter would be like. Hopefully they figured out how to do things my way. She eventually brought in some tea. It was hard to pay attention to that. The comfortable chair was lulling me to sleep as my ever-energetic body was slowing down. Hopefully the tea would increase my energy again. I didn't feel it happening. The weight of the world was crushing me. I'd been running for so long, I hadn't even noticed how tired I was. I accepted the cup from her almost greedily. Anything to stay awake for this moment.

"What was that about?" she asked.

"I have a new crew member I have to train. Someone at the office. It's not really a big deal." I took a sip of the tea. It smelled wonderful. Spiced chai. One of my favorites.

She nodded slowly, the way someone does when they don't really understand but want you to know they're listening. She set her own cup down on the side table and made her way to the bookshelf at the far end of the room. Her fingers ran along the spines until they stopped on something thick and softbound. She pulled it out with both hands.

"I dug this out before you came," she said. "Thought you might want to see."

She brought it back over and lowered herself into the chair across from me. The album opened with a soft creak of old leather. Pages thick as cardboard. The kind that had little black corners stuck on to hold each photo in place. She turned to the first page and angled it toward me.

"This is your great-grandmother. My mother. She was a tough old thing."

I leaned forward enough to see. A black-and-white photograph of a woman in a high-collared dress, hair pulled back so tight it looked painful. She wasn't smiling. None of them did, in those days.

"She looks strict," I said.

"Oh, she was. But she'd give you the last bite off her plate if she thought you needed it more." Rosalyn smiled and turned the page. A few more faces. Names I didn't catch. People who had lived and died long before I'd ever drawn a breath.

I let her go. It was easier than answering. The chair was already pulling at me, and her voice had a rhythm to it, like wind moving through a screen door.

She turned another page, and the rhythm changed.

"And this," she said, more carefully now, "is your mother."

I looked.

A young woman, sitting on the steps of a porch I didn't recognize. Thin. Tired in a way that didn't belong on a face that young. She was holding something in her lap — a bundle. A baby. She wasn't looking at the camera. She was looking down at the bundle.

I tried to remember her. I tried to picture the porch, the sound of her voice, anything at all. I came back with nothing. A blank wall where a person should have been. Whoever she was, she lived in someone else's head. Not mine.

"How old was she?" I asked.

"Just barely an adult." Rosalyn touched the edge of the photograph with one finger. "She and your father — they didn't have anything, sweetheart. Not really. Not enough for a child. They tried, for a while. But there's a kind of poor that doesn't bend, and they were in it." She paused. "I would have taken you in myself if I'd known. By the time I found out, your aunt had already stepped up. And she was good for you. I told myself that."

I nodded. There wasn't anything to say to that.

I took another sip of the tea.

She turned the page slower this time. "And there she is. Your aunt."

The picture was in color. A woman in a kitchen, hair tied back with a faded bandana, laughing at something off-camera. There was flour on her hands.

I knew that kitchen. I knew the bandana. I knew the laugh — loud, sudden, a little embarrassing in public. I knew the smell of her bread, which she made every Sunday and ruined every other time she tried. I knew the way she said my name when she was tired but not angry. I knew the weight of her hand on my back the night she taught me how to hold a knife properly. I knew the morning she didn't come down for breakfast, and how the house had felt wrong before I'd even started up the stairs to check on her.

"She was kind," I said.

"Was she happy?" Rosalyn asked. Quiet. Not pushing. Just wanting to know.

I thought about that. She had been a lot of things. Tired. Stubborn. Full of stories. Scared, sometimes, of things she wouldn't talk about. But happy?

"I think so," I said. "Most of the time."

Rosalyn's eyes had gone wet, but she didn't make a thing of it. She nodded once and turned the page.

The next photo stopped me for a different reason.

A man — middle-aged, broad-shouldered — standing in a clearing somewhere wooded. A rifle propped against his shoulder. A deer at his feet, freshly taken. He was smiling, but it was a tight kind of smile, like he wasn't really comfortable with the camera.

"My husband," Rosalyn said. "He's been gone a long time now. He'd have liked you, I think. He used to do something a bit like what you do — tracked nuisance animals, mostly. Bears that got into the trash. The occasional cat that started taking livestock."

I felt something tighten in my chest. Of course he did.

"He'd ask you a thousand questions about it," she went on. "What kind of rifle, what range you work at, how often you have to take a shot at running game. He always wanted to know more than he let on."

"It's mostly close work," I said. Carefully. "Tracking. Containment. The shooting part isn't the hard part."

"That's what he used to say." Rosalyn laughed once. "Said anyone could pull a trigger. Hard part was being sure you should."

I made myself smile a little. Not enough to show teeth. "Yeah."

She watched me for a half-second longer than felt natural. Then she turned the page.

A few more photos. A house she used to live in. A garden that was no longer there. Then, finally, she closed the album in her lap and rested both hands flat on the cover.

"I've been hoping the agency would call for years," she said. She said it like she was admitting to a small thing, not a heavy one. "I knew about you, you know. Always knew there was a granddaughter out there somewhere. Just couldn't ever find the thread to pull."

I didn't know what to do with that. I sat with the cup in my lap and stared at it.

"This place gets quiet," she said. "Quieter than I thought it would. The phone doesn't ring much. My hands aren't what they were. Some mornings I open jars with the back of a butter knife." She laughed, soft. "Don't tell anyone."

"I won't."

She looked at me a moment longer, then patted the album once, gently, like saying goodnight to it.

"You look tired, honey."

I was tired.

"Why don't you take the back room. The one at the end of the hall. The bed's made up. I'd been hoping you might."

I nodded. I didn't trust my voice.

I set the cup down, stood up, and let her show me the way.

The back room was small. Wood paneling on the walls, a single bed made up with a hand-stitched quilt, a low dresser, a window that looked out into the dark of the yard. A second door on the right opened onto a bathroom — I could see a strip of tile through the gap.

"Towels are under the sink," Rosalyn said. "Hot water takes a minute. Give it a minute."

"Thank you."

"There's a nightshirt in the top drawer if it fits. Was mine, a long time ago. You're a fair bit taller than I ever was, but it might do." She paused at the doorway. "I'll be in the kitchen. Tea if you want it. Otherwise — sleep well, sweetheart."

She closed the door behind her.

I stood in the middle of the room and listened. Her footsteps moved off down the hall. A cabinet opened. A faucet ran. Then the slow shuffling sound of a woman who'd walked the same floorboards long enough to do it without looking.

I let out a breath I hadn't realized I was holding.

---

I undressed in pieces.

The hat first. I set it on the dresser the way you'd set something fragile. My ears straightened and gave a slow twitch — they'd been pressed flat for hours and the muscles ached. I rubbed at the base of them with both hands. A small, embarrassing relief.

Then the dress. The hem caught briefly on my tail, the way it always did. I shook it loose and folded it on the chair. My tail uncurled from where I'd kept it tucked tight against my leg, and I felt that familiar prickle of pins and needles as the circulation came back.

I sat on the edge of the bed and worked the contacts out, one at a time, blinking them into the little case. The world went a little crisper. Reds went a little redder. I stared at myself in the dresser mirror — really stared, for the first time all day.

Long ears. Long tail. Red eyes that caught the lamplight in a way no human eye ever did.

I looked like myself.

I hated how much I'd missed it.

---

The shower helped.

I stood under it longer than I needed to. Hot water on the back of my neck, on the muscles along my spine, on the place between my shoulder blades that always carried tension I couldn't quite name. The fur on my ears and tail flattened out and turned a darker black. I washed my hair twice. I didn't have a reason. It just felt good.

When I stepped out I wrapped one of Rosalyn's towels around myself. It was too small for my frame, but it covered what mattered. I rubbed my hair down with a second towel, and then I did the thing I only did when no one could see me.

I shook. Head, ears, the whole frame of me — one hard sharp shake the way a dog does coming out of a lake. Water flew everywhere. The mirror got it. The wall got it. A spray hit the ceiling and beaded back down in slow fat drops.

I didn't have to do that. I could have held perfectly still — every twitch I made was a choice. That was what made it embarrassing. The shake wasn't something I couldn't help. It was something I picked, every single time, because it felt good every single time.

I shook again. Smaller. Just to feel the ears whip. The floor was a wet mess. I did not care.

The nightshirt was in the top drawer like she'd said. Soft cotton, faded blue, embroidered along the collar in a pattern I didn't recognize. On her it had probably come down past her knees. On me it landed a few inches above. The sleeves were short. I pulled it on. The fabric caught on my tail and I worked it through and let the tail hang free behind me.

It would have to do. I wasn't planning on standing up.

---

I opened the bathroom door and stepped out into the bedroom.

Rosalyn was standing in the doorway to the hall with a folded blanket in her hands.

For a long second neither of us moved.

I'd left the bedroom door unlocked. That was my mistake. I'd assumed she'd knock. I'd assumed a lot of things, apparently.

Her eyes went to my ears first. Then my tail. Then back up to my face — to the eyes I hadn't put the contacts back in for.

The blanket was still folded in her hands.

I stood there in the borrowed nightshirt and tried to make myself smaller. Tail still. Ears down. Hands at my sides. Tried to take up less of the room than I actually did.

It didn't work. There was no version of standing in a doorway under a lamp that didn't look like this. The ears. The tail. The eyes. The edge of a canine wherever my mouth wasn't fully closed. Holding still just gave her eyes longer to settle on each piece.

I saw her see it. Her hand tightened on the blanket. Her chin came up an inch. The kind of stillness a person goes into when their eyes are still catching up to what's in front of them.

Here it was. The part I knew.

I'd seen this before. Not from her — from receptionists, landlords, a doctor or two. The face going polite like a curtain coming down. The excuse to step into another room. The phone call to my chauffeur ten minutes later: something's come up, I'm so sorry. The goodbye that was really, "please don't come back", dressed up nice. I knew every beat. I'd gotten good at making the leaving easy on the person doing it.

I just hadn't expected to need the practice on her.

Then her shoulders came back down. The flicker passed. She let out a breath, and when she looked at me again she wasn't looking at a predator. She wasn't even looking at a stranger.

She was looking at me the way you look at a child who's come in from the yard with something scraped up that needs seeing to before bed.

"Oh, sweetheart," she said. Very softly. "Come into the light. Let me look at you properly."

I didn't move at first. The catalogue in my head said this was a different version of goodbye. The one where she'd look me over carefully so she could tell herself later that she had tried. I'd been told kindly to leave before. The kindness didn't make the leaving stop. I kept watching her hands for the moment they'd find something to do that took her out of the room.

But she wasn't moving toward the door. She was moving toward me.

The whole frame of my panic shifted sideways and didn't quite fit anymore. The frustration of being seen — and I had been frustrated, viciously, in the half-second after the door opened — drained partway out of me. Not all the way. I didn't trust the rest of it yet. I'd had this feeling before. It usually broke.

She didn't step into the room like you'd step into a barn with a wary horse. She just stepped in. She set the blanket down on the bed and held out a hand toward me, palm up, the way you'd ask a child to come closer so you could check something.

I went over. I don't know why. My body did it before my head caught up.

She brought her other hand up — slow, asking with her face. I tilted my head down so she could reach. She touched the outside of one of my ears. Gentle. The kind of touch you'd use checking the edges of a scab.

"Does this hurt?"

"No." I was still waiting. The hand on my ear, this close, was where the recoil usually came in. The little involuntary pull-back. The face you make when you've touched something and only just registered what it was.

It didn't come.

"Has it ever — when it was happening. Did you get sick from it? Fevers? Skin trouble where the fur came in?"

"Not after the first year."

"Mm." She turned my head a fraction to look at the other ear. Her thumb traced just behind the base of it, where the fur met skin. Clinical. Worried in the small specific way you're worried about a thing you might still be able to fix. "Can you move them?"

I flicked one ear forward. Then the other. Then both at once.

"Oh, that's clever."

I nearly lost my composure on the word. Clever. It was the wrong word. It was the word you used when a kid showed you a small thing they were proud of. It wasn't anywhere in the catalogue. I didn't have a slot for it. I kept waiting for the next thing she said to take it back.

"And the tail?" she said.

I moved it once, slow, side to side. Then stopped it dead.

"All right." She let go of my ear and stepped back half a step to take stock of the whole of me, the way you take stock of a kid's full report after they've come in crying.

And then her face did a thing.

Not the recoil. Something else. Something I'd been less prepared for, because I'd never gotten this far with anyone. Her mouth went thin. Her eyes went a little bright in a way that wasn't softness.

I went very still. Whatever this was, the catalogue didn't have it either.

"And you walked into my house," she said, "in a hat. And a dress. And those contacts that hurt your eyes."

"Yeah."

"All afternoon."

"Yeah."

"Sitting in my chair and drinking my tea and not smiling at me because you didn't want me to see your teeth."

"Yes."

There it is, I thought. Different shape, same animal. Not disgust — disappointment. I'd taken her hospitality under false pretenses. I'd let her bring out a photo album and tell stories to a person who hadn't really been in the room. The half-step she'd let me close was about to slip, and I started getting my body ready to give it back to her without making it her problem.

Then she spoke again, and I realized the bright thing in her eyes wasn't pointed at me. It was pointed at the air just past my shoulder. At the world I'd had to walk through to get to her front door. At the version of me she'd had to meet first instead of this one.

"Sweetheart." There was still an edge on it. "You don't ever do that in this house. Do you understand me? Not in this house. I don't care what you look like under there. I'd have wanted to know you straight off."

I didn't answer right away. I was still working out whether I'd heard her right. I kept turning the sentence over to see if there was a hook in it I'd missed.

There wasn't.

"All right," I said. Small.

"Sit down, honey. Tell me what happened to you. Slow if you need to."

I sat.

The bed creaked under me. My tail came to rest across my lap and I put one hand on it without thinking. A nervous tell.

"It was the lab," I said. "When I was sixteen."

She nodded once.

"After Auntie passed, the state put me in foster care. The people they placed me with sold me to the lab. That's how I got there."

She didn't nod this time. She went very still.

"They were experimenting on people. Kids, mostly. Most of them didn't survive. I did."

"What did they do to you." It came out rough. A voice I hadn't heard from her all day.

I took a breath. Lined up the part she could hear.

"It was a virus. They had a whole shelf of versions of it — numbered. The one they gave me was called Chimera-9. That's the one that makes you part wolf. The other kids got other numbers."

I rubbed at my eye with the back of my wrist.

"Mine took. Most of them didn't. The ones that did sometimes came out worse than I did. Things that stopped looking like people on the way through. I don't know what was done with those ones after."

She didn't say anything.

"After the place came down, the agency found me. Put me to work. The dangerous-animals part isn't quite right — most of what they send me out for isn't an animal at all. Cryptids, mostly. Things that warp the air around them. Objects that don't behave the way objects should. I track them. I observe. I write up what was there. Sometimes I have to put one down. The killing isn't usually the point."

She was quiet a long moment.

"The biggest hurdle is I can't go outside without a disguise," I said. "People see me and they get scared. Or they get violent. Or they get scared and call somebody who'll get violent. The hat and the dress and the contacts — that's how I buy groceries. That's how I get on a bus. That's how I knock on a front door without somebody phoning it in."

"Mm." She didn't disagree.

"The teeth are the worst part. The rest you can hide if you're careful. Can't hide a smile."

"May I see, sweetheart? You don't have to."

I opened my mouth a little. Canines longer than they had any business being, sharp where they ought to have been rounded.

She looked. She didn't look away.

"Your aunt would have loved you exactly like this," she said.

I had to look at the floor for a minute.

---

She didn't leave right away. I think she knew that if she stood up to go I'd start crying, and that wasn't a thing either of us wanted to do in front of the other.

So she sat in the little chair by the dresser and asked me, very gently, about the small things. Did the ears hurt under the hat. Did my tail get cold in winter. Could I eat normal food. Did I sleep on my back or my side, with a tail.

I answered most of them. Some of them I laughed at — a small dry laugh that came out before I could stop it. The contacts were the worst part, I told her. Twelve hours and they felt like sand.

"Then take them out, honey. In this house. Always."

"Okay."

"And the hat."

"Okay."

She paused. Her mouth went a little quirky on one side. "Now. I'll only say this once. The state of that bathroom floor isn't from the shower, is it."

I felt my face go hot all at once.

"No."

"Mm-hm."

"I was drying off."

"You were drying off."

"In a particular way."

"Sweetheart, I lived with a man who used to come home from the woods wearing half the mud. A wet floor isn't going to scandalize me. Just maybe put a towel down next time."

I made a noise into the back of my hand. Half a laugh, half wanting to die. The first sound out of me all day that I hadn't calculated.

"There it is," Rosalyn said, pleased with herself.

She watched me for another second, then her face went easy again. "And if you'd like to sleep without the nightshirt because of the — " She gestured, vaguely. " — the arrangement back there. You go right ahead. It's just me here."

I almost laughed again. "I'll keep the nightshirt."

"Suit yourself." She smiled. "I just don't want you uncomfortable."

She stood, finally. The chair creaked under her the way the bed had under me. She picked up the blanket from the foot of the bed and shook it out and laid it across again, neater this time.

"In case you get cold," she said. "Old houses."

"Thank you."

She paused at the door. Her hand on the frame.

"Osyra."

"Yeah."

"I'm glad you came."

She closed the door softly behind her.

I sat in the lamplight for a long time before I lay down. My tail curled around against the small of my back, the way it always did when I slept. My ears flattened against the pillow.

I had not, in fact, fallen apart.

I'd spent the last hour sitting in front of someone as myself. Ears out. Tail out. Mouth closed only out of habit, not because I had to. Nothing in me ached the way it always did at the end of a day in the disguise. Nothing in me was bracing.

It was a small thing. It was a thing I hadn't known I could miss.

The house creaked. Settled. Somewhere down the hall, a kettle clicked off.

I closed my eyes.
