# Chapter 9 - Broken Down

The first week was not about being different. It was about being broken down.

The humiliation was total, but it was democratic. It wasn't personal, because nothing here was personal. You were a body in uniform, and your body was weak, slow, and stupid. When one recruit fumbled a drill step, the entire platoon paid for it in push-ups on the gravel, the DI's voice shredding the air above them. When someone's footlocker was a millimeter out of alignment during inspection, they all stood at attention for an hour in the biting cold as he methodically dumped every locker in the bay onto the floor. The lesson was a blunt instrument: you are a cell in an organism. One cell fails, the whole body suffers.

Her unique status as the "attachment" meant she stood apart during formations, but she suffered alongside them during the consequences. She did the push-ups. She stood in the cold. She scrubbed. And she stood a full head taller than almost everyone while doing it. At 6'4", her height had always been a fact; here, in ranks of recruits who averaged 5'10", it became a permanent feature of the landscape. She had to consciously slump during some drills to not break the line, a habit the DIs quickly beat out of her. "STAND AT YOUR GOD-GIVEN ATTENTION, RENAYA! DON'T APOLOGIZE FOR THE BLUEPRINT!"

There was so much cleaning. Floors had to gleam like obsidian. Latrines had to smell of nothing but bleach. They scrubbed with toothbrushes, with rags, with their own fingernails. The smell of industrial cleaner became a permanent note in the back of her throat. Her height was an advantage here; no shelf was out of reach, and she was often tasked with the high dusting and light fixtures while others grumbled at baseboards.

The physical training was a relentless grind. Calisthenics at 0400 in the dark. Long, grueling runs in formation, the pack's rhythm a punishing, collective heartbeat. Her enhanced physiology handled the cardio better than most-her lungs burned, but they didn't scream-and her long legs gave her a stride that made the runs feel almost natural, though she had to constantly check her pace to avoid overtaking the file. The sheer, repetitive volume of it wore on everyone. Her muscles ached with a new, deep kind of fatigue, and her tail often felt like a dead weight, a clumsy appendage she had to consciously tuck and control during exercises.

By the third day, a plague of respiratory illness swept through the bay. The sound of wet, hacking coughs and congested sniffling was a constant chorus. Sinus pain made faces puffy and eyes watery. The DIs showed no mercy. "I DON'T CARE IF YOU'RE COUGHING UP A LUNG, MAGGOT! YOU MOVE!" She watched recruits stumble through drills, shivering with fever, only to collapse afterward. She stayed healthy-another side-effect of Chimera 9; the lab's logs had noted a 'robust adaptive immune response'-but the misery around her was palpable, a fog of human vulnerability she looked down upon from her healthy, unwanted height.

Then came the weapons.

The armory was a temple of oil and cold metal. They filed past, and the armorer, a grizzled specialist with sergeant's stripes, called out assignments. "Smith, M4. Gonzalez, M4. Carter, M4."

He reached her. His eyes flicked from his manifest up to her face-a longer journey than for most-then to a different, heavier rack. "Renaya. Mk 13 Mod 7. .300 Winchester Magnum. Bolt-action precision rifle. With this."

He handed her the rifle. It was a beast. Over four feet long, with a thick, fluted barrel, a massive muzzle brake, and a chunky telescopic sight. The weight settled into her hands-a dense, profound thirteen pounds of cold steel, polymer, and glass. It felt less like a weapon and more like a piece of industrial machinery meant to be mounted. Compared to the M4 carbines the others cradled like toys, it was from another world. In her hands, its scale finally made sense. It didn't look oversized; it looked correct. A full-sized weapon for a full-sized frame.

A stunned silence, then a sharp, collective intake of breath. Heads turned. A recruit named Finch, two people ahead, craned his neck, his eyes wide as saucers.

Before he could even blink, the armorer's voice cracked like a whip. "FINCH! YOU SEE SOMETHING PRETTY? YOU ON A TOUR? YOUR WEAPON IS IN YOUR HANDS! EYES ON YOUR OWN GEAR UNLESS YOU WANT TO CLEAN EVERY WEAPON IN THIS ARMORY WITH A TOOTHBRUSH!"

Finch's head snapped forward so fast she heard his neck pop. The lesson was immediate: staring at the anomaly was a failure of focus. It became a pattern. During zeroing on the range, as she settled behind the massive rifle, she'd feel eyes on her. A flicker of movement in her peripheral vision.

"HILL! YOU TRYING TO LEARN BY OSMOSIS? WISHING WON'T MAKE YOUR GROUPING TIGHTER! FOCUS ON YOUR FRONT SIGHT POST OR I'LL HAVE YOU WEARING IT AS AN EARRING!"

On a march, a recruit stumbling behind her would gawk at the rifle on her shoulder or the tip of her tail, adjusting to the rhythm of the walk.

"VALDEZ! THE ROAD IS IN FRONT OF YOU, NOT ON HER BACK! YOU LOST? NEED A MAP? PICK UP THE PACE AND WATCH YOUR SECTOR!"

The DIs never mocked her for being looked at. They mocked the others for looking. It was a brilliant, ruthless piece of psychological conditioning. It made her difference not a source of personal humiliation, but a test for everyone else. A distraction to be overcome. A temptation to fail. Every time a head turned in her direction, it was an excuse for the DI to pounce, to punish, to reinforce the only thing that mattered: the task. Her presence became a metric of their discipline.

The drawback of the Mk 13 was a constant, grinding presence. But so was the advantage, which revealed itself during a tactical drill. They were simulating guarding a perimeter. The DI pointed at her. "Renaya! That cannon is a liability inside ten meters. Simulate a security hand-off. Briggs! Secure her primary!"

Briggs, who had muscles on his muscles but stood a good eight inches shorter, stepped forward. She saw his eyes dart from her face to the rifle, a flicker of that same awe and apprehension.

"BRIGGS! YOU TAKING A CENSUS OF HER FEATURES OR SECURING A WEAPON? MOVE!"

He snapped to, but the damage was done-he was already off-balance. She unslung the Mk 13 and passed it to him. The moment the full weight transferred, his body jerked. His eyes widened as his posture sank, his arms instinctively tightening to wrestle the unexpected heft and length.

"Looks like a struggle, Briggs! You admiring the craftsmanship or fighting a bear? Renaya, you're weaponless! Demonstrate a hostile take-down. Non-lethal. Go."

Unburdened by the thirteen-pound anchor, her body became pure potential energy. She was across the gap in one long stride before Briggs could reset. She used his rooted, unbalanced posture against him, her longer arms easily bypassing his guard, sweeping his legs and driving him to the mat with a controlled thud.

The DI didn't praise. He simply narrated, his voice cutting through the damp air as the platoon stood rigid, eyes fixed on nothing. "Observe. The hammer is heavy. The arm is built to carry it. A tool for every task." He paused, letting the silence hang. "Briggs, firewatch. The rest of you, look alive-at your own damn jobs."

It was a visceral, undeniable lesson. Her "different" was a tactical specification, and noticing it was a punishable lapse. The recruits learned. The stares didn't stop, but they became covert, fleeting, followed by an immediate, paranoid snap back to whatever they were supposed to be doing. She was no longer a spectacle; she was a hazard. A distraction hazard. It was the most anonymity she'd had since she got there.

The only respite was food. The chow hall was a haven of shocking, greasy abundance. The food was, against all odds, awesome. Giant portions of pasta, real eggs, mountains of potatoes. Calories as fuel, and they were furnaces. Her metabolism, coupled with her size, demanded a shocking amount of it. She ate with a single-minded, mechanical focus, often going back for seconds while others picked at their plates. Even the MREs-Meals, Ready-to-Eat-were acceptable in their weird, vacuum-sealed way. At the table, for fifteen minutes at a time, they were not maggots. They were just hungry animals, refueling in silence.

She sat alone, as always, at the end of a table. But now, the space around her felt less like an exile and more like a cleared range. No one looked too long. No one sat too close. She could eat in peace, the monstrous rifle leaning against the table like a silent guardian. She was the one who carried the thirteen-pound answer, and she was built on a scale that made it look logical. And if you were smart, you kept your eyes on your own tray.

She took a bite of gravy-soaked meatloaf. It was fuel. For now, that was enough. The rest was just physics, and she was finally starting to feel like she was built for the equation. The silence of their averted eyes was the closest thing to respect she was likely to get.
