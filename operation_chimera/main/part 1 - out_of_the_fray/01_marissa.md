# Chapter 1 - Marissa

Before any of it, there was a kitchen.

It was small and it was always too warm, because the oven ran most of the day and the window over the sink only opened halfway, swollen shut in its frame since before Osyra was old enough to reach the latch. The linoleum had a pattern on it once. By the time Osyra knew it, it was worn pale in two places — in front of the sink, and in front of the stove — two soft ghosts of a woman who stood in the same two spots every day of her life. The rest of the pattern hung on in the corners, under the table where feet didn't go, a faded thing that might have been flowers.

Marissa stood in the ghost by the stove now, her back to the room, and she was laughing.

She laughed the way she did everything, all at once and without checking the room first — a loud, sudden bark of it that filled the small space and spilled out the halfway window into the yard. Osyra was four, or five, sitting on the floor with her back against the cabinet where the pots were, and she did not know what was funny. It didn't matter. The laugh was a weather. You didn't need to know where it came from to be warm in it.

"Don't tell your — " Marissa started, and then remembered there was no one to tell, and laughed again at that too, at herself, at the reflex of a woman who had spent her life in bigger families than this one. She wiped her hands on the front of her apron and turned around, and the bandana was slipping — it always slipped by afternoon, working loose from the knot at the back of her head until a piece of dark hair fell across her forehead, and she blew it up out of her eyes instead of fixing it. There was flour on her hands. There was always flour on her hands, or dish soap, or the grease she cut with lemon and never quite got out from under her nails.

"You're going to sit under there your whole life," she said to Osyra, not unkindly. "Little cave dog."

She said things like that. Little cave dog. Osyra would carry it for years — would surface it, in fact, on a night twenty years and a different body later, and go very still when she did, because by then it had stopped being a joke.

---

The apartment was two rooms over a laundromat, and the whole place carried the laundromat's smell up through the floor: hot lint and industrial soap, a warmth that came up through the soles of your feet in winter and was the only heat worth counting on. Marissa called it a feature. She called a lot of things features. The neighbor who played his radio too loud was a feature, because it meant you were never truly alone in the dark. The bus that ran under the window every eleven minutes was a feature, because you could set your bread by it. She had a way of taking the things that were wrong with a place and turning them, out loud, into the reasons it was good, and she did it so constantly and so cheerfully that Osyra was most of the way to being a person before she understood it had been a discipline. That her aunt had been holding a line. That you could be that poor and that tired and still decide, every single morning, out loud, that the noise was company.

They did not have money. Osyra knew this the way children know things, which is completely and without any of the words for it. She knew it in the way Marissa read the mail standing up by the door, sorting it fast, some of it going straight into the drawer that didn't open all the way. She knew it in the winters, when the laundromat heat wasn't enough and they slept in the same bed with their coats laid over the blanket. She knew it in the specific, careful joy Marissa brought to small things — an orange, split in half and eaten in sections, was a whole event; a movie on the free channel was a night out. Osyra did not experience any of this as deprivation. She experienced it as the shape of the world, and the world had Marissa in the middle of it, laughing, and so the world was fine.

She did not know she'd been given up. That word — given up — she would not have understood, and Marissa did not offer it. Once, when Osyra was small enough to ask a question and not feel its weight, she asked where her mother was, and Marissa went quiet over the sink, her hands stopping in the water, and then she said, "Some people can't hold on to a thing, baby, even when they want to. Even when they want to so bad it breaks them. It's not about wanting." And she'd dried her hands and come and sat on the floor, in the pot cabinet's ghost, and pulled Osyra into her lap and said nothing else about it, and that had been the whole of it. It was the closest Marissa ever came to telling her the truth, and it was more or less the truth, and Osyra was too young to keep it. She only kept the lap, and the smell of lemon and flour, and the fact that her aunt had come down onto the cold floor to say it instead of saying it from up at the sink.

---

The bread was Sundays.

Every Sunday, the same, a thing that did not move for weather or mood or the drawer that didn't open all the way. Marissa made bread on Sunday, and it was good — genuinely good, two dark loaves with a crust that cracked when you pressed it and a smell that got into the walls and stayed till Wednesday. She was proud of the bread the specific way people are proud of the one thing they do well, which is to say she pretended not to be and watched your face closely while you ate it.

The joke of it, the family joke, the only running joke a family of two can have, was that she could only make it on Sundays. Any other day of the week she tried, it came out wrong — flat, or dense, or burned on one side and raw on the other, some new failure each time, as if the bread knew what day it was and refused. She'd stand there with an oven mitt and a ruined loaf and swear at it with real feeling, and Osyra would laugh, and Marissa would point the ruined loaf at her like an accusation and say *you think it's funny,* and it was, it was the funniest thing in the world, and then they'd eat the ruined bread anyway because you didn't waste it. Wednesday bread, they called the failures. There was almost always Wednesday bread. It was almost always terrible. Osyra ate more of it, over the years, than the good kind, and could not have told you which she missed more later, when there was no more of either.

The knife came out for the bread.

It was a real knife, a long serious one with a wooden handle gone dark and smooth from a lifetime of hands, and it was the one thing in the apartment Osyra was not allowed to touch. That made it enormous. She used to watch it the way other children watched television — Marissa drawing it down through the crust in a single smooth stroke, the loaf falling open in even slices, the blade going back to its magnet strip on the wall, up high, out of reach.

The night Marissa finally let her hold it, Osyra was eleven, and it did not feel like a small thing and it was not meant to.

"Not like that. You'll take a finger off and I'm not driving you anywhere at this hour." Marissa came around behind her and set her hand flat on Osyra's back, between the shoulder blades, a warm steady pressure, and with the other hand closed Osyra's fingers around the handle the right way. "There. You don't saw at it. You let the knife do it. It's heavier than you think and that's the point, you let the weight fall and you just — guide it. You're not fighting the bread. You're pointing it." The hand on her back did not move the whole time. "Slow. There's no prize for fast. Fast is how you get to the hospital." And Osyra drew the blade down through the crust in one long pull, the loaf falling open in even slices, clean, the same even slices that fell for Marissa and no one else, and the pride that went through her was so large and so total that she had to stand very still to hold it.

"There," Marissa said, close to her ear, and Osyra felt the word more than heard it. "There she is."

She would use a knife, in her life, to do a great many things. None of them would ever feel like that one. But she would hold every knife she ever held the way Marissa taught her that night — let the weight fall, guide it, don't fight the thing, point it — and she would not always remember why her hands already knew, and the not-remembering would be its own kind of loss, later, one she couldn't name because she'd lost the name along with the rest.

---

There is no way to tell it that makes it fair, so it will just be told.

Osyra was eleven, and it was a Tuesday, and Marissa did not come down.

The bread on Sunday had been good — the last good loaf, though of course no one knew to mark it — and the Monday had been ordinary, and Osyra had gone to sleep in the warm from the laundromat with the bus going by under the window every eleven minutes, company, a feature. She woke on her own, which was the first wrong thing. Marissa woke her. Marissa had woken her every morning of her life, from the doorway, with her aunt's tired-not-angry voice saying her name — *Osyra* — two soft weights on the two soft syllables, the sound the whole day started with.

The apartment was quiet. Not asleep-quiet. Osyra lay in the bed and listened to the specific quality of it and something in her, some animal thing she would not have a name for or a use for until much later, some old part under the language, went cold and certain before she had thought a single thought. The house felt wrong. It felt wrong before she was out of the bed, wrong on the cold linoleum, wrong at the bottom of the stairs, and she went up the stairs anyway because there was nothing else a person could do, one hand on the wall, into the wrong.

Marissa was in her bed. She looked asleep. She looked, in fact, entirely peaceful, which was the cruelty of it — that it asked you to hope, standing in the doorway, for the half-second before you understood — and Osyra stood in the doorway and understood.

She did not scream. This is worth saying, because everything after would be built on it. Eleven years old, in the wrong quiet, with the only person who had ever kept her lying still and warm-looking and gone, and Osyra did not scream and did not run. She crossed the room and she put her hand on her aunt's back, flat, between the shoulder blades, in the place Marissa's hand had gone on hers, and it was already not warm, and she left her hand there anyway for a long time, because she did not know what else her hand was for.

That was the end of the kitchen. Osyra was eleven, and she did not know yet that the rest of her life had just been decided — that the warm two rooms over the laundromat were already gone, that the men who would come and file her into the system were already, somewhere, opening a folder — she only knew the quiet, and the not-warm, and her own hand on her aunt's back, keeping still, keeping the last of it, for as long as the world would let her.

The world did not let her long.
