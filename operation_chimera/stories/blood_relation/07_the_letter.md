# Chapter 7 - The Letter

A few moments of silence. There was something I wanted to say.

I'd been rehearsing it since the photo went on the fridge. Two hours of it - the wording, the tone, how to make it land matter-of-fact, which is a thing I was trained to do and am good at. I had a version that came out clean. Professional. The kind of thank-you you give a colleague who covered your shift. I'd run it enough times that it was safe.

The silence stretched. I didn't fill it. Neither did she.

I looked at her.

The clean version dissolved.

"Thank you for accepting me for who I am."

It came out small. The last word cracked.

She didn't say anything. She got up. Came around the table. Put a hand on my shoulder.

And that was it. That was all it took, and I'll be honest about how little it was: a hand, on a shoulder, from a woman who'd known me for nineteen hours.

I have been braced for so long that bracing stopped registering as an activity. It's not something I do. It's the shape I am. I built the disguise, and the silence, and the whole catalogue of polite goodbyes for after people see what I am, and every piece of that was engineering - deliberate, tested, load-rated. It works. It has never failed.

Nothing in it was built for a hand on my shoulder that meant it.

I broke.

It was short and hard and I couldn't stop it, and I did try - I want that much down - that I did the thing I do, went for the tension, went to take hold of my own breathing the way I've done in worse rooms than this one, and there was nothing there to take hold of. The controls were not connected to anything. I have never once in my adult life not been able to do that.

She was worried. She asked if she'd done something wrong. She hadn't. I couldn't get my voice to work well enough to tell her, which meant for about forty seconds my grandmother sat there believing she'd hurt me, and I couldn't fix it, and that made it worse, which made it longer.

When it was done I was empty.

That's the only word for it. Something that had been carried was, briefly, not being carried. No responsibilities. No hiding. Not here, anyway. Not for a minute.

I apologized. I didn't know what else to say. It was the only sentence I had in the drawer that fit.

"What are you apologizing for, sweetheart."

I wiped my face with the back of my wrist. The words came out flat - the debrief voice, because it was the only one I trusted with this.

"I'm used to being told to leave. Every time. By the third meeting somebody starts looking for a reason to end it. I came in here expecting that. I didn't have a frame for the other thing."

"Mm."

"I'm not asking you to feel bad. It's just what's true. I came in ready to leave."

She didn't make a thing of it. She kept her hand where it was a long moment, then let it slide off in a way that didn't feel like leaving.

She sat back down across from me. Picked up her mug. Didn't fill the silence. I've noticed she does that - she leaves the room for you to walk into, and doesn't stand in the doorway while you decide.

I took a drink of mine. The room had gone brighter, the window letting in enough that the lamp wasn't pulling its weight anymore. Her eggs had gone cold. She didn't seem to notice.

"Can I ask you something."

"Anything, sweetheart."

I looked at the picture on the fridge a second. Then back at her.

"My mother. My father. The ones in the photo."

She nodded once.

"Are they - " I couldn't find the rest of it.

"Still living?"

"Yeah."

She took her time with the answer.

"As far as I know. Last word I had was a few years back. They settled in the end - bought a small patch of land up north and put a trailer on it. They grow most of what they eat. There's a kind of struggling that goes quiet once it stops moving."

I nodded. Didn't trust my voice yet.

"I might have something, though. Let me see."

She got up slow and went down the hall. Past the room I'd slept in. To a door at the end I hadn't noticed, which I'd like to flag, because I notice doors. That's not a boast, it's a condition. I'd catalogued that hallway on the way to bed and I had four doors and she opened a fifth, and it means the catalogue was already coming apart in that house, and had been since the chair.

Hinges complained. A drawer opened. Papers moved. Something heavier - a box - scraped across a wood floor. The sound a person makes when they're not sure where the thing is, only that it's in there.

I stayed at the table. Drank my coffee. Looked at the photo on the fridge so I wasn't looking at nothing.

After a while she came back. Slower than she went. An envelope in one hand and a small folded paper in the other.

"Here." She set them down. "Last letter I got from your mother. Few years old now. It's got a return address - a P.O. box for a small town up north. They'll be near it. They wouldn't have left that land." A pause. "She used to write me once a year. Christmas. Then it stopped. I figured she ran out of things to say. Could mean they're fine."

I picked up the envelope. The handwriting on the front was careful in the way handwriting gets when paper is a thing you don't waste. I know that hand. Not hers - the type. I've seen it on foster paperwork, on forms filled out by people who are being careful because being careless costs money.

"They're proud, mind you," she said. Quiet. "They wouldn't take money. Wouldn't take charity. They taught themselves how to do everything because they had to - how to grow, how to mend, how to fix what most people would throw out. If you go to them - and I'm not telling you to or not to - you bring something they can use, not something that makes them feel small."

"Like what."

She thought about it. "Things you can't pull out of a garden. Salt. Coffee. Spices. Good vinegar. The little things people stop having when there's nothing left at the end of the month." She looked at me. "You'd know better than me, once you'd seen them."

I turned the envelope over. The flap was still sealed at one corner where she'd opened it.

"You take it," she said. "It belongs to you more than it belongs to me."

I didn't answer.

I put the letter against my chest. Hadn't planned to. My hand did it, the same way it went across the kitchen when she held hers out.

She watched me do it and didn't say a word about it.

"Thank you," I said.

"Of course, sweetheart."
