# The Visit

Osyra was nervous. There were so many things that could go wrong.

She thought it while looking out the window as Korval drove her to the destination. He was up front, behind the wheel — Korval, a tough, Russian ex-military type. Her chauffeur and bodyguard both. He always followed her everywhere. Not that she needed it. She was usually the dangerous one in any room.

The agency had turned up word of one of her biological family members and given her the opportunity to visit. She'd taken it. Her grandmother — Rosalyn Renaya. Despite Osyra's protests — that she didn't need a bodyguard just to blend in as family — her boss had insisted and would not back down.

"How much further until we get there?" she said. The words came out before she'd decided to say them.

"About thirty minutes," Korval answered in his thick Russian accent. Then he added, "Why are you so impatient? You're always calm."

A strange sensation washed over her. She'd never wanted to be close to family before. Maybe this was a new experience. She had nothing to say back. No reply. She stared out the window as the metropolis turned into rural neighborhoods.

"Okay, we're just about there," Korval said. "Make sure you dress appropriately."

For a moment she'd nearly forgotten that her grandmother didn't know about her altered form. She climbed into the back seat and changed her jeans for a dress. Put a hat on, added contacts. She felt like a completely different person. She couldn't tell which version of her was real. The one where she was Chimeran? Or the one where she was human?

---

She was Chimera now. She wasn't always Chimera. This came from a lab. She'd been given up for adoption as an infant. Her aunt had taken her in and raised her until she passed. After that she'd gone to foster care — they were the ones who sold her to the medical facility. She was sixteen. They experimented on her. They experimented on many other kids. Most of them didn't make it. She did.

She became a Chimera — part wolf, part human. You couldn't classify her as human or animal. While they were running their tests, there was a breach. Someone blew up part of the building. The facilities shut down, and she got free. They wrote it off as a total loss.

But then came the problem: how to get work? How to get money when she looked like this? She was discovered by someone sifting through the paperwork in the building. They recognized her, found her, and offered her a job. Provide her capabilities — which had proven adequate. They wanted her to hunt down and fix things. Deal with anomalous entities and situations. The pay was good. She got to be the secret they didn't point and laugh at.

She had wanted to go outside occasionally, so she'd learned how to conceal her form:

- Color contacts to hide her red eyes
- Big hats to hide her ears
- Long dresses to hide her tail

Didn't need to smile much. You couldn't see her canines anyway.

People she came into contact with just thought she was angry. That was good enough. They left her alone.

After a while lost in thought, she was suddenly snapped back to reality. This was her sign to get ready — prepare any way she saw fit. Double-check her dress was set just right. Hat hiding her ears. No smiling. Standard procedure.

It was a relatively warm day. The sun was shining, and she looked like a bronze beauty.

---

The house was a bit covered in ivy and just looked a little overgrown. A small stone path led to the front door. When they parked, an old lady walked out.

"Osyra. You're Rosalyn, right? My grandmother?"

"Ah yes, I got a phone call saying that you would be around soon."

"That must have been my boss."

"Lieutenant something. I can't remember his name."

"He worries too much."

"Well, that means he cares about you, honey. We can talk about it inside. Does your driver want to come in too?"

Osyra side-eyed Korval. "No. He prefers the quiet."

Korval said nothing. He just nodded slightly, then walked to the front of the car and sat on the hood. His sunglasses reflecting.

"He'll head to a hotel when it gets dark. Back in the morning," Osyra explained.

"All right then, sweetheart." Rosalyn turned around and walked inside.

Osyra looked back to Korval for just a second, nodded her head, and followed her grandmother in.

Once inside, she chose a comfortable seat close to the door. She didn't want to wander too far into the home without an invitation. The seat was almost too comfortable. The age had made it relax into the exact shape of a body. She looked around the room. The light was not blinding. It was also not dull. It was relaxing. The wood trims on the walls, the furniture, everything felt like it was a snapshot back in time. The smell in there was of newspaper and sunshine.

Somehow she felt like this was a memory in the back of her mind. Something she missed. Something she needed. For today, the plan was to sleep here tonight and go on the next mission tomorrow. She and Korval had agreed to meet up in the morning, and he would transport her to the next location.

"What do you do for work, dear?"

"Can't say much. It's military."

"Oh, I understand. I don't want to get you in any trouble," Rosalyn said softly.

"Combat unit. Mostly eliminating dangerous animals."

"What a world we live in. They come up with new jobs every day, don't they?" She smiled. "As long as they're paying you well and treating you right."

"They are," Osyra said.

"How about some pictures? So I can remember you once this day's over." Rosalyn had been wandering around this whole time. She finally reached onto a counter and picked up a Polaroid.

Osyra felt her face go red. She didn't take pictures. This was completely embarrassing. She couldn't deny her grandmother. That'd be cruel. But to accept was just so damn awkward. She'd have to consider this. Did Rosalyn want a picture of her? Should she take a picture of the person Osyra pretended to be? "Later," she said.

"Of course, honey. You must be tired."

Rosalyn drifted toward the kitchen, murmuring about putting on the kettle. Osyra stayed in the comfortable chair and let her go. She'd update Vanity later about all of it — the visit, the grandmother, whatever else the afternoon turned up. Vanity kept the record on Osyra's one-woman war against paperwork. The job didn't stop for family.

Rosalyn eventually brought in some tea. It was hard for Osyra to pay attention to that. The comfortable chair was lulling her to sleep as her ever-energetic body slowed down. Hopefully the tea would increase her energy again. She didn't feel it happening. The weight of the world was crushing her. She'd been running for so long, she hadn't even noticed how tired she was. She accepted the cup from her grandmother almost greedily. Anything to stay awake for this moment. She took a sip. It smelled wonderful. Spiced chai. One of her favorites.

Rosalyn watched her drink for a moment. Then she set her own cup down on the side table and made her way to the bookshelf at the far end of the room. Her fingers ran along the spines until they stopped on something thick and softbound. She pulled it out with both hands.

"I dug this out before you came," she said. "Thought you might want to see."

She brought it back over and lowered herself into the chair across from Osyra. The album opened with a soft creak of old leather. Pages thick as cardboard. The kind that had little black corners stuck on to hold each photo in place. Rosalyn turned to the first page and angled it toward Osyra.

"This is your great-grandmother. My mother. She was a tough old thing."

Osyra leaned forward enough to see. A black-and-white photograph of a woman in a high-collared dress, hair pulled back so tight it looked painful. She wasn't smiling. None of them did, in those days.

"She looks strict," Osyra said.

"Oh, she was. But she'd give you the last bite off her plate if she thought you needed it more." Rosalyn smiled and turned the page. A few more faces. Names Osyra didn't catch. People who had lived and died long before she'd ever drawn a breath.

Osyra let her go. It was easier than answering. The chair was already pulling at her, and Rosalyn's voice had a rhythm to it, like wind moving through a screen door.

Rosalyn turned another page, and the rhythm changed.

"And this," she said, more carefully now, "is your mother."

Osyra looked.

A young woman, sitting on the steps of a porch she didn't recognize. Thin. Tired in a way that didn't belong on a face that young. She was holding something in her lap — a bundle. A baby. She wasn't looking at the camera. She was looking down at the bundle.

Osyra tried to remember her. She tried to picture the porch, the sound of her voice, anything at all. She came back with nothing. A blank wall where a person should have been. Whoever this woman was, she lived in someone else's head. Not Osyra's.

"How old was she?" Osyra asked.

"Just barely an adult." Rosalyn touched the edge of the photograph with one finger. "She and your father — they didn't have anything, sweetheart. Not really. Not enough for a child. They tried, for a while. But there's a kind of poor that doesn't bend, and they were in it." She paused. "I would have taken you in myself if I'd known. By the time I found out, your aunt had already stepped up. And she was good for you. I told myself that."

Osyra nodded. There wasn't anything to say to that.

She took another sip of the tea.

Rosalyn turned the page slower this time. "And there she is. Your aunt."

The picture was in color. A woman in a kitchen, hair tied back with a faded bandana, laughing at something off-camera. There was flour on her hands.

Osyra knew that kitchen. She knew the bandana. She knew the laugh — loud, sudden, a little embarrassing in public. She knew the smell of her aunt's bread, which she'd made every Sunday and ruined every other time she tried. She knew the way her aunt had said her name when she was tired but not angry. She knew the weight of her aunt's hand on her back the night she taught her how to hold a knife properly. She knew the morning her aunt didn't come down for breakfast, and how the house had felt wrong before she'd even started up the stairs to check on her.

"She was kind," Osyra said.

"Was she happy?" Rosalyn asked. Quiet. Not pushing. Just wanting to know.

Osyra thought about that. The aunt had been a lot of things. Tired. Stubborn. Full of stories. Scared, sometimes, of things she wouldn't talk about. But happy?

"I think so," Osyra said. "Most of the time."

Rosalyn's eyes had gone wet, but she didn't make a thing of it. She nodded once and turned the page.

The next photo stopped Osyra for a different reason.

A man — middle-aged, broad-shouldered — standing in a clearing somewhere wooded. A rifle propped against his shoulder. A deer at his feet, freshly taken. He was smiling, but it was a tight kind of smile, like he wasn't really comfortable with the camera.

"My husband," Rosalyn said. "He's been gone a long time now. He'd have liked you, I think. He used to do something a bit like what you do — tracked nuisance animals, mostly. Bears that got into the trash. The occasional cat that started taking livestock."

Osyra felt something tighten in her chest. Of course he did.

"He'd ask you a thousand questions about it," Rosalyn went on. "What kind of rifle, what range you work at, how often you have to take a shot at running game. He always wanted to know more than he let on."

"It's mostly close work," Osyra said. Carefully. "Tracking. Containment. The shooting part isn't the hard part."

"That's what he used to say." Rosalyn laughed once. "Said anyone could pull a trigger. Hard part was being sure you should."

Osyra made herself smile a little. Not enough to show teeth. "Yeah."

Rosalyn watched her for a half-second longer than felt natural. Then she turned the page.

A few more photos. A house Rosalyn used to live in. A garden that was no longer there. Then, finally, she closed the album in her lap and rested both hands flat on the cover.

"I've been hoping someone would call for years," Rosalyn said. She said it like she was admitting to a small thing, not a heavy one. "I knew about you, you know. Always knew there was a granddaughter out there somewhere. Just couldn't ever find the thread to pull."

Osyra didn't know what to do with that. She sat with the cup in her lap and stared at it.

"This place gets quiet," Rosalyn said. "Quieter than I thought it would. The phone doesn't ring much. My hands aren't what they were. Some mornings I open jars with the back of a butter knife." She laughed, soft. "Don't tell anyone."

"I won't."

Rosalyn looked at her a moment longer, then patted the album once, gently, like saying goodnight to it.

"You look tired, honey."

Osyra was tired.

"Why don't you take the back room. The one at the end of the hall. The bed's made up. I'd been hoping you might."

Osyra nodded. She didn't trust her voice.

She set the cup down, stood up, and let Rosalyn show her the way.
