# Chapter 3 - The Visit

I was nervous, which took me most of an hour to identify.

That sounds absurd. I have been shot at. I know what fear does in my body down to the second - the drop, the narrowing, the way sound goes flat and useful. This wasn't that. This was diffuse and pointless and had no object I could name, and I sat in it a long time before I got a word onto it, because there was nothing to fight and nothing to leave and so the instrument had nothing to point at.

I looked out the window while Korval drove. He follows me everywhere. Not that I need it. I am usually the dangerous one in any room, and both of us know it, and neither of us has ever said so.

The agency had turned up word of a biological family member and given me the opportunity to visit. I'd taken it. My grandmother. Rosalyn Renaya.

"How much further until we get there?" The words came out before I'd decided to say them, which was the second bad sign in an hour.

"About thirty minutes," Korval said, the accent thick as ever. Then: "Why are you so impatient? You're always calm."

I had no reply. I'd never wanted to be close to family before - there'd been nobody to want it at, so the wanting had never had anywhere to go. Now there was somewhere, and it turned out the wanting had been sitting there the whole time, fully assembled, waiting for an address.

I stared out the window as the metropolis turned into rural neighborhoods.

"Okay, we're just about there," Korval said. "Make sure you dress appropriately."

I had nearly forgotten that my grandmother didn't know about my altered form.

I climbed into the back seat and changed my jeans for a dress. Put a hat on. Put the contacts in.

I felt like a completely different person. I could not tell which version was the real one. I have never been able to, and I have stopped expecting the question to resolve itself on a drive.

---

It was a warm day. The sun was out. In the window glass I looked like somebody's daughter on her way to lunch, and that was the point of the exercise, and I did not enjoy how well it worked.

---

The house was covered in ivy and looked a little overgrown, in the way of a place tended by someone who has decided which battles are worth it. A small stone path led to the front door. When we parked, an old lady walked out.

"I'm Osyra, and you're Rosalyn, right? My grandmother?"

"Ah yes, I got a phone call saying that you would be around soon."

"That must have been my boss."

"Lieutenant something. I can't remember his name."

"He worries too much."

"Well, that means he cares about you, honey. We can talk about it inside. Does your driver want to come in too?"

I gave Korval a subconscious side eye. "No. He prefers the quiet."

Korval said nothing. He just nodded slightly, then walked to the front of the car and sat on the hood. His sunglasses reflecting.

"He'll head to a hotel when it gets dark. Back in the morning," I said.

"All right then, sweetheart." Rosalyn turned around and walked inside.

I looked back at Korval for a second, nodded, and followed my grandmother in.

Once inside I took a seat close to the door.

I told myself it was manners - that I didn't want to wander into a stranger's home uninvited. That held up for about four seconds. It was the door. It's always the door. I do not sit down in a room without knowing the count of exits, and I was doing it here, in an ivy-covered house belonging to a sixty-eight-year-old pediatrician, against the possibility that I would need to leave her living room at speed. I considered getting up and moving to the couch, just to prove I could.

I didn't. I stayed where I was and let myself have the door, and told myself I'd move later, which is what I always tell myself.

The seat was almost too comfortable. Age had let it relax into the exact shape of a body. The light wasn't blinding and wasn't dull. The wood trim, the furniture - all of it a snapshot back in time. The place smelled like newspaper and sunshine.

It felt like a memory, which was not possible. I have never been in that house. But something in the back of my head kept insisting it recognized the room, and I couldn't make it stop, and after a while I stopped trying and just sat in the chair that knew what a person was shaped like.

The plan was simple: stay the night, see what the morning looked like. There was no job at the end of this one. Korval would be back after breakfast, and after that the days were mine - the first time in years that sentence had been true.

"What do you do for work, dear?"

"Can't say much. It's military."

"Oh, I understand. I don't want to get you in any trouble," Rosalyn said softly.

"Combat unit. Mostly eliminating dangerous animals."

"What a world we live in. They come up with new jobs every day, don't they?" She smiled. "As long as they're paying you well and treating you right."

"They are," I said.

"How about some pictures? So I can remember you once this day's over." She'd been wandering the whole time we talked. She reached onto a counter and came up with a Polaroid.

My face went red.

I don't take pictures. There's an operational reason and I reached for it first, because it's clean and it's true and it isn't the reason. Then I got to the actual problem, which was this: she wanted a picture of her granddaughter, and the thing sitting in her chair was a woman in a hat with brown contacts over red eyes. If I said yes she'd get a photograph of somebody who doesn't exist. She'd put it on the shelf with her mother in the high collar. Forty years from now somebody would find it and never know they were looking at a costume.

I could take the hat off. The thought arrived fully formed and I looked at it the way you look at a live wire on wet ground. Right there, in the good chair, in the light, two hours from the lodge and thirty seconds from finding out whether the whole day had been a mistake. She'd have flinched or she wouldn't. That's all it would have cost. One second of information.

I have made harder decisions in worse rooms with people shooting at me.

"Later," I said.

"Of course, honey. You must be tired."

She drifted toward the kitchen, murmuring about the kettle. I stayed in the chair and let her go, and sat with the fact that I'd just declined to find out something I'd driven two hours to learn. I told myself it was pacing. Give her the afternoon first. Ease into it. That's a strategy, and it is also what a coward says, and both were true at once and I couldn't get a blade between them.

I'd call Vanity tonight, the way I'd been told. Once a day, so somebody at headquarters knew I was still breathing. Just my voice down the line, and Vanity on the other end taking down whatever I felt like saying, patient, keeping it for the record.

She brought in tea eventually. I had trouble paying attention. The chair was pulling me under, my body slowing the way it never slows, and I kept waiting for the tea to fix it and it didn't. I'd been running so long I hadn't noticed I was tired. I mean that plainly. I had no idea. The instrument doesn't point inward, and it had been reading *fine* for years while the answer was *no*.

I took the cup almost greedily. Anything to stay awake for this.

Chamomile.

I got about half of it down before the joke landed on me. She hadn't brought me anything to stay awake on. There was nothing in that cup but hot water and a flower, and I'd been drinking it like a stimulant and waiting to be fixed by it.

Which was fine. Coffee costs me an hour of my stomach arguing, and tea's the same at half the price - it's a canine thing, one of the small ones, an annoyance rather than a problem, and it is not the sort of annoyance a person mentions to a host. I'd have drunk whatever she handed me and paid for it later and said nothing, the way I always do.

She hadn't asked. I want to be careful here, because I have watched people be kind at me for twenty-nine years and I know what it looks like when someone has done their homework and wants credit for it. That wasn't this. There was no glance to see if I'd noticed. She'd looked at a woman who'd driven two hours and hadn't slept and was sitting in her front room with her hands wrong, and she'd gone and boiled the thing you give somebody who needs to come down.

Forty years of small frightened people, and it comes out of her hands automatically, and she doesn't know she's doing it.

She watched me drink for a moment. Then she set her own cup down on the side table and went to the bookshelf at the far end of the room. Her fingers ran along the spines until they stopped on something thick and softbound. She pulled it out with both hands.

"I dug this out before you came," she said. "Thought you might want to see."

She brought it back over and lowered herself into the chair across from me. The album opened with a soft creak of old leather. Pages thick as cardboard. The kind with little black corners stuck on to hold each photo in place. She turned to the first page and angled it toward me.

"This is your great-grandmother. My mother. She was a tough old thing."

I leaned forward enough to see. A black-and-white photograph of a woman in a high-collared dress, hair pulled back so tight it looked painful. She wasn't smiling. None of them did, in those days.

"She looks strict," I said.

"Oh, she was. But she'd give you the last bite off her plate if she thought you needed it more." She smiled and turned the page. A few more faces. Names I didn't catch. People who had lived and died long before I ever drew a breath.

I let her go. It was easier than answering. The chair was pulling at me and her voice had a rhythm, like wind through a screen door.

She turned another page, and the rhythm changed.

"And this," she said, more carefully now, "is your mother."

I looked.

A young woman, sitting on the steps of a porch. Thin. Tired in a way that didn't belong on a face that young. She was holding something in her lap - a bundle. A baby. She wasn't looking at the camera. She was looking down at the bundle.

I knew the porch.

That's what got me. Not the woman. The porch - the paint coming off the third step in a shape like a bird. I have put my hand on that step. I couldn't tell you when or why, but the knowing came up out of my body without asking permission, the way a smell does, and it was certain, and it was worth nothing.

I looked at the face and got nothing back. Six years of a person and all I kept was a step.

I've been told this is normal, at various points, by various people paid to tell me things. That nobody keeps much from before seven. That the mind is still deciding what it is. It has not once helped to be told, and it doesn't now, and I have never understood why people offer the explanation as though it were the same thing as the memory.

"How old was she?" I asked.

"Just barely an adult." Rosalyn touched the edge of the photograph with one finger. "She and your father - they didn't have anything, sweetheart. Not really. Not enough for a child. They tried, for a while. But there's a kind of poor that doesn't bend, and they were in it."

I nodded. There wasn't anything to say to that.

I took another sip of the tea.

She didn't turn the page. She sat with her hand flat on it, the way you hold a door you haven't decided to open.

"They wouldn't let me near you," she said. "That's the bit I've never gotten all the way around. I knew you were born. I knew where. She'd answer the phone maybe one time in five, and she was always about to call me back." Her thumb moved once against the paper. "I think she couldn't stand for me to see it. The state of things. She'd have rather starved than had her mother-in-law standing in that kitchen counting what wasn't in it. And I let her have that. Six years, I let her have her pride, because I kept telling myself I'd get one more chance to ask."

She said it evenly, which was worse than if she hadn't.

"Then a school nurse filled out a form," Rosalyn said. "Some woman who'd never met any of us, doing her job, writing down what she saw. And that was enough to take you. But not one person in that whole process thought to ask whether the child had anybody." She looked at her hands. "They had the name. It's the same name. It was in the phone book."

I looked at the woman on the porch steps instead of at her. It seemed easier for both of us.

"You couldn't have known they'd take me," I said.

"No," Rosalyn agreed. "But I could have driven down. Any Tuesday of those six years, I could have driven down and knocked, and I didn't, because she'd have been embarrassed." She let out a breath and it shook coming out, and she got it back under control the way a person does when they've had years of practice. "That's the whole of it, sweetheart. I was being polite. I'm sorry. You didn't come here to hold this for me."

Then, quieter, not really to me at all: "There's no picture of you after that one. I've looked. Not one anywhere in this house, because nobody was taking any."

The album stayed open on the porch steps a moment longer than it needed to. Then she turned the page.

The next photo stopped me for a different reason.

A man - middle-aged, broad-shouldered - standing in a clearing somewhere wooded. A rifle propped against his shoulder. A deer at his feet, freshly taken. He was smiling, but it was a tight kind of smile, like he wasn't comfortable with the camera.

"My husband," she said. "He's been gone a long time now. He'd have liked you, I think. He used to do something a bit like what you do - tracked nuisance animals, mostly. Bears that got into the trash. The occasional cat that started taking livestock."

Something went tight in my chest. Of course he did.

"He'd ask you a thousand questions about it," she went on. "What kind of rifle, what range you work at, how often you have to take a shot at running game. He always wanted to know more than he let on."

"It's mostly close work," I said. Carefully. "Tracking. Containment. The shooting part isn't the hard part."

"That's what he used to say." She laughed once. "Said anyone could pull a trigger. Hard part was being sure you should."

I made myself smile. Not enough to show teeth. "Yeah."

She watched me for a half-second longer than felt natural. Then she turned the page.

A few more photos. A house she used to live in. A garden that was no longer there. Then she closed the album in her lap and rested both hands flat on the cover.

"I've been hoping someone would call for years," she said. She said it like she was admitting to a small thing, not a heavy one. "I knew about you, you know. Always knew there was a granddaughter out there somewhere. Just couldn't ever find the thread to pull."

I didn't know what to do with that. I sat with the cup in my lap and stared at it.

"This place gets quiet," she said. "Quieter than I thought it would. The phone doesn't ring much. My hands aren't what they were. Some mornings I open jars with the back of a butter knife." She laughed, soft. "Don't tell anyone."

"I won't."

She looked at me a moment longer, then patted the album once, gently, like saying goodnight to it.

"You look tired, honey."

I was tired.

"Why don't you take the back room. The one at the end of the hall. The bed's made up. I'd been hoping you might."

I nodded. I didn't trust my voice.

I set the cup down, stood up, and let her show me the way.
