# Chapter 8 - The Road North

Korval's car came down the road outside, slow on the gravel.

Rosalyn looked toward the window. Then back at me.

"Oh, sweetheart."

"Yeah."

"I don't have your number."

"I don't have yours."

We both moved at once.

She went for a drawer by the wall phone and pulled out a pad of grocery-list paper. I got my phone out. Korval honked once outside - polite.

"How fast can you write," I said.

"Faster than you'd think."

She called her number out from memory. Ten digits, slow and clear. I saved her as ROSALYN R, no embellishment. Then I called mine out, twice, because I didn't trust the first time. She wrote it down in handwriting that hadn't gotten any less careful since the envelope.

Korval honked again. Less polite.

"He doesn't normally do that twice."

"Then we'd better move."

I slung my bag. She pressed the envelope and the folded letter into my hands.

"Take care of yourself, sweetheart."

"I'll call you."

"You'd better." A small swat at my shoulder.

I want it noted that a woman I met yesterday hit me. Lightly. As a scolding. I have no record of a relative doing that at any point in my life, and I did not have a reaction ready, and so my face did nothing at all, which I think she read correctly anyway.

I nodded. Turned toward the door. Got to it. Stopped. Turned back.

"I'll come back when I can."

"I know, honey."

I walked to the car. Korval had the back door open and was looking pointedly somewhere that was not the porch. I got in. The door closed.

He pulled away.

She was on the porch when I turned to look. She raised a hand. I raised mine. The house got smaller.

"Good visit?" Korval asked.

"Good visit."

He didn't ask what was in the envelope. He never asks.

---

The road toward the highway ran straight a long while, fields on both sides going gold in the late light. I sat in the back with the envelope in my lap. I'd meant to put it in the bag. My hands hadn't done it.

I turned it over. The return address in the corner, careful handwriting, a P.O. box for a town I'd never heard of, somewhere up north. My mother's hand. A few years old, she'd said. *They wouldn't have left that land.*

"Korval."

"Mm."

"How far north can this car go before you start complaining."

He met my eyes in the mirror for the first time all day. Considered it. Looked back at the road.

"It is a good car," he said. "It does not complain. The driver, maybe, needs coffee."

"There's coffee on the way."

"Then the car goes as far as you want."

I looked at the address a moment longer.

I could have gone home. That was the whole shape of the thing: I'd come for a grandmother, I'd got one, the operation was complete and successful and nothing about it required a second act. Going north was not the assignment. Going north meant knocking on a trailer door to ask two people why they'd kept me on nothing for six years and then let a nurse's form decide the rest of it, and I did not have a question prepared, and I have never once walked into a room without knowing what I was there to get.

I went anyway. I'd like to be able to tell you what I was going for. I couldn't have told Korval if he'd asked, which is possibly why he doesn't.

Then I clipped the comms pin to the base of my ear and keyed it.

"Chimera One to Wiki."

"Wiki receiving. That's twice in two days you've called when nobody made you."

"I'm extending. Few more days. Maybe a week."

A pause that was Vanity deciding how much to make of it, and deciding on nothing. "Extending where."

"North. Rosalyn gave me a letter. There's a return address on it." I turned the envelope face-up against my knee. "My mother. My father. They're up there. Or they were."

"And you're going to knock on that door too."

"I'm going to drive to the town and see if the box is still rented. After that I don't know."

"Then I don't either." Her voice didn't change, but the cadence did - the version of her that writes things down without being asked. "I'll move your leave to open-ended and keep the desk off you. Same arrangement. Call me once a day, just so I know you're alive."

"You keep saying that like it's a real risk."

"You keep going to places where it is." A half-beat. "Bring them something useful, Operator. Not money. Rosalyn's right about that."

I didn't ask how she knew what Rosalyn had said. She always knows more than she's been told; it's the whole of what she's for, and asking would only make her explain it, and then I'd have to know.

"Roger that."

"Drive safe. Let the man have his coffee."

The pin cooled a degree. Open, idle. I left it.

I slid the envelope into the inside pocket of my jacket, next to the photograph from 1985. The grandmother, and now the way to the rest of them, riding together over my heart.

"North," I told Korval.

"North," he agreed, and took the on-ramp the other way than home.

The fields gave out to trees. The trees got taller and closer to the road, and the light through them went the ordinary green of a long evening, and I watched the town where I'd found my grandmother fall away behind me, and let myself, for once, look forward to the next thing instead of bracing for it.
