# Chapter 1 - The Hunting Lodge

The lodge smelled like cedar and old smoke. The agency picked it for me on purpose - a hunting lodge at the dead end of a logging road, the kind of place where a tall woman who comes and goes at strange hours and brings back strange cargo is just a hunter. Nothing more. A hunter living in a hunting lodge. Nobody looks twice. That is the entire point of it, and I have never decided whether I resent how well it works.

The walls are cedar gone honey-dark with age. Taxidermy everywhere - a whitetail buck over the cold fireplace, a brace of pheasants frozen mid-flush, a bobcat on a shelf with glass eyes that catch the light better than the real ones ever did. An antler chandelier hangs over the main room, a dozen sheds wired together into something meant to read as rustic luxury, which mostly reads as a lot of dead animals over your head while you eat. I didn't choose a single piece of it. The agency dressed the set before I moved in. I've only added to it, here and there, when a job left me something worth keeping.

My equipment fits the lie without trying. The charge rifle stands in the rack by the door, between a hunting bow and a lever-action that has never been fired. The lead-lined containment boxes stacked in the mudroom look like gun cases. The breaching tool hangs on a pegboard of axes and saws and looks like family. A stranger walking through would see a hunter's home and nothing underneath it.

The disguise is the house. The house is good. I live inside a costume that is also a building.

I got back near dawn. The job was done and bagged - routine, contained, nothing I'd lose sleep over. The thing I pulled out of it sat by the door in a vault box, sealed, quiet now that the lid was shut. I'd run it to headquarters in the morning and not think about it again.

So I told myself.

It wasn't the box I kept circling back to.

It was the photograph on my nightstand.

I found it months ago, on another job - a house at the edge of a town that had thinned out around it, empty for decades, behind cracked glass in a hallway nobody had walked in twenty years. I took it off the wall without deciding to. That should have told me something. I do not do things without deciding to; the whole architecture of me is built to prevent exactly that. I took it anyway, and I haven't been able to put it down since. It travels from coat pocket to table to the nightstand by my bed, where I set it most nights before I sleep, the way some people set down a watch. I've told myself a dozen times to log it and let it go.

I picked it up and stood over it in my coat, contacts already in, looking at it the way I look at everything I don't understand yet - which is to say, too long, and from too many angles, as though the answer is a matter of finding the correct approach.

The colors have gone bad with the years. Reds drained, blues turned green, the whole thing sitting behind its cracked glass like it gave up trying to be a photograph. The face didn't go with the colors. A young woman, hair parted in the middle, smiling at someone off-frame. The eyes aren't red. The ears are ordinary.

But the bones underneath are a face I have been looking at in my own mirror my whole life without knowing whose it was.

I don't have a name for the feeling. I have names for very few of them; it's a gap in the instrument I've never bothered to close, because for most of my life nothing has come up that required the distinction. This did. And somewhere in the night I'd decided: today I take it to Vanity, and stop pretending I don't need to know.

Headlights swept the front windows. Gravel under tires. Korval, on time, the way he is always on time.

I slid the photograph into the inside pocket of my coat, against my chest, and picked up the box.

Outside, the cold met my face and my ears flattened back under the hat by themselves. I let them. Korval's SUV idled in the turnaround, exhaust ghosting white in the dark.

"Morning," he said as I set the box on the back seat.

"Headquarters." I got in. "I have something to report. And something that isn't a report."

He didn't ask. He never asks until I want him to, and I have never worked out whether that's discipline or disinterest, and I have never asked either, which probably answers it.

He pulled out onto the logging road. The lodge shrank in the side mirror - cedar and dark glass, the antler chandelier a faint warm smudge through the front window where I'd left one lamp burning.

Then the trees took it, and there was only the road, and the box, and the photograph against my chest like a second heartbeat I hadn't asked for.
