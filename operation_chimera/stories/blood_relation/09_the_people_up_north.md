# Chapter 9 - The People Up North

The drive took the better part of a day.

The highway thinned as it went, four lanes down to two, two down to a county road with grass coming up through the seams. The country changed with it. Fields gave out to woods, woods to low hills, and the towns got further apart and smaller in between, each one a gas station and a church and a handful of houses that had been good once and were holding on now.

I watched it go by from the back seat. I've seen poor. I've worked in it. But there's a difference between a city's poor - loud, crowded, fast, a thing happening *to* people at speed - and this. This is quiet. This is a place where the struggling stopped moving long enough to put down roots and grow a garden in it.

*A kind of struggling that goes quiet once it stops moving.* Rosalyn's words. They fit the window.

Korval didn't talk much on the long stretches. He drank his coffee from the gas station two towns back and let the road do the talking, and once, when a deer stepped out onto the shoulder and thought better of it, he slowed without a word and let it change its mind.

---

I called Vanity in the early afternoon, somewhere between a town with a name and a town without one.

"Chimera One to Wiki."

"Wiki receiving. Still got a world."

"Still got a world. Getting close."

"Mm." The sound of her setting it down somewhere only she can read. "The box is still rented, by the way. I checked. Same name on it as the letter. Renewed eight months ago, in cash, at the counter."

I closed my eyes a second. Eight months. Recent enough.

"You weren't going to tell me you checked."

"I just did tell you." A half-beat. "I didn't want you driving a day on a dead address. That's all. The rest is yours to find."

That's the arrangement with her, and it took me years to see the shape of it. She'll pull the thread that saves you a day. She won't pull the one that saves you the walk. She has never once explained the difference and she has never once gotten it wrong.

"Thanks, Vanity."

"Call me tonight." And she was gone.

---

The town was called Hadley's Fork, and it was a feed store, a diner, a post office the size of a shed, and a general store that sold a little of everything because there was nowhere else to sell it.

I went in disguised. Hat. Dress. The contacts already grinding by the time I pushed the door open. The man behind the counter looked up, took me in the way men in small stores take in a stranger, and went back to his crossword when I didn't turn out to be interesting.

I took my time.

Salt - the good coarse kind, two boxes. A bag of whole coffee beans and a second of ground, because I didn't know if they had a grinder. Black pepper. A jar of cinnamon and one of cardamom. A bottle of cider vinegar with some age on the label. A tin of good tea, on a guess about my mother. Matches. A coil of decent rope, because rope is rope and everyone up here uses it.

Nothing that looked like charity. Everything that looked like a person had thought about it.

I stood in that aisle longer than the shopping needed. It's the only problem I've been handed in years that I could actually work - a real one, with constraints, with a right answer. Two people I've never met, too proud for money, and a budget of what fits in a bag. That's a puzzle. I can do puzzles. I understood while I was doing it that I was hiding in the arithmetic, and I let myself, because it was the last thing between me and the road out of town.

At the counter I paid cash, like everyone does, and when the man rang it up he said, "Passing through?"

"Looking for family," I said. True enough to say flat.

"Mm. Lot of family up these roads." He bagged the salt. "Most of it likes to be left alone."

"I'll keep it polite."

He nodded like that was the right answer and didn't ask the name. I'd had a half-dozen ways ready to not answer. Didn't need any of them.

---

The land was four miles out, up a road that stopped being paved and then stopped being graded and was, by the end, two ruts and a strip of weeds down the middle that hissed along the underside of the car.

Korval took it slow. The trees pressed close. Then they opened.

A clearing. A trailer set back from the road on cinder blocks, its skirt patched with sheet metal in three different colors, every patch square and deliberate, done by someone who'd had to learn how. A garden beside it - bigger than a garden, near an acre, fenced against deer with posts that didn't match and wire that had been spliced and respliced until it was more splice than wire. Rain barrels at the corners of the roof. A woodpile, stacked tight and high and covered, that said somebody here takes winter seriously and meets it head-on every year. A clothesline. A dog run with no dog in it, the gate standing open.

Everything mended. Nothing new. The whole place a long argument with going without, and winning it, barely, one season at a time.

I read that clearing the way I read a site, because that's the instrument I have. Entry points. Sightlines. Where a person would come from. And what it gave me back, item by item, was competence. Every single thing in that clearing was a problem somebody had solved with their hands because nobody was coming to solve it for them.

I have been told my whole life that I came from nothing. I was looking at the nothing. It had a fence around it that had been repaired eleven times.

Korval stopped the car at the mouth of the clearing, well back from the trailer, the way he stops anywhere he doesn't yet understand. Put it in park. Did not turn the engine off.

"You want me closer."

"No. Here's good. Better they see the car than have it sneak up."

"Mm." He looked at the trailer through the windshield, then at me in the mirror. For once he didn't look away. "You are not going in as the soldier."

"No."

"As the other one. The salt and the coffee."

"As the granddaughter," I said. "If that's a thing I get to be twice."

He considered that, seemed to find it acceptable, and went back to watching the trees.

---

I got out.

I gathered the bags from the seat beside me - salt and coffee and the rest of it, the weight of a person who'd thought about you before she'd met you - and stood in the ruts a moment and let myself notice how loud my own heartbeat had gotten.

Smoke off the stovepipe. Thin and pale against the evening. Somebody was home. Somebody was inside, right now, doing the ordinary thing of keeping warm.

The contacts itched. The hat sat heavy. The dress was wrong for the road and wrong for the place and wrong, I understood standing there, for the whole of what I'd come to do.

Rosalyn's voice again. *You don't ever do that in this house.*

But this wasn't Rosalyn's house. This was a door I'd never knocked on, held shut by two people who gave me away because the world didn't leave them another choice, and I did not know - standing in the ruts with a bag of salt in each hand - whether they'd open it to the careful stranger, or to what was under the hat, or at all.

Here is what I had: no plan. Nineteen hours ago I'd have called that impossible. I don't move without a plan; the plan is the thing that keeps the room from happening to me. I had run every version of this on the drive and every version required knowing what I wanted, and I didn't, and I still don't, and I got out of the car anyway.

I made myself walk.

Past the garden. Past the woodpile. Up the two cinder-block steps to the door, where a coffee can of cigarette butts long gone to rust sat on the rail and a windchime made of bent spoons turned without ringing in the still air.

I set the bags down on the step. Freed my hands.

My fingers went to the brim of the hat. Stopped there. Held it.

I didn't decide. There wasn't time to decide. There was only the door, close enough now to see the grain in it, and the smoke overhead, and the small ordinary sound of someone moving around inside - a chair scraping, a cupboard, a voice I couldn't make out saying something to no one in particular.

I raised my hand, and knocked.
