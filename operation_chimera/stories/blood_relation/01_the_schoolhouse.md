# The Schoolhouse

The agency called it urbex on the paperwork. That was for tax reasons. What it really was: Osyra walking into places people had stopped going into for a reason, and finding out what the reason was.

The school had been closed for twenty years. Boarded. Fenced. The fence had rusted out. The neighborhood around it had built itself up and then thinned back down, and the school was the one thing that hadn't changed. The agency had been keeping it on a quiet list almost as long as it had been closed.

Every few decades, the school woke up again.

That was what the file said. Osyra had read the file in the car. She didn't believe the file. She didn't disbelieve it either. The files were never wrong, exactly. They were just usually missing the part that mattered.

Korval parked a block down, in a spot that had probably been a curb once.

"Backup?" he said.

"You'd slow me down."

"I'll be here."

She got out. Closed the door without slamming it. Walked.

---

The chain on the front gate had been replaced inside the last year. New padlock. New steel. Somebody had been in. The agency hadn't logged it. Civilians, then, or somebody from a list Osyra wasn't read into. She cut the chain with the breaching tool from her bag, slid the gate the minimum, slid through, slid it shut behind her.

The playground was a tetanus exhibit. Weeds had come up through the asphalt in shapes that almost looked deliberate.

The front doors were boarded. She went around to the side. The side door had been kicked in years ago — old damage, weather inside the frame — and she stepped through the broken plywood into a hallway that smelled like forest of mold from weather damage. There was a strange cold where expected humidity should be.

She knew that cold. She'd worked in it before.

The light through the windows was the wrong color. Not the wrong color of a sunset or a storm. The wrong color of nothing she could put a name to. The agency had a name for it. The name didn't help.

She moved.

---

Lockers down both walls. Some intact, some pulled half off the brick. Old papers on the floor — homework mostly. A spelling test from 1989 with a star sticker still on it. The sticker had outlasted the kid by some margin, probably.

She stepped past.

The hallway was lined with classroom doors. Half were padlocked. Half stood open. She didn't go in the open ones yet. The open ones were the ones somebody wanted her to look in.

She went up the stairs.

The wrong-color light got thicker on the second floor. She came around the landing into cold that hit her face like a freezer door left open. Her ears flattened back under the hat by themselves. She let them.

The classroom was halfway down the hall. She knew before she opened the door. The cold pressed against the doorknob like it wanted to keep her out.

She went in anyway.

---

A kindergarten room.

Tiny chairs in a circle. A rug shaped like a tortoise. A chalkboard that still had a date on it: SEPTEMBER 14. No year. The year did not seem relevant.

And the voice.

It was a small voice. A child. Reciting the alphabet, slow, the way a kid does when they don't yet know all the letters. *A. B. C. D.* The voice paused at D the way a child pauses at D when they're not sure what comes next.

The voice was not coming from the room. The voice was coming from the closet.

Osyra unholstered. Approached. The closet door was the louvered kind, the louvers warped, the dark beyond them too dark for the time of day.

*E. F. G.*

She opened the closet.

There was no child.

There was a cassette tape player. The kind a teacher used to use, with a cassette in the deck — a child's voice on Side A in cheap handwritten marker, the writing faded but still readable. The reels were not turning. The voice was coming out of it anyway.

*H. I. J.*

She unplugged it.

The voice kept going. *K. L. M.*

She thumbed the EJECT button. The mechanism whirred. The cassette popped up into her hand.

The voice kept going. *N. O. P.*

She held the tape in one palm and the player in the other and waited. The voice did not care which one she was holding. It kept going.

She picked the player up properly, kept the tape in her other hand, and carried both out of the closet. Set them on the kindergarten teacher's desk.

*Q. R. S. T. U.*

She opened her bag and got out the agency containment box — black, lead-lined, a switch on the lid. Set the player inside. Set the tape in on top of it, just to be sure. Closed the lid. Flipped the switch.

The voice cut off mid-letter.

The cold in the room broke. Not slow. All at once. The air came back up to ordinary, the way it does when a freezer door has been shut long enough, and the wrong-color light went back to ordinary afternoon light coming through ordinary dirty windows.

She had done the right thing.

She let out a breath she hadn't realized she was holding.

---

She turned to face the classroom again.

The kindergartner chairs. The tortoise rug. The chalkboard with SEPTEMBER 14. And on the wall by the door, behind cracked glass, the wall of class photos.

She hadn't noticed them on the way in.

A row of photographs, one for each year. Each photo: a kindergarten class arranged on bleachers, a young teacher in the middle. The same young teacher, for years. The hair changed. The dress changed. The face stayed.

The brass plates underneath the photos read MRS. RENAYA, with a year. 1985. 1986. 1987. All the way up to 1995.

Osyra looked at MRS. RENAYA, 1985 for a long time.

The colors had gone bad with the years. Reds drained, blues turned green, the whole photograph sitting behind cracked glass like it had given up trying to be a photograph. But the face hadn't gone with the colors. It looked back at her from inside a 1985 classroom and looked too much like Osyra to leave on the wall. Hair darker. Longer. Parted in the middle. The eyes were not red. The ears were ordinary. But the bones underneath were a face Osyra had been looking at in her own mirror her whole life without knowing whose it was.

She lifted the frame off its nail. The cardboard backing came away half-rotted in her hand. She pried the rest of it loose, slid the faded photograph free, and tucked it into the inside pocket of her jacket. The empty frame and the brass plate she set face-down on a low chair so nobody would step on the glass.

Renaya was not a common name. She'd grown up answering to her aunt's surname. She hadn't learned her own until she was nine, and a piece of foster paperwork had it printed on a line. Even then she hadn't thought of it as hers. She had thought of it as evidence.

Osyra took her phone out and photographed every remaining plate, every year. 1986 through 1995. Those she left in place. They were not her job. Vanity could do the rest.

She picked up the box.

Walked out.

---

Korval was where she'd left him, reading a folded newspaper on the hood.

"Done?"

"Done."

"What's in the box?"

"A cassette player that won't shut up."

He nodded the way he always did, like that was an ordinary answer.

She got in the back seat. Settled the box on the floor between her feet. The box stayed quiet. The containment had held all the way out the door, down the cracked path, through the gate. She would hand it to the containment desk at headquarters and not think about it again.

She would think about MRS. RENAYA, 1985.

Korval pulled into the road. The school shrank in the rearview.

Osyra tipped the hat back a finger's width and clipped the comms pin to the base of her left ear — a small plastic device, hair-clip shaped, designed to seat against the fur of a wolf ear without pulling. The pin warmed a degree against the skin as it found the channel. She seated the hat down again. Touched the side of her jaw to key transmit.

"Chimera One to Wiki. I have something to report."

A pause. Vanity processed faster than she answered, always. Then her voice, clean and level through the pin.

"Wiki receiving. Go ahead, Chimera One."

"Anomaly contained. Source object is a portable cassette deck and a tape, both bagged together in a vault box. Voice persisted after power-off and after the tape was ejected. Lead containment muted it, eventually quieted it. Room came back to normal on close. I'd recommend the deeper vault."

"Acknowledged. Vault four is open."

"There's one more thing." A small adjustment. "Off the record."

"Off the record, Chimera One."

"Wall in the kindergarten room had a row of class photos. Teacher on every one of them. Brass plate reads Renaya. 1985 through 1995. I have the 1985 photograph with me, off the wall. The rest I have on my phone."

A different pause. Not because Vanity needed to think. Because she had already thought.

"Stand by."

"Standing by."

The channel went quiet. Osyra waited. She knew what Vanity was doing on the other end. She had built her career on knowing what Vanity was doing on the other end. Her handler was going to take whatever was in those photos and run it against every Renaya the agency had ever logged. Including the one whose name was on Osyra's foster paperwork.

The road outside the window kept moving. Korval drove the way he always drove. He did not look at her in the rearview.

The channel came back.

"Chimera One. Stay on this line until you're home. We'll want to talk."

"Roger that."

The pin in her ear cooled a degree. Open, idle. She left it.

She did not turn to look at the school as it disappeared behind them.
