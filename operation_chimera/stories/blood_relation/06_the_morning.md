# Chapter 6 - The Morning

I woke before the sun.

The house had that early quiet - the kind where you can hear the floor settling and the refrigerator cycling on and off down the hall and nothing else. My ears caught the click of pipes somewhere on the far side of a wall. I lay there a minute. Tail flat against the small of my back. Ears flat on the pillow.

I'd slept hard. I can't tell you the last time I slept hard.

I got up the way I move on jobs. Quiet. That one isn't a choice anymore and I've stopped pretending it is.

---

The hall floor was cold. The kitchen was darker than the bedroom - heavy curtains, a north-facing window. I felt my way to the lamp on the counter and clicked it on low.

The Polaroid was where she'd left it the day before, on the counter next to a ceramic jar of pens.

I looked at it for a while.

She'd offered. I'd put her off. I'd told myself she meant a picture of the version of me she'd been allowed to meet - the hat, the dress, the careful unsmiling mouth - and I hadn't wanted to hand her that.

That wasn't the version she meant. I know that now. I knew it the second she said *come into the light*, and I'd spent the whole night not looking directly at the fact that I'd been wrong about her before I'd even met her. I'd had her sorted into a category before the car stopped. Same as everyone. It's efficient and it's fast and it has never once been wrong in a way that cost me anything.

It was wrong here. That's the first time. I don't know what to do with a first time.

I picked the camera up. Tested the weight. Checked the cartridge - half a pack of film left. Pulled a chair out from the table and sat under the lamp.

---

The first one was for the record.

I sat straight. Held the camera at arm's length, angled down, framed for head and shoulders. Ears up. Eyes open. The kind of picture you take when you're documenting something. Shutter. The camera whirred and spat the film into my hand.

I set it face-down to develop.

The second was a profile. Turned my head left, held the camera further out. The long shape of one ear from the side, the line of my jaw, the edge of a canine where my mouth wasn't quite closed.

The third - I bit down lightly on the side of one finger and lifted my lip on the other side. Showed the tooth clean. Like a horse trader. The kind of picture you'd send a doctor who asked.

Three records. Three small pieces of evidence: *this is what I am. This is what she accepted.*

I set them face-down and let the camera rest in my lap.

Then, without really planning to, I lifted it again.

---

The fourth one I almost laughed at while I was taking it.

Two fingers up beside my face. A peace sign. The thing kids do into phones now. Camera up high, overhead, the way teenagers hold them at bus stops, head tilted a little. Ears asymmetric. One up, one back.

Shutter. Whir.

The fifth, I blew a kiss at the lens. Pursed mouth. Eyes half-closed. I felt absurd. I kept going.

The sixth was every tooth at once. Full canine display. The smile I have never given anyone in my life. It came out looking less like a snarl and more like an animal that had just been told a very good joke.

I looked at that one a long time after it developed.

---

I spread them across the table. Six. Three for the record, three for no reason except that I'd wanted to.

I've been trying since to work out what the last three were, because they weren't documentation and they weren't for her - she wasn't awake and I hadn't decided to show her. I took them in an empty kitchen for nobody. There's a word for taking pictures of yourself pulling faces at four in the morning because it's funny and you're the only one home, and the word is not one that has ever applied to me.

The peace-sign one was the best. I knew it immediately. Ears uneven. Eyes a little bright. A half-smile with nothing serious behind it.

It looked like a person. Not an exhibit.

I got up and stuck it to the fridge under a magnet shaped like a pear.

The other five I shuffled into a stack and slid under the body of the camera, face-down. She'd find them next time she used it. I could see her at this table working through them one at a time, and I knew which one she'd pick up first and which one she'd put down last, and I was right about both, which I'll get to.

I left the camera where it had been. Reset. Like nothing happened.

---

The coffee maker was the kind with a glass carafe and a metal basket. The bag in the freezer was a dark roast I didn't recognize. I scooped a generous amount and started a pot.

Eggs in the fridge. A loaf on the counter, half wrapped. Butter left out from the night before. A cast iron pan on a peg over the stove.

I know how to do this. Hadn't in a long time. Still knew how. Four eggs into a bowl, scrambled with the back of a fork. Four slices cut and set aside. Burner lit. Pan on. A knob of butter went in and started to foam.

Six years, maybe seven, since I made breakfast for anyone but myself. Korval doesn't count - Korval eats when he's told to and not when he isn't. There was a colleague who came by the safehouse on Saturdays for a while, and once or twice I made him a bacon and egg on toast. Not the same.

This was the same.

The coffee finished. I poured hers and set it at the far end of the counter, near the doorway from the hall. The kitchen filled up with butter and eggs and dark roast.

That'd do it, I figured. The coffee, at least.

The chamomile was in the cupboard over the sink, second shelf, in a tin with a hinged lid. I'd found it while I was looking for the pan and I'd stood there with the lid open longer than a person needs to.

I made myself a cup. It's what I drink. It has been what I drink for years, in my own kitchen, where nobody has ever once handed it to me.

Somewhere down the hall, floorboards.

---

I lowered the heat on the pan.

She came in wearing a long flannel robe, slippers, hair tied back loose at the nape of her neck. Blinked at the lamp. Then at me.

Then at the fridge.

Her hand came up slow and rested on the doorframe. She looked at the photo a long second. Looked back at me. The corner of her mouth did something.

"Well," she said. "Look at that."

"Coffee's there." I tilted my head at the mug by the door.

"Bless you." She crossed the kitchen and picked it up with both hands, the way old people hold coffee on a cold morning. Long sip. Eyes closed.

"Strong enough?"

"Right strength." She opened her eyes and went back to the fridge. "When did you take that."

"A few minutes ago."

"Mm." She walked over and stood in front of it. Reached up and touched the corner, gentle, the way she'd touched my ear the night before.

"You look like somebody who'd taken a picture of herself in a kitchen," she said.

"I was."

"You should do that more often." She turned, mug in hand. "I'll have to find a frame for it."

I didn't know what to say to that.

She went to the counter where the camera was. Set her mug down. Lifted the Polaroid to move it aside, and the little stack underneath came up against her hand and fanned out across the counter.

She made a small sound. Not surprised. Pleased.

I plated the eggs and buttered the toast and didn't look.

I heard her settle into a chair behind me. The soft sound of photographs being shuffled, examined, set down one at a time. A small laugh somewhere in there. The kind you make to yourself.

"Sweetheart."

"Yeah."

"This one is a crime."

"Which one."

"The teeth one. The big one. You look like the cover of a heavy metal album."

"That's the kind I figured."

"It's wonderful."

I brought both plates over and set hers down in front of her. She was still holding the bared-teeth picture between her thumb and forefinger like a tarot card. First one she picked up. Last one she put down.

"Eat first," I said.

She set it down carefully, face up. "Bossy."

---

We ate.

She asked things between bites. Did I cook often. Where had I learned. Did the eggs come out the way I liked them. Small questions. The kind you ask to keep somebody talking.

I answered. Not many words to most of it. Enough.

Then, not looking up from her plate: "You made a whole pot and you're not drinking any of it."

"It doesn't sit right."

"Mm." She let that be the end of it, which I hadn't expected. There was no follow-up. No *is that a wolf thing.* She ate another forkful and I watched her decide not to ask, and I understood that she'd file it and it would simply be true from now on - that in that house, going forward, there would be chamomile, and nobody would ever say why.

After a while she set her fork down, picked her mug up, and said, "What happened to you after that place came down."

I set my fork down too. "Someone from the people I work for now found me. They had paperwork on me. They offered me a job."

"Just like that."

"Just like that. I'm not really supposed to talk about who or how."

"Mm. I won't ask."

I took a sip. "He brought me a sandwich before he asked me anything else. That part stuck."

She smiled faintly. "Mm. They trained you, after."

"For a while, yeah."

"What was the hard part."

"Learning how to look human again. Don't flick your ears at every loud noise. Smile without showing your teeth. Don't sniff things." I shook my head a little. "Don't sniff things was the one that kept catching me out. I'd lean toward something without realizing."

"Did you ever get past it."

"Eventually."

She laughed once. Quietly.
