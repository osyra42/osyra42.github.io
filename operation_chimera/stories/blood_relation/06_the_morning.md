# The Morning

Osyra woke up before the sun.

The house had that early-morning quiet to it - the kind where you can hear the floor settling and the refrigerator cycling on and off down the hall and nothing else. Her ears caught the click of pipes somewhere on the far side of a wall. She lay there for a minute. Tail flat against the small of her back. Ears flat on the pillow. She'd slept hard. She couldn't remember the last time she'd slept hard.

She got up the way she moved on jobs. Quiet.

---

The hall floor was cold. The kitchen was darker than the bedroom had been - heavy curtains, a north-facing window. She felt her way to the lamp on the counter and clicked it on low.

The Polaroid was where Rosalyn had left it the day before. On the counter, next to a ceramic jar of pens.

Osyra looked at it for a while.

Rosalyn had offered. Osyra had put her off. She'd told herself her grandmother meant a picture of the version of her she'd been allowed to meet - the hat, the dress, the careful unsmiling mouth - and she hadn't wanted to give her that.

That wasn't the version Rosalyn had meant. Osyra knew that now.

She picked the camera up. Tested the weight. Checked the cartridge - half a pack of film left. Pulled a chair out from the table and sat under the lamp.

---

The first one was for the record.

She sat straight. Held the camera at arm's length, angled down, framed for her head and shoulders. Ears up. Eyes open. The kind of picture you'd take if you were trying to document something. She pressed the shutter. The camera whirred and spat the film out into her hand.

She set it face-down on the table to develop.

The second was a profile. She turned her head left and held the camera further out. Caught the long shape of one ear from the side, the line of her jaw, the edge of a canine where her mouth wasn't quite closed.

The third - she bit down lightly on the side of one finger and lifted her lip on the other side of her mouth. Showed the tooth clean. Like a horse trader, almost. The kind of picture you'd send to a doctor who'd asked.

Three records. Three small pieces of evidence: this is what I am. This is what she'd accepted last night.

Osyra set them all face-down to develop and let the camera rest in her lap for a moment.

Then, without really planning to, she lifted it again.

---

The fourth one she almost laughed at while she was taking it.

She put two fingers up beside her face. A peace sign. The kind of thing kids did into phones now. She held the camera up high - overhead, the way the teenagers held them at bus stops - and tilted her head a little. Ears asymmetric. One up, one back.

Shutter. Whir.

The fifth she blew a kiss at the lens. Pursed mouth. Eyes half-closed. She felt absurd. She kept going.

The sixth was her trying to bare every tooth at once. Full canine display. The kind of smile she'd never given anyone, ever. It came out looking less like a snarl and more like an animal who'd just been told a very good joke.

She let herself look at that one for a long second after it developed.

---

She spread them out across the table. Six total. Three for the record, three for no reason except she'd wanted to take them.

The peace-sign one was the best. She knew it immediately. Ears uneven. Eyes slightly bright. The half-smile that had nothing serious behind it. It looked like a person, not an exhibit.

She got up and stuck it to the fridge under a magnet shaped like a pear.

The other five she shuffled into a small stack and slid under the body of the Polaroid camera on the counter, face-down. Rosalyn would find them when she went to use it next. Osyra could imagine her grandmother sitting at this same table, working through them one at a time. She could imagine which one Rosalyn would pick up first and which one she'd put aside last.

She left the camera where it had been. Reset, like nothing had happened.

---

The coffee maker was the kind with a glass carafe and a metal basket. The bag in the freezer was a dark roast she didn't recognize. She scooped a generous amount and started a pot.

There were eggs in the fridge. A loaf of bread on the counter, half wrapped. Butter left out from the night before. A cast iron pan hanging on a peg over the stove.

She knew how to do this. She hadn't done it in a long time, but she knew how. Cracked four eggs into a bowl. Scrambled them with the back of a fork. Cut four slices of bread and set them aside. Lit the burner. Pan on. A knob of butter went in and started to foam.

It had been six, maybe seven years since she'd made breakfast for someone other than herself. Korval didn't count - Korval ate when he was told to and not when he wasn't. There'd been a colleague who came by the safehouse on Saturdays sometimes, and once or twice she'd made him a bacon and egg on toast sandwich. Not really the same.

This was the same.

The coffee finished. She poured two mugs and set one on the far end of the counter, near the doorway from the hall. The kitchen filled with the smell of butter and eggs and dark roast.

She figured that was what would do it. The coffee, at least.

Somewhere down the hall, she heard floorboards.

---

Osyra lowered the heat on the pan.

Rosalyn came into the kitchen in a long flannel robe, slippers on, hair tied back loose at the nape of her neck. She blinked at the lamp. Then at Osyra.

Then at the fridge.

Her hand came up slowly and rested on the doorframe. She looked at the photo a long second. She looked back at Osyra. The corner of her mouth did a thing.

"Well," she said. "Look at that."

"Coffee's there." Osyra tilted her head at the second mug.

"Bless you." Rosalyn crossed the kitchen and picked it up with both hands, the way old people hold coffee on a cold morning. Took a long sip. Closed her eyes.

"Strong enough?"

"Right strength." She opened her eyes again and went back to looking at the fridge. "When did you take that."

"A few minutes ago."

"Mm." She walked over to it. Stood there in front of the photo. Reached up and touched the corner gently, the way she'd touched Osyra's ear last night.

"You look like somebody who'd taken a picture of herself in a kitchen," she said.

"I was."

"You should do that more often." She turned, mug in hand. "I'll have to find a frame for it."

Osyra didn't know what to say to that.

Rosalyn went over to the counter where the camera was. Set her mug down. Lifted the Polaroid to move it aside. The little stack underneath came up against her hand and fanned out across the counter.

She made a small sound. Not surprised. Pleased.

Osyra plated the eggs and buttered the toast and didn't look.

She heard Rosalyn settle into a chair behind her. The soft sound of photographs being shuffled, examined, set down one at a time. A small laugh somewhere in there. The kind you make to yourself.

"Sweetheart."

"Yeah."

"This one is a crime."

"Which one."

"The teeth one. The big one. You look like the cover of a heavy metal album."

"That's the kind I figured."

"It's wonderful."

Osyra brought both plates over and set Rosalyn's down in front of her. Her grandmother was still holding the bared-teeth picture between her thumb and forefinger like a tarot card.

"Eat first," Osyra said.

Rosalyn set it down carefully, face up. "Bossy."

---

They ate.

Rosalyn asked things between bites. Did Osyra cook often. Where had she learned. Did the eggs come out the way she liked them. What about coffee - did she take it black at home or did she let herself add things. Small questions. The kind you ask someone to keep them talking.

Osyra answered. Not many words to most of it. Enough.

After a while Rosalyn set her fork down, picked her mug up, and said, "What happened to you after that place came down."

Osyra set her fork down too. "Someone from the people I work for now found me. They had paperwork on me. They offered me a job."

"Just like that."

"Just like that. I'm not really supposed to talk about who or how."

"Mm. I won't ask."

Osyra took a sip of the coffee. "He brought me a sandwich before he asked me anything else. That part stuck."

Rosalyn smiled faintly. "Mm. They trained you, after."

"For a while, yeah."

"What was the hard part."

"Learning how to look human again. Don't flick your ears at every loud noise. Smile without showing your teeth. Don't sniff things." Osyra shook her head a little. "Don't sniff things was the one that kept catching me out. I'd lean toward something without realizing."

"Did you ever get past it."

"Eventually."

Rosalyn laughed once. Quietly.
