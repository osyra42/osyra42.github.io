# Chapter 4 - The Reveal

The back room was small. Wood paneling, a single bed made up with a hand-stitched quilt, a low dresser, a window looking out into the dark of the yard. A second door on the right opened onto a bathroom - I could see a strip of tile through the gap.

"Towels are under the sink," she said. "Hot water takes a minute. Give it a minute."

"Thank you."

"There's a nightshirt in the top drawer if it fits. Was mine, a long time ago. You're a fair bit taller than I ever was, but it might do." She paused at the doorway. "I'll be in the kitchen. Tea if you want it. Otherwise - sleep well, sweetheart."

She closed the door behind her.

I stood in the middle of the room and listened. Her footsteps moved off down the hall. A cabinet opened. A faucet ran. Then the slow shuffling sound of a woman who's walked the same floorboards long enough to do it without looking.

I let out a breath I hadn't known I was holding.

---

I undressed in pieces.

The hat first. I set it on the dresser the way you set down something fragile. My ears came up and gave a slow twitch - they'd been flat for hours and the muscle at the base of them was aching. I rubbed at it with both hands. A small, embarrassing relief.

Then the dress. The hem caught on my tail where it always catches. I shook it loose and folded it on the chair, and my tail uncurled from against my leg where I'd kept it tucked all day, and the circulation came back in that familiar prickle.

I sat on the edge of the bed and worked the contacts out, one at a time, blinking them into the case. The world went crisper. Reds went redder.

I looked in the dresser mirror. Really looked, for the first time all day.

Long ears. Long tail. Red eyes catching the lamplight in a way no human eye does.

I looked like myself.

There's a version of this where I say I hated how much I'd missed it, and that would be tidy, and it isn't accurate. What I hated was the arithmetic. Twelve hours in the costume and I come out of it feeling like a person - which means the thirteen years I've spent calling the costume *practical* were thirteen years of paying a bill I told myself I wasn't paying. I'd rather have not known the number.

---

The shower helped.

I stood under it longer than I needed to. Hot water on the back of my neck, down the muscles along my spine, on the place between my shoulder blades that always carries something I can't name. The fur on my ears and tail flattened out and went darker. I washed my hair twice. No reason. It felt good.

When I stepped out I wrapped one of her towels around me. Too small for my frame, but it covered what mattered. I rubbed my hair down with a second one.

And then I did the thing I only do when nobody can see me.

I shook. Head, ears, the whole frame of me - one hard sharp shake, the way a dog does coming out of a lake. Water went everywhere. The mirror got it. The wall got it. A spray hit the ceiling and came back down in slow fat drops.

I didn't have to do that. That's the part. I could have stood perfectly still; there is not one twitch in my body I can't override, and I have spent my life proving it in rooms where the proving mattered. The shake isn't a thing I can't help. It's a thing I pick. Every time. Because it feels good every time, and in thirteen years of running the numbers on what I let myself have, this is what survived the cut - eleven seconds of being an animal on a bathroom floor with the door shut.

I shook again. Smaller. Just to feel the ears whip.

The floor was a wet mess. I did not care.

The nightshirt was in the top drawer like she'd said. Soft cotton, faded blue, embroidered along the collar in a pattern I didn't recognize. On her it had probably come past the knees. On me it landed a few inches above. The sleeves were short. I pulled it on and worked the fabric over my tail and let it hang free behind me.

It would do. I wasn't planning on standing up.

---

I opened the bathroom door and stepped out into the bedroom.

She was standing in the doorway to the hall with a folded blanket in her hands.

For a long second neither of us moved.

I'd left the bedroom door unlocked. That was mine. I'd assumed she would knock, and I want to be clear that assuming is not a thing I do - I had gotten comfortable, in a house, in one afternoon, at the age of twenty-nine, and this is what it cost.

Her eyes went to my ears first. Then the tail. Then back up to my face, to the eyes I hadn't put the contacts back in for.

The blanket was still folded in her hands.

I stood there in the borrowed nightshirt and tried to make myself smaller. Tail still. Ears down. Hands at my sides. Tried to take up less of the room than I do.

It didn't work. There's no version of standing in a doorway under a lamp that isn't intimidating when you're built like this. The ears. The tail. The eyes. The edge of a canine wherever my mouth isn't all the way shut. Holding still only gave her longer to land on each piece in turn, which I know, and I did it anyway, because the alternative was moving and moving is worse.

I watched her see it. Her hand tightened on the blanket. Her chin came up an inch. That stillness a person goes into when their eyes are still catching up to what's in front of them.

Here it was. This part I know.

Not from her - from receptionists, landlords, a doctor or two. The face going polite like a curtain coming down. The excuse to step into another room. The phone call to my driver ten minutes later: *something's come up, I'm so sorry.* The goodbye that means *please don't come back*, dressed up nice. I know every beat of it. I've gotten good at making the leaving easy on the person doing it, which is a skill I acquired on purpose and have not been thanked for.

I just hadn't expected to need the practice on my grandmother.

Then her shoulders came down. The flicker passed. She let out a breath, and when she looked at me again she wasn't looking at a predator. She wasn't even looking at a stranger.

She was looking at me the way you look at a kid who's come in from the yard with something scraped up that wants seeing to before bed.

"Oh, sweetheart," she said. Very softly. "Come into the light. Let me look at you properly."

I didn't move.

The catalogue had an answer ready, and the answer was that this was a different kind of goodbye - the careful one, where she looks me over thoroughly so that later she can tell herself she tried. I've been told to leave kindly before. The kindness doesn't stop the leaving; it just makes it take longer and hurt in a different place. So I watched her hands. That's where it shows first. The hands find something to do that happens to be in another room.

Her hands didn't do that. She was moving toward me.

And the whole frame I had built for what was happening slid sideways and stopped fitting. I'd been furious - viciously, in the half-second after the door opened, at her for not knocking and at myself for the lock and at the entire arrangement of my life. Most of that drained out. Not all. I didn't trust the rest of it yet. I've had this feeling before. It usually breaks.

She didn't come in the way you'd come into a barn with a wary horse in it. She just came in. Set the blanket on the bed and held a hand out to me, palm up, the way you'd ask a child to come closer so you could check something.

I went over. My body did it before my head caught up, and I'd like to know what that says, because I've never done that in my life.

"Before I do this," she said. Quiet. "You should know what you're letting close to you. I'm a doctor, well, a Pediatrician now. General practice before that. I want to look at the modifications. May I?"

Something in my chest went loose a half-turn.

The look on her face had a name now. That was all it was. I'd read clinical worry as revulsion because I have never once in thirteen years had cause to build a category for a person who looks at me that way and wants to *help*. Not a stranger reaching. Not morbid curiosity. A doctor finding where it hurts.

I nodded.

She brought her other hand up - slow, asking with her face. I tilted my head down so she could reach. She touched the outside of one ear. Gentle. The touch you'd use checking the edges of a scab.

"Does this hurt?"

"No."

I was still half-waiting. The hand on my ear, that close, is where the recoil comes. The little involuntary pull-back. The face you make when you've touched a thing and only then registered what it was.

It didn't come.

"Pressure here?" Her thumb pressed lightly at the base of the ear. "Or here?"

"No. The base used to ache after a long day in the hat. Doesn't anymore."

"Mm." She palpated the cartilage along the rim. Slow. Cataloguing. "Has it ever - when it was happening. Did you get sick? Fevers? Skin trouble where the fur came in?"

"For about a year. Itched mostly. Not after that."

"Mm-hm." Something soft under the clinical. She turned my head a fraction to see the other ear, thumb tracing just behind the base where the fur meets skin. Worried in the small specific way you're worried about a thing you might still be able to fix. "Can you move them?"

I flicked one forward. Then the other. Then both.

"Oh, that's clever."

I nearly lost it there.

*Clever.* That's the wrong word. That's the word you use when a kid shows you a small thing they're proud of. Nobody has ever applied it to my body. My body is a security problem, an operational advantage, a thing people have written *specimen* next to on a chart. It is not clever. There was no slot for it and I stood there with the word sitting on top of my head like a bird that had landed on me, waiting for her next sentence to take it back.

"And the tail?" she said.

I moved it once, slow, side to side. Then stopped it dead.

"All right." She let go of my ear and stepped back to take stock of the whole of me, the way you take stock of a kid's full report once they've finished crying.

And then her face did a thing.

Not the recoil. Something else, and I had no read on it, because I have never gotten this far with anyone. Her mouth went thin. Her eyes went bright in a way that wasn't softness.

I went very still.

"And you walked into my house," she said, "in a hat. And a dress. And those contacts that hurt your eyes."

"Yeah."

"All afternoon."

"Yeah."

"Sitting in my chair and drinking my tea and not smiling at me because you didn't want me to see your teeth."

"Yes."

*There it is.* Different shape, same animal. Not disgust. Disappointment. I'd taken her hospitality under false pretenses and let her haul out a photo album for somebody who wasn't in the room. That's a fair thing to be angry about. I'd have been angry about it. I started getting my body ready to hand back the half-step she'd let me have, without making it her problem, because that is the one courtesy I can still offer at the end of these.

Then she spoke again, and I understood that I had the vector wrong.

The bright thing in her eyes wasn't pointed at me. It was pointed past my shoulder. At the world I'd had to cross to get to her front door. At the version of me she'd had to meet first.

"Sweetheart." The edge was still on it. "You don't ever do that in this house. Do you understand me? Not in this house. I don't care what you look like under there. I'd have wanted to know you straight off."

I didn't answer right away. I was checking the sentence for a hook. I went over it twice. That's not a figure of speech either - I have been handed sentences like that before and they always have something in them, some small clause that comes due later, and I have learned to find it before I accept delivery.

There wasn't one.

"All right," I said. Small.

"Sit down, honey. Tell me what happened to you. Slow if you need to."

I sat.

The bed creaked. My tail came around across my lap and I put a hand on it without thinking, which is a tell, and I know it's a tell, and I did it anyway.

"It was a lab," I said. "When I was sixteen."

She nodded once. Waited.

I waited too. I wanted to give her more. My body wouldn't do it. Every version of this conversation I've ever rehearsed ends with a door closing politely behind me, and the rehearsals are load-bearing; I could not get out from under them just because the room was different. I have told the long version to nobody who didn't already have the file open in front of them.

"That's all I can do tonight," I said. Small.

"Then that's all you need to do tonight."

She meant it. I waited for the look that says otherwise - the kindness with a clock on it. It didn't come. Her hand stayed loose in her lap. Her eyes stayed where they were.

"I'd have loved you exactly like this," Rosalyn said. "At sixteen. At six. Whatever came off that table. I want that on the record, since nobody ever gave me the chance to say it out loud."

I had to look at the floor for a minute.
