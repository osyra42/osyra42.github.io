# The Reveal

The back room was small. Wood paneling on the walls, a single bed made up with a hand-stitched quilt, a low dresser, a window that looked out into the dark of the yard. A second door on the right opened onto a bathroom — Osyra could see a strip of tile through the gap.

"Towels are under the sink," Rosalyn said. "Hot water takes a minute. Give it a minute."

"Thank you."

"There's a nightshirt in the top drawer if it fits. Was mine, a long time ago. You're a fair bit taller than I ever was, but it might do." She paused at the doorway. "I'll be in the kitchen. Tea if you want it. Otherwise — sleep well, sweetheart."

She closed the door behind her.

Osyra stood in the middle of the room and listened. Rosalyn's footsteps moved off down the hall. A cabinet opened. A faucet ran. Then the slow shuffling sound of a woman who'd walked the same floorboards long enough to do it without looking.

Osyra let out a breath she hadn't realized she was holding.

---

She undressed in pieces.

The hat first. She set it on the dresser the way you'd set something fragile. Her ears straightened and gave a slow twitch — they'd been pressed flat for hours and the muscles ached. She rubbed at the base of them with both hands. A small, embarrassing relief.

Then the dress. The hem caught briefly on her tail, the way it always did. She shook it loose and folded it on the chair. Her tail uncurled from where she'd kept it tucked tight against her leg, and she felt that familiar prickle of pins and needles as the circulation came back.

She sat on the edge of the bed and worked the contacts out, one at a time, blinking them into the little case. The world went a little crisper. Reds went a little redder. She stared at herself in the dresser mirror — really stared, for the first time all day.

Long ears. Long tail. Red eyes that caught the lamplight in a way no human eye ever did.

She looked like herself.

She hated how much she'd missed it.

---

The shower helped.

She stood under it longer than she needed to. Hot water on the back of her neck, on the muscles along her spine, on the place between her shoulder blades that always carried tension she couldn't quite name. The fur on her ears and tail flattened out and turned a darker black. She washed her hair twice. She didn't have a reason. It just felt good.

When she stepped out she wrapped one of Rosalyn's towels around herself. It was too small for her frame, but it covered what mattered. She rubbed her hair down with a second towel, and then she did the thing she only did when no one could see her.

She shook. Head, ears, the whole frame of her — one hard sharp shake the way a dog does coming out of a lake. Water flew everywhere. The mirror got it. The wall got it. A spray hit the ceiling and beaded back down in slow fat drops.

She didn't have to do that. She could have held perfectly still — every twitch she made was a choice. That was what made it embarrassing. The shake wasn't something she couldn't help. It was something she picked, every single time, because it felt good every single time.

She shook again. Smaller. Just to feel the ears whip. The floor was a wet mess. She did not care.

The nightshirt was in the top drawer like Rosalyn had said. Soft cotton, faded blue, embroidered along the collar in a pattern Osyra didn't recognize. On Rosalyn it had probably come down past her knees. On Osyra it landed a few inches above. The sleeves were short. She pulled it on. The fabric caught on her tail and she worked it through and let the tail hang free behind her.

It would have to do. She wasn't planning on standing up.

---

She opened the bathroom door and stepped out into the bedroom.

Rosalyn was standing in the doorway to the hall with a folded blanket in her hands.

For a long second neither of them moved.

Osyra had left the bedroom door unlocked. That was her mistake. She'd assumed Rosalyn would knock. She'd assumed a lot of things, apparently.

Rosalyn's eyes went to her ears first. Then her tail. Then back up to her face — to the eyes Osyra hadn't put the contacts back in for.

The blanket was still folded in Rosalyn's hands.

Osyra stood there in the borrowed nightshirt and tried to make herself smaller. Tail still. Ears down. Hands at her sides. Tried to take up less of the room than she actually did.

It didn't work. There was no version of standing in a doorway under a lamp that didn't look intimidating. The ears. The tail. The eyes. The edge of a canine wherever her mouth wasn't fully closed. Holding still just gave Rosalyn's eyes longer to settle on each piece.

Osyra saw her see it. Rosalyn's hand tightened on the blanket. Her chin came up an inch. The kind of stillness a person goes into when their eyes are still catching up to what's in front of them.

Here it was. The part Osyra knew.

She'd seen this before. Not from Rosalyn — from receptionists, landlords, a doctor or two. The face going polite like a curtain coming down. The excuse to step into another room. The phone call to her chauffeur ten minutes later: something's come up, I'm so sorry. The goodbye that was really, "please don't come back", dressed up nice. She knew every beat. She'd gotten good at making the leaving easy on the person doing it.

She just hadn't expected to need the practice on her grandmother.

Then Rosalyn's shoulders came back down. The flicker passed. She let out a breath, and when she looked at Osyra again she wasn't looking at a predator. She wasn't even looking at a stranger.

She was looking at Osyra the way you look at a child who's come in from the yard with something scraped up that needs seeing to before bed.

"Oh, sweetheart," she said. Very softly. "Come into the light. Let me look at you properly."

Osyra didn't move at first. The catalogue in her head said this was a different version of goodbye. The one where Rosalyn would look her over carefully so she could tell herself later that she had tried. Osyra had been told kindly to leave before. The kindness didn't make the leaving stop. She kept watching Rosalyn's hands for the moment they'd find something to do that took her out of the room.

But Rosalyn wasn't moving toward the door. She was moving forward.

The whole frame of Osyra's panic shifted sideways and didn't quite fit anymore. The frustration of being seen — and she had been frustrated, viciously, in the half-second after the door opened — drained partway out of her. Not all the way. She didn't trust the rest of it yet. She'd had this feeling before. It usually broke.

Rosalyn didn't step into the room like you'd step into a barn with a wary horse. She just stepped in. She set the blanket down on the bed and held out a hand toward Osyra, palm up, the way you'd ask a child to come closer so you could check something.

Osyra went over. She didn't know why. Her body did it before her head caught up.

"Before I do this," Rosalyn said. Quiet. "You should know what you're letting close to you. I'm a doctor, well, a Pediatrician now. General practice before that. I want to look at the modifications. May I?"

Something in Osyra's chest loosened a half-turn. The clinical worry on Rosalyn's face had a name now. Not a stranger reaching. Not morbid curiosity. The thing a doctor does when something hurts and she's trying to find where.

Osyra nodded.

Rosalyn brought her other hand up — slow, asking with her face. Osyra tilted her head down so Rosalyn could reach. Rosalyn touched the outside of one of her ears. Gentle. The kind of touch you'd use checking the edges of a scab.

"Does this hurt?"

"No." Osyra was still half-waiting for something — the hand on her ear, this close, was where the recoil usually came in. The little involuntary pull-back. The face you make when you've touched something and only just registered what it was.

It didn't come.

"Pressure here?" Rosalyn's thumb pressed lightly at the base of the ear. "Or here?"

"No. The base used to ache after a long day in the hat. Doesn't anymore."

"Mm." She palpated the cartilage along the rim. Slow. Cataloguing. "Has it ever — when it was happening. Did you get sick? Fevers? Skin trouble where the fur came in?"

"For about a year. Itched mostly. Not after that."

"Mm-hm." Something soft in her voice. Cataloguing and approving at the same time. She turned Osyra's head a fraction to look at the other ear. Her thumb traced just behind the base of it, where the fur met skin. Clinical. Worried in the small specific way you're worried about a thing you might still be able to fix. "Can you move them?"

Osyra flicked one ear forward. Then the other. Then both at once.

"Oh, that's clever."

She nearly lost her composure on the word. Clever. It was the wrong word. It was the word you used when a kid showed you a small thing they were proud of. It wasn't anywhere in the catalogue. She didn't have a slot for it. She kept waiting for the next thing Rosalyn said to take it back.

"And the tail?" Rosalyn said.

Osyra moved it once, slow, side to side. Then stopped it dead.

"All right." Rosalyn let go of her ear and stepped back half a step to take stock of the whole of her, the way you take stock of a kid's full report after they've come in crying.

And then Rosalyn's face did a thing.

Not the recoil. Something else. Something Osyra had been less prepared for, because she'd never gotten this far with anyone. Rosalyn's mouth went thin. Her eyes went a little bright in a way that wasn't softness.

Osyra went very still. Whatever this was, the catalogue didn't have it either.

"And you walked into my house," Rosalyn said, "in a hat. And a dress. And those contacts that hurt your eyes."

"Yeah."

"All afternoon."

"Yeah."

"Sitting in my chair and drinking my tea and not smiling at me because you didn't want me to see your teeth."

"Yes."

There it is, Osyra thought. Different shape, same animal. Not disgust — disappointment. She'd taken Rosalyn's hospitality under false pretenses. She'd let her bring out a photo album and tell stories to a person who hadn't really been in the room. The half-step Rosalyn had let her close was about to slip, and Osyra started getting her body ready to give it back to her without making it her problem.

Then Rosalyn spoke again, and Osyra realized the bright thing in her eyes wasn't pointed at her. It was pointed at the air just past Osyra's shoulder. At the world Osyra had had to walk through to get to her front door. At the version of Osyra she'd had to meet first instead of this one.

"Sweetheart." There was still an edge on it. "You don't ever do that in this house. Do you understand me? Not in this house. I don't care what you look like under there. I'd have wanted to know you straight off."

Osyra didn't answer right away. She was still working out whether she'd heard right. She kept turning the sentence over to see if there was a hook in it she'd missed.

There wasn't.

"All right," she said. Small.

"Sit down, honey. Tell me what happened to you. Slow if you need to."

Osyra sat.

The bed creaked under her. Her tail came to rest across her lap and she put one hand on it without thinking. A nervous tell.

"It was a lab," she said. "When I was sixteen."

Rosalyn nodded once. Waited.

Osyra waited too. She wanted to give more. Her body wouldn't let her. The catalogue still had a hand on the back of her neck — every version of this conversation she'd rehearsed had ended with a door closing politely behind her. She had told the long version to no one who didn't already have the file open.

"That's all I can do tonight," she said. Small.

"Then that's all you need to do tonight."

Rosalyn meant it. Osyra waited for the look that said otherwise — the kindness with a clock on it. It didn't come. Her grandmother's hand stayed loose in her lap. Her eyes stayed where they were.

"Your aunt would have loved you exactly like this," Rosalyn said.

Osyra had to look at the floor for a minute.
