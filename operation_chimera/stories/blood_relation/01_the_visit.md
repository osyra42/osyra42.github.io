# The Visit

I was nervous. There were so many things that could go wrong.

I thought this while looking out the window as my chauffeur drove me to my destination. Up front, behind the wheel, was Korval—a tough, Russian ex-military guy. He was primarily my chauffeur and bodyguard. He always followed me everywhere. Not that I needed it. I was usually the dangerous one.

I had recently gotten word that the agency found one of my biological family members and gave me the opportunity to visit. I took it. My grandmother: Rosalyn Renaya. Despite my protests—I didn't need a bodyguard just to blend in as family—my boss insisted and would not back down.

"How much further until we get there?" I wondered out loud.

"About thirty minutes," Korval answered in his thick Russian accent. Then he added, "Why are you so impatient? You're always calm."

A strange sensation washed over me. I'd never wanted to be close to family before. Maybe this was a new experience. I had nothing to say back. No reply. I stared out the window as the metropolis turned into rural neighborhoods.

"Okay, we're just about there," Korval said. "Make sure you dress appropriately."

For a moment, I'd nearly forgotten that my grandmother didn't know about my altered form. I climbed into the back seat and changed my jeans for a dress. Put a hat on, added contacts. I felt like a completely different person. I couldn't tell which version of me was real. The one where I'm Chimeran? Or the one where I was human?

---

I was Chimera now. I wasn't always Chimera. This came from a lab. I was given up for adoption as an infant. My aunt took me in and raised me until she passed. After that I went to foster care — they're the ones who sold me to the medical facility. I was sixteen. They experimented on me. They experimented on many other kids. Most of them didn't make it. I did.

I became a Chimera—part wolf, part human. You couldn't classify me as human or animal. While they were running their tests, there was a breach. Someone blew up part of the building. The facilities shut down, and I got free. They wrote it off as a total loss.

But then came the problem: how to get work? How to get money when I looked like this? I was discovered by someone sifting through the paperwork in the building. They recognized me, found me, and offered me a job. Provide my capabilities—which had proven adequate. They wanted me to hunt down and fix things. Deal with anomalous entities and situations. The pay was good. I got to be the secret they didn't point and laugh at.

I had wanted to go outside occasionally, so I learned how to conceal my form:

- Color contacts to hide my red eyes
- Big hats to hide my ears
- Long dresses to hide my tail

Didn't need to smile much. You couldn't see my canines anyway.

People I came into contact with just thought I was angry. That was good enough. They left me alone.

After a while lost in thought, I was suddenly snapped back to reality. Korval said we had about fifteen minutes until we arrived. This was my sign to get ready—prepare any way I saw fit. Double-check my dress was set just right. Hat hiding my ears. No smiling. Standard procedure.

It was a relatively warm day. The sun was shining, and I looked like a bronze beauty.

---

The house was a bit covered in ivy and just looked a little overgrown. A small stone path led to the front door. When we parked, an old lady walked out.

"Osyra. You're Rosalyn, right? My grandmother?"

"Ah yes, I got a phone call saying that you would be around soon."

"That must have been my boss."

"Lieutenant something. I can't remember his name."

"He worries too much."

"Well, that means he cares about you, honey. We can talk about it inside. Does your driver want to come in too?"

I side-eyed Korval. "No. He prefers the quiet."

Korval said nothing. He just nodded slightly, then walked to the front of the car and sat on the hood. His sunglasses reflecting.

"He'll head to a hotel when it gets dark. Back in the morning," I explained.

"All right then, sweetheart." She turned around and walked inside.

I looked back to Korval for just a second, nodded my head, and walked inside following her.

Once inside, I chose a comfortable seat close to the door. I didn't want to wander too far into the home without an invitation. The seat was almost too comfortable. The age had made it relax into the exact shape of a body. I looked around the room. The light was not blinding. It was also not dull. It was relaxing. The wood trims on the walls, the furniture, everything felt like it was a snapshot back in time. The smell in there was of newspaper and sunshine.

Somehow I felt like this was a memory in the back of my mind. Something I missed. Something I needed. For today, the plan was to sleep here tonight and go on the next mission tomorrow. Korval and I had agreed to meet up in the morning, and he would transport me to the next location.

"What do you do for work, dear?"

"Can't say much. It's military."

"Oh, I understand. I don't want to get you in any trouble," Rosalyn said softly.

"Combat unit. Mostly eliminating dangerous animals."

"What a world we live in. They come up with new jobs every day, don't they?" She smiled. "As long as they're paying you well and treating you right."

"They are," I said.

"How about some pictures? So I can remember you once this day's over." Rosalyn had been wandering around this whole time. She finally reached onto a counter and picked up a Polaroid.

I felt my face go red. I didn't take pictures. This was completely embarrassing. I couldn't deny her. That'd be cruel. But to accept was just so damn awkward. I'd have to consider this. Did she take a picture of me? Should she take a picture of the person I pretended to be? "Later," I told her.

"Of course, honey. You must be tired."

Just then there was a knock on the door. "Now who could that be?" Rosalyn said, turning toward the sound.

It was Korval. What could he possibly want, I wondered. I got up and walked over.

"Captain Renaya, I have to inform you—headquarters has assigned you a new agent. They'll be taking notes on your operations for records. Commander says their name is Vanity."

I stared at him.

"You don't need to do anything on your end. They've already swapped out the agent. Contact them through normal means. Chain of command says we still have to inform you that there was a change."

"What about Corporal Bennett?" I asked.

"He's been reassigned. There's nothing we can do," Korval said.

"Bullshit. I don't want some rookie I have to break in."

His name was Bennett. It took me a couple of months to explain the way I'd like my notes. Aggression, nihilism, and all. At first I didn't even want to take notes at all. This was the compromise between me and corporate. And then he took on a few more people. I think he had four people under him. He was taking the notes for them. What a paper pusher. But honestly, I did really enjoy his company. It was gone now. Such was life.

I finally spoke again. "Do you know where they're taking him?"

"All I was told was standard transfer," Korval said. "Anyway, that's all I have to report, Captain. I'm out." He did a short bow, turned, got in the car, and left. Like there was nothing else to do in the world. Stiff, mechanical.

I shut the door and turned around to face Rosalyn, but she wasn't there. She seemed to be in the kitchen. I wandered back to the comfortable chair. Sat down. It was relaxing. It took the edge off. I couldn't have explained the military secrets if I had to. I kept these thoughts to myself.

For a moment I was alone with my thoughts. I had nothing to think about except what this new recruiter would be like. Hopefully they figured out how to do things my way.

What kind of name was Vanity, anyway. Just Vanity. No rank in front, no surname behind. That wasn't how the agency announced people — you got Corporal Bennett, you got Captain Renaya. You didn't get one word and nothing else. I noticed. I didn't ask. The agency had its quirks, and pulling on one of them wasn't my job.

She eventually brought in some tea. It was hard to pay attention to that. The comfortable chair was lulling me to sleep as my ever-energetic body was slowing down. Hopefully the tea would increase my energy again. I didn't feel it happening. The weight of the world was crushing me. I'd been running for so long, I hadn't even noticed how tired I was. I accepted the cup from her almost greedily. Anything to stay awake for this moment.

"What was that about?" she asked.

"I have a new crew member I have to train. Someone at the office. It's not really a big deal." I took a sip of the tea. It smelled wonderful. Spiced chai. One of my favorites.

She nodded slowly, the way someone does when they don't really understand but want you to know they're listening. She set her own cup down on the side table and made her way to the bookshelf at the far end of the room. Her fingers ran along the spines until they stopped on something thick and softbound. She pulled it out with both hands.

"I dug this out before you came," she said. "Thought you might want to see."

She brought it back over and lowered herself into the chair across from me. The album opened with a soft creak of old leather. Pages thick as cardboard. The kind that had little black corners stuck on to hold each photo in place. She turned to the first page and angled it toward me.

"This is your great-grandmother. My mother. She was a tough old thing."

I leaned forward enough to see. A black-and-white photograph of a woman in a high-collared dress, hair pulled back so tight it looked painful. She wasn't smiling. None of them did, in those days.

"She looks strict," I said.

"Oh, she was. But she'd give you the last bite off her plate if she thought you needed it more." Rosalyn smiled and turned the page. A few more faces. Names I didn't catch. People who had lived and died long before I'd ever drawn a breath.

I let her go. It was easier than answering. The chair was already pulling at me, and her voice had a rhythm to it, like wind moving through a screen door.

She turned another page, and the rhythm changed.

"And this," she said, more carefully now, "is your mother."

I looked.

A young woman, sitting on the steps of a porch I didn't recognize. Thin. Tired in a way that didn't belong on a face that young. She was holding something in her lap — a bundle. A baby. She wasn't looking at the camera. She was looking down at the bundle.

I tried to remember her. I tried to picture the porch, the sound of her voice, anything at all. I came back with nothing. A blank wall where a person should have been. Whoever she was, she lived in someone else's head. Not mine.

"How old was she?" I asked.

"Just barely an adult." Rosalyn touched the edge of the photograph with one finger. "She and your father — they didn't have anything, sweetheart. Not really. Not enough for a child. They tried, for a while. But there's a kind of poor that doesn't bend, and they were in it." She paused. "I would have taken you in myself if I'd known. By the time I found out, your aunt had already stepped up. And she was good for you. I told myself that."

I nodded. There wasn't anything to say to that.

I took another sip of the tea.

She turned the page slower this time. "And there she is. Your aunt."

The picture was in color. A woman in a kitchen, hair tied back with a faded bandana, laughing at something off-camera. There was flour on her hands.

I knew that kitchen. I knew the bandana. I knew the laugh — loud, sudden, a little embarrassing in public. I knew the smell of her bread, which she made every Sunday and ruined every other time she tried. I knew the way she said my name when she was tired but not angry. I knew the weight of her hand on my back the night she taught me how to hold a knife properly. I knew the morning she didn't come down for breakfast, and how the house had felt wrong before I'd even started up the stairs to check on her.

"She was kind," I said.

"Was she happy?" Rosalyn asked. Quiet. Not pushing. Just wanting to know.

I thought about that. She had been a lot of things. Tired. Stubborn. Full of stories. Scared, sometimes, of things she wouldn't talk about. But happy?

"I think so," I said. "Most of the time."

Rosalyn's eyes had gone wet, but she didn't make a thing of it. She nodded once and turned the page.

The next photo stopped me for a different reason.

A man — middle-aged, broad-shouldered — standing in a clearing somewhere wooded. A rifle propped against his shoulder. A deer at his feet, freshly taken. He was smiling, but it was a tight kind of smile, like he wasn't really comfortable with the camera.

"My husband," Rosalyn said. "He's been gone a long time now. He'd have liked you, I think. He used to do something a bit like what you do — tracked nuisance animals, mostly. Bears that got into the trash. The occasional cat that started taking livestock."

I felt something tighten in my chest. Of course he did.

"He'd ask you a thousand questions about it," she went on. "What kind of rifle, what range you work at, how often you have to take a shot at running game. He always wanted to know more than he let on."

"It's mostly close work," I said. Carefully. "Tracking. Containment. The shooting part isn't the hard part."

"That's what he used to say." Rosalyn laughed once. "Said anyone could pull a trigger. Hard part was being sure you should."

I made myself smile a little. Not enough to show teeth. "Yeah."

She watched me for a half-second longer than felt natural. Then she turned the page.

A few more photos. A house she used to live in. A garden that was no longer there. Then, finally, she closed the album in her lap and rested both hands flat on the cover.

"I've been hoping the agency would call for years," she said. She said it like she was admitting to a small thing, not a heavy one. "I knew about you, you know. Always knew there was a granddaughter out there somewhere. Just couldn't ever find the thread to pull."

I didn't know what to do with that. I sat with the cup in my lap and stared at it.

"This place gets quiet," she said. "Quieter than I thought it would. The phone doesn't ring much. My hands aren't what they were. Some mornings I open jars with the back of a butter knife." She laughed, soft. "Don't tell anyone."

"I won't."

She looked at me a moment longer, then patted the album once, gently, like saying goodnight to it.

"You look tired, honey."

I was tired.

"Why don't you take the back room. The one at the end of the hall. The bed's made up. I'd been hoping you might."

I nodded. I didn't trust my voice.

I set the cup down, stood up, and let her show me the way.
