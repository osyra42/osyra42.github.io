# The Reveal

The back room was small. Wood paneling on the walls, a single bed made up with a hand-stitched quilt, a low dresser, a window that looked out into the dark of the yard. A second door on the right opened onto a bathroom — I could see a strip of tile through the gap.

"Towels are under the sink," Rosalyn said. "Hot water takes a minute. Give it a minute."

"Thank you."

"There's a nightshirt in the top drawer if it fits. Was mine, a long time ago. You're a fair bit taller than I ever was, but it might do." She paused at the doorway. "I'll be in the kitchen. Tea if you want it. Otherwise — sleep well, sweetheart."

She closed the door behind her.

I stood in the middle of the room and listened. Her footsteps moved off down the hall. A cabinet opened. A faucet ran. Then the slow shuffling sound of a woman who'd walked the same floorboards long enough to do it without looking.

I let out a breath I hadn't realized I was holding.

---

I undressed in pieces.

The hat first. I set it on the dresser the way you'd set something fragile. My ears straightened and gave a slow twitch — they'd been pressed flat for hours and the muscles ached. I rubbed at the base of them with both hands. A small, embarrassing relief.

Then the dress. The hem caught briefly on my tail, the way it always did. I shook it loose and folded it on the chair. My tail uncurled from where I'd kept it tucked tight against my leg, and I felt that familiar prickle of pins and needles as the circulation came back.

I sat on the edge of the bed and worked the contacts out, one at a time, blinking them into the little case. The world went a little crisper. Reds went a little redder. I stared at myself in the dresser mirror — really stared, for the first time all day.

Long ears. Long tail. Red eyes that caught the lamplight in a way no human eye ever did.

I looked like myself.

I hated how much I'd missed it.

---

The shower helped.

I stood under it longer than I needed to. Hot water on the back of my neck, on the muscles along my spine, on the place between my shoulder blades that always carried tension I couldn't quite name. The fur on my ears and tail flattened out and turned a darker black. I washed my hair twice. I didn't have a reason. It just felt good.

When I stepped out I wrapped one of Rosalyn's towels around myself. It was too small for my frame, but it covered what mattered. I rubbed my hair down with a second towel, and then I did the thing I only did when no one could see me.

I shook. Head, ears, the whole frame of me — one hard sharp shake the way a dog does coming out of a lake. Water flew everywhere. The mirror got it. The wall got it. A spray hit the ceiling and beaded back down in slow fat drops.

I didn't have to do that. I could have held perfectly still — every twitch I made was a choice. That was what made it embarrassing. The shake wasn't something I couldn't help. It was something I picked, every single time, because it felt good every single time.

I shook again. Smaller. Just to feel the ears whip. The floor was a wet mess. I did not care.

The nightshirt was in the top drawer like she'd said. Soft cotton, faded blue, embroidered along the collar in a pattern I didn't recognize. On her it had probably come down past her knees. On me it landed a few inches above. The sleeves were short. I pulled it on. The fabric caught on my tail and I worked it through and let the tail hang free behind me.

It would have to do. I wasn't planning on standing up.

---

I opened the bathroom door and stepped out into the bedroom.

Rosalyn was standing in the doorway to the hall with a folded blanket in her hands.

For a long second neither of us moved.

I'd left the bedroom door unlocked. That was my mistake. I'd assumed she'd knock. I'd assumed a lot of things, apparently.

Her eyes went to my ears first. Then my tail. Then back up to my face — to the eyes I hadn't put the contacts back in for.

The blanket was still folded in her hands.

I stood there in the borrowed nightshirt and tried to make myself smaller. Tail still. Ears down. Hands at my sides. Tried to take up less of the room than I actually did.

It didn't work. There was no version of standing in a doorway under a lamp that didn't look like this. The ears. The tail. The eyes. The edge of a canine wherever my mouth wasn't fully closed. Holding still just gave her eyes longer to settle on each piece.

I saw her see it. Her hand tightened on the blanket. Her chin came up an inch. The kind of stillness a person goes into when their eyes are still catching up to what's in front of them.

Here it was. The part I knew.

I'd seen this before. Not from her — from receptionists, landlords, a doctor or two. The face going polite like a curtain coming down. The excuse to step into another room. The phone call to my chauffeur ten minutes later: something's come up, I'm so sorry. The goodbye that was really, "please don't come back", dressed up nice. I knew every beat. I'd gotten good at making the leaving easy on the person doing it.

I just hadn't expected to need the practice on her.

Then her shoulders came back down. The flicker passed. She let out a breath, and when she looked at me again she wasn't looking at a predator. She wasn't even looking at a stranger.

She was looking at me the way you look at a child who's come in from the yard with something scraped up that needs seeing to before bed.

"Oh, sweetheart," she said. Very softly. "Come into the light. Let me look at you properly."

I didn't move at first. The catalogue in my head said this was a different version of goodbye. The one where she'd look me over carefully so she could tell herself later that she had tried. I'd been told kindly to leave before. The kindness didn't make the leaving stop. I kept watching her hands for the moment they'd find something to do that took her out of the room.

But she wasn't moving toward the door. She was moving toward me.

The whole frame of my panic shifted sideways and didn't quite fit anymore. The frustration of being seen — and I had been frustrated, viciously, in the half-second after the door opened — drained partway out of me. Not all the way. I didn't trust the rest of it yet. I'd had this feeling before. It usually broke.

She didn't step into the room like you'd step into a barn with a wary horse. She just stepped in. She set the blanket down on the bed and held out a hand toward me, palm up, the way you'd ask a child to come closer so you could check something.

I went over. I don't know why. My body did it before my head caught up.

She brought her other hand up — slow, asking with her face. I tilted my head down so she could reach. She touched the outside of one of my ears. Gentle. The kind of touch you'd use checking the edges of a scab.

"Does this hurt?"

"No." I was still waiting. The hand on my ear, this close, was where the recoil usually came in. The little involuntary pull-back. The face you make when you've touched something and only just registered what it was.

It didn't come.

"Has it ever — when it was happening. Did you get sick from it? Fevers? Skin trouble where the fur came in?"

"Not after the first year."

"Mm." She turned my head a fraction to look at the other ear. Her thumb traced just behind the base of it, where the fur met skin. Clinical. Worried in the small specific way you're worried about a thing you might still be able to fix. "Can you move them?"

I flicked one ear forward. Then the other. Then both at once.

"Oh, that's clever."

I nearly lost my composure on the word. Clever. It was the wrong word. It was the word you used when a kid showed you a small thing they were proud of. It wasn't anywhere in the catalogue. I didn't have a slot for it. I kept waiting for the next thing she said to take it back.

"And the tail?" she said.

I moved it once, slow, side to side. Then stopped it dead.

"All right." She let go of my ear and stepped back half a step to take stock of the whole of me, the way you take stock of a kid's full report after they've come in crying.

And then her face did a thing.

Not the recoil. Something else. Something I'd been less prepared for, because I'd never gotten this far with anyone. Her mouth went thin. Her eyes went a little bright in a way that wasn't softness.

I went very still. Whatever this was, the catalogue didn't have it either.

"And you walked into my house," she said, "in a hat. And a dress. And those contacts that hurt your eyes."

"Yeah."

"All afternoon."

"Yeah."

"Sitting in my chair and drinking my tea and not smiling at me because you didn't want me to see your teeth."

"Yes."

There it is, I thought. Different shape, same animal. Not disgust — disappointment. I'd taken her hospitality under false pretenses. I'd let her bring out a photo album and tell stories to a person who hadn't really been in the room. The half-step she'd let me close was about to slip, and I started getting my body ready to give it back to her without making it her problem.

Then she spoke again, and I realized the bright thing in her eyes wasn't pointed at me. It was pointed at the air just past my shoulder. At the world I'd had to walk through to get to her front door. At the version of me she'd had to meet first instead of this one.

"Sweetheart." There was still an edge on it. "You don't ever do that in this house. Do you understand me? Not in this house. I don't care what you look like under there. I'd have wanted to know you straight off."

I didn't answer right away. I was still working out whether I'd heard her right. I kept turning the sentence over to see if there was a hook in it I'd missed.

There wasn't.

"All right," I said. Small.

"Sit down, honey. Tell me what happened to you. Slow if you need to."

I sat.

The bed creaked under me. My tail came to rest across my lap and I put one hand on it without thinking. A nervous tell.

"It was the lab," I said. "When I was sixteen."

She nodded once.

"After Auntie passed, the state put me in foster care. The people they placed me with sold me to the lab. That's how I got there."

She didn't nod this time. She went very still.

"They were experimenting on people. Kids, mostly. Most of them didn't survive. I did."

"What did they do to you." It came out rough. A voice I hadn't heard from her all day.

I took a breath. Lined up the part she could hear.

"It was a virus. They had a whole shelf of versions of it — numbered. The one they gave me was called Chimera-9. That's the one that makes you part wolf. The other kids got other numbers."

I rubbed at my eye with the back of my wrist.

"Mine took. Most of them didn't. The ones that did sometimes came out worse than I did. Things that stopped looking like people on the way through. I don't know what was done with those ones after."

She didn't say anything.

"After the place came down, the agency found me. Put me to work. The dangerous-animals part is half the truth. The other half is — rich people lose things they'd rather nobody else find. A phone in a forest. A ring that went over the side of a boat. Strange jobs, weird hours, more money than the work is worth. The kind of money people pay you to forget about the job once it's done. The rest of it I can't really talk about."

She was quiet a long moment. She was looking at me the same way she'd looked at me when I came out of the bathroom — like I was somebody who'd come in from the yard with something scraped up. Except this time the scrapes were under the skin, and she could see them, and she wasn't going to mention it.

"The biggest hurdle is I can't go outside without a disguise," I said. "People see me and they get scared. Or they get violent. Or they get scared and call somebody who'll get violent. The hat and the dress and the contacts — that's how I buy groceries. That's how I get on a bus. That's how I knock on a front door without somebody phoning it in."

"Mm." She didn't disagree.

"The teeth are the worst part. The rest you can hide if you're careful. Can't hide a smile."

"May I see, sweetheart? You don't have to."

I opened my mouth a little. Canines longer than they had any business being, sharp where they ought to have been rounded.

She looked. She didn't look away.

"Your aunt would have loved you exactly like this," she said.

I had to look at the floor for a minute.
