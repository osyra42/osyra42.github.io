# Chapter 5 - The Letting Go

She didn't leave right away.

She sat down in the little chair by the dresser instead, and it took me a minute to work out that it was a decision. If she'd stood up and gone, I'd have started crying. She knew that before I did. I read rooms for a living and I had missed it in my own.

She asked me about the small things.

Did the ears hurt under the hat. Did my tail get cold in winter. Could I eat normal food.

I answered most of them. The contacts were the worst - twelve hours and they felt like sand.

"Then take them out, honey. In this house. Always. And the hat."

I nodded.

Her mouth went a little quirky on one side. "Now. I'll only say this once. The state of that bathroom floor isn't from the shower, is it."

My face went hot all at once.

"No."

"Mm-hm."

"I was drying off."

"You were drying off?"

"In a particular way."

"Sweetheart, I lived with a man who used to come home from the woods wearing half the mud. Just maybe put a towel down next time."

I made a noise into the back of my hand - half a laugh, half a wince. The first uncalculated sound out of me all day.

"There it is," she said, pleased with herself.

The laugh had done something. It took me a while to work out what.

She had made a joke about my body - the water, the mess, the animal fact of me - and the joke wasn't about whether I should exist. Every one I'd been on the end of since I was sixteen had that in it somewhere, underneath, holding the thing up. Hers didn't, and the whole structure stayed standing anyway.

The catalogue went quiet. I hadn't known it could.

I didn't know how long the window would stay open. Windows don't, in my experience. So I was already deciding to use it - which is not the same as trusting her. I was spending something before it expired.

"It was a virus," I said. Quiet.

She settled back into the chair instead of standing.

"They had a whole shelf of versions of it. Numbered. The one they gave me was Chimera 9. They are all different methods of splicing DNA. The other kids got other numbers."

I rubbed at my eye with the back of my wrist.

"Mine took. Most of them didn't. The ones that did sometimes came out worse than I did. Things that stopped looking like people on the way through. I don't know what was done with those ones after."

She didn't say anything.

"After the place came down, the agency found me. Put me to work. The dangerous-animals part is half the truth. The other half is - rich people lose things they'd rather nobody else find. A phone in a forest. A ring that went over the side of a boat. Strange jobs, weird hours, more money than the work is worth. The kind of money people pay you to forget about the job once it's done. The rest of it I can't really talk about."

She was quiet a long moment. She was looking at me the same way she'd looked at me when I came out of the bathroom - like somebody who'd come in from the yard with something scraped up. Except this time the scrapes were under the skin. She could see them. She wasn't going to mention it.

"The biggest hurdle is I can't go outside without a disguise," I said. "People see me and they get scared. Or they get violent. Or they get scared and call somebody who'll get violent. The hat and the dress and the contacts - that's how I buy groceries. That's how I get on a bus. That's how I knock on a front door without somebody phoning it in."

"Mm." She didn't disagree.

She moved closer and tilted my chin up under the lamp. "Let me look at the eyes properly."

I looked. Red into a face that did not flinch.

"Mm-hm. Pupils respond fine. Whole-iris color, not just a ring around the edge - that's a clean conversion. Any blurriness through the day? Halos around lights?"

"No."

"And the dryness when the contacts come out. Five seconds of saline rinse, next time. Cuts the recovery."

"Mm."

"Night vision better than mine?"

"Yes." I nodded.

"I'd hope so." A small quick smile. She let go of my chin and stepped back half a step.

"The teeth are more of a challenge. The rest you can hide if you're careful. Can't hide when I bite food."

"May I see, sweetheart? You don't have to."

I opened my mouth a little. Canines longer than they had any business being, sharp where they ought to have been rounded.

She looked. She tilted my chin up gently to catch the lamplight. Looked at the gum line. The cusps. The way the canines sat in the socket. "Do they cut your lip when you talk?"

"No, not really. My mouth seems to have adjusted to the shape of the bite."

"And the rest - open wider for me."

I opened. She looked along the back of the bite.

"All right. The bite's even. That's good news. You'd have known by now if it wasn't - jaw, headaches, the whole catalogue. The fact that you don't says the rest of your jaw came along with them."

She didn't look away.

After a long moment she nodded, like she was filing the picture somewhere safe. Then she stood. Picked the blanket up from the foot of the bed and laid it neater across. Paused at the door.

"Osyra."

"Yeah."

"I'm glad you came. Even as you are."

She closed the door softly behind her.

I lay down. My tail curled around against the small of my back, finding the sleeping curl on its own. My ears flattened against the pillow.

I had not, in fact, fallen apart.

Nothing in me was bracing. I went looking for it the way your tongue goes to a tooth that's gone, and it wasn't there, and I was still here.

I'd always assumed the bracing was load-bearing. That it was the thing holding the rest up. It had come off and nothing had fallen down, and I lay in the dark with that, not liking it as much as I should have. It's a specific feeling, finding an error in your own work. If I was wrong about that, I'd been wrong about it every day, in every room, since I was six years old.

The house creaked. Settled. Somewhere down the hall, a kettle clicked off.

I reached over to the dresser, found the comms pin where I'd set it next to the hat, and clipped it to the base of my ear. Keyed it once.

"Chimera One to Wiki. Checking in."

A pause that wasn't really a pause. "Wiki receiving. You're a day early on sounding human."

"Don't get used to it."

"I won't. Still in one piece?"

"Still in one piece. Staying the night. Maybe longer."

"Noted." A small sound on the line - Vanity, logging it, the way she logged everything. A line in a record only she kept. "Sleep, Operator. Call me tomorrow."

"Roger."

I unclipped the pin and set it back by the hat.

I closed my eyes.
