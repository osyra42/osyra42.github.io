# Chapter 8 - Shark Attack

The tension in the gymnasium settled into a fragile watchful stalemate over the week. The mockery died, choked off by the super-soldier rumor and by my own deliberate silence.

I perfected the stare. A flat unblinking look that makes red eyes read less like a flaw and more like a targeting laser. I'd aim it at anyone who lingered and they'd find something else to look at.

It works. That's the part I'd underestimated. I'd spent eighteen years trying to be smaller and it turns out the whole time there was a lever right there marked *be worse* and it works instantly and costs nothing up front.

Processing was a blur of custom fittings, psych evals, endless waiting. On the second day they issued the uniform: standard Titan Reach fatigues, digital grey-green, but mine had been subtly altered. Collar widened. Trousers reinforced with a gusset of tougher material at the base of the spine.

Wearing it felt strange. Less like clothes. More like being fitted for a new and official kind of skin.

The message was clear, and it was two messages at once, which I think is the most honest thing that building ever said to me: *you are standardized* and *you are apart.* Same garment. They had to build a special version of the uniform that makes everyone the same.

---

The day of transport to the main barracks, the distinction went public.

Pre-dawn formation in the yard outside the bunker. Fifty of us, a ragged shivering line in identical fatigues, clutching duffels of standard-issue gear. No discipline yet. Just a jostling anxious crowd.

A staff sergeant with a face like a hatchet stood on a crate. "Listen up, maggots! You are no longer civilians. You are names on a manifest. You will be addressed by your last names, and you will respond with 'Yes, Sergeant!' or 'No, Sergeant!' Is that understood?"

A mumbled uneven chorus came back.

"Pathetic! Now, first row, move! And keep your traps shut!"

As the first recruits shuffled toward the idling bus, his eyes found me at the back. He pointed a rigid finger.

"You. Renaya. The special project. You're separate. You do not board until I say. You will take the last seat, the one marked with red tape. You will not speak to anyone. You follow all instructions given to the group, but you do it alone. You are not part of this platoon. You are an attachment. Do you comprehend?"

Every head in uniform turned.

*Renaya.* He used my name. It was the first time in that building anybody had, and he used it to explain to fifty people that I wasn't one of them.

"Yes, Sergeant," I said. Clear and toneless.

"Good. Remember it."

One by one the last names were barked and recruits filed on. I watched them go with my duffel at my feet. Finally: "Renaya! Forward!"

I walked the gauntlet of faces in the bus windows. Curious. Hostile. Afraid. I climbed the steps. The interior was silent, the air thick.

And there at the very back was a single seat, its cracked vinyl marked with a glaring X of red duct tape.

An island. Somebody had gone out there with a roll of tape, before dawn, and marked it. That's a work order. Somewhere there's a line item for the tape.

"Sit!" the sergeant yelled from outside.

I slid in and stowed my gear. The space around me felt electrically charged. A buffer zone of pure isolation. The driver closed the door and the bus lurched forward, winding deeper into the forest.

---

For the first hour, tense exhausted quiet.

Then, thirty minutes out, it began.

A single brutal shout from the front. A drill instructor I hadn't seen board was suddenly on his feet, face contorted with fury.

"WHAT IS THIS, A GIRL SCOUT TOUR? THIS BUS IS NOW A WARZONE! HEADS! BETWEEN! YOUR! KNEES! NOW! ALL OF YOU! DO IT!"

Chaos. Recruits fumbled, diving forward, caps tumbling. "I CAN'T HEAR YOUR FOREHEADS HITTING THE SEATS! HARDER! YOU THINK THE ENEMY GIVES YOU NECK PILLOWS?"

The Shark Attack. The deliberate overwhelming verbal assault built to erase individuality and install instant blind obedience. He paced the aisle, voice a constant deafening percussion against the metal shell.

"NO LOOKING! EYES ON YOUR BOOTS OR THE DECK! YOU LOOK AT ME, I'LL PERSONALLY PEEL YOUR EYELIDS BACK! SHUT UP! THE ONLY SOUND I WANT IS YOUR WEAK LITTLE HEARTS TRYING NOT TO GIVE OUT!"

I followed the order, forehead against the cool metal of the optic case in my hands, the stiff brim of my cap digging into my wrist. My ears, pinned flat under it, rang with the volume. Every shout a physical slap.

He did not single me out.

His boots crunched past the red-taped seat without pause. I was just another recruit in fatigues with her head down, obeying.

"YOU ARE NOTHING! YOU ARE WORTHLESS! YOU WILL DO WHAT YOU ARE TOLD, WHEN YOU ARE TOLD, HOW YOU ARE TOLD! YOUR GEAR IS YOUR ONLY FRIEND! GET IT BETWEEN YOUR LEGS! HUG IT! LOVE IT! IT'S THE ONLY THING THAT MIGHT SAVE YOUR MISERABLE LIFE!"

The bus echoed with scrambling, with duffels wrestled into position, with panicked held breaths.

And I want to put down what happened in me during that half hour, because I have not been able to explain it to anybody since and I am not sure it reflects well on me.

It was the best I had felt in two years.

*You are nothing.* He screamed it at fifty people and he meant every one of them and he did not look at me when he said it. Nobody in that bus was a chimera candidate. Nobody was an auricular structure or a caudal integration point or a non-human cellular line. We were all worthless, identically, at the top of a man's lungs, and for thirty minutes I was not the exception to anything.

That's all it took. That's the entire price of admission to the human race, as far as I can tell: find someone who hates everyone equally.

I hugged a duffel between my knees with my forehead on an optic case and my ears bleeding sound, and I was so grateful I could have laughed.

The Shark Attack ran the whole final half hour. By the time the bus crunched onto gravel, the silence that replaced the shouting was hollow and ringing. My neck ached. My hands were numb from gripping the case.

"OFF MY BUS! NOW! MOVE, MOVE, MOVE! FORM A LINE ON THE YARD! DUFFELS AT YOUR FEET! EYES FRONT! MOVE LIKE YOUR LIVES DEPEND ON IT, BECAUSE STARTING TODAY, THEY DO!"

Recruits stumbled into the aisle, uniforms rumpled, movements jerky with residual fear.

I waited, as instructed, until the bus was nearly empty. Then I stood, adjusted my cap, slung my duffel, took up the case.

Last one down the steps.

---

The yard was shell-shocked recruits trying to form lines under the blistering shouts of three drill instructors. One of them - a mountain of a man with a shaved scalp and the same voice that had run the bus - saw me. His eyes took in the uniform, the case, my position. No personal malice in them. Just the flat assessing glare he'd give any piece of irregular equipment on his manifest.

"RENAYA!" he bellowed. Not a taunt. A designation. "The attachment! You don't get in line! You stand there!" He pointed to a spot ten feet left of the ragged formation, the gesture as precise as a surveyor's. "You are a shadow! You follow all training, but you are not of this platoon! Understood?"

"Understood, Drill Sergeant!" I shouted. Raw in my throat.

I marched to the spot, dropped my duffel, snapped to attention, the case rigid at my side.

Thirty minutes. That's how long it lasted. Off the bus and back onto my own square of gravel, ten feet from everybody, measured out by a man with a pointing finger.

He gave me one final impersonal glance - a commander confirming a logistical detail - then turned his full terrifying focus back to the platoon. "WHAT ARE YOU STARING AT, MAGGOTS? YOUR JOB IS TO LEARN, NOT GAPE! EYES FRONT!"

I stared straight ahead, over the heads of the identically dressed recruits, at the stark grey wall of the nearest barracks.

The case bit into my palm through the glove. The Shark Attack's adrenaline was still going in my veins with nowhere to put itself.

Ten feet. It's not far. You could hand somebody a cup of coffee across ten feet.
