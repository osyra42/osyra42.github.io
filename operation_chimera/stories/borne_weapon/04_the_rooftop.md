# Chapter 4 - The Rooftop

A week passed. The couriered package arrived: a blister pack of pills and a clinical-grade basal thermometer.

I left it on the kitchen counter. Untouched. A tiny rebellion, and I knew exactly how tiny while I was having it. Every itch, every sharpened smell, I monitored with a dread that curdled into something like defiance.

*This is still mine.*

That's the whole argument. That's all I had. It isn't much of one - I was defending my ownership of a thing that was installed in me by men I never consented to meet, which makes it about as mine as a bullet is mine once it's in me. But there's a difference between a thing being done to you and a thing you decide to carry, and the difference is the only property I've ever actually held title to.

The drive window came two weeks after the exam, right on the calendar I've never needed to write down.

Not a surprise. An ambush I chose.

The restlessness I'd carried for days sharpened, by dusk, into something with claws. A wire pulled taut. My apartment turned into a torture chamber of amplified sensation - the fridge motor a jackhammer, the smell of my own skin sour and overwhelming. The pills gleamed on the counter.

Taking them felt like letting them win. Not taking them felt like insanity. I want to note that I could see, even then, that "letting them win" is not a medical consideration. I could see it and it did not move me one inch.

I couldn't be contained. I went to the only place left. The roof.

---

The night air was cool on skin that ran too hot and too aware. The city sprawled below, a map of smells and distant sounds. I paced the gravel, tail lashing, ears swiveling at every siren, every shout off the streets. The need was a physical pressure, a hollow pulling throb.

That was when I caught his scent.

It cut through the chemical haze of the city - clean soap, fresh linen, and under it something warm and vital. Human. But distinct. Compelling.

My head snapped up.

He was at the far end of the roof, leaning on the parapet, looking at the skyline. A silhouette.

I was across the roof without deciding to go. My body moved and my mind came along behind it, and I'd like to state plainly that this is the thing I fear most in the world and I did it in under four seconds for a man I could smell.

He turned as I came. Tall. Kind eyes that widened in surprise, then went to careful curiosity as he took me in - the hood, the ears in the moonlight, the agitated tail.

Most people run. Or scream. He watched.

His eyes went from my pinned ears to my too-bright gaze, and his expression shifted from curiosity to a dawning, careful comprehension. He didn't look at my ears like they were costumes. He looked at them like they were symptoms.

"You're… in some kind of trouble," he said softly. Not a question about what I was. A question about the state I was in.

"You shouldn't be up here alone," I said, and it came out a rough scrape. It wasn't a warning. It was a confession.

"Neither should you." Calm. A deep, steady sound that vibrated in my chest. "I'm Leo. I live downstairs."

I stopped a few feet off, close enough that his scent washed over me. The animal screamed to press closer, to rub my cheek against his jacket, to taste the salt on his skin.

I locked my knees to stay still.

"Osyra," I managed.

He nodded, gaze dropping to my twitching ears, then back to my eyes. He didn't flinch from the red. He glanced from my face to the city far below, and something in his expression settled with decision, not fear.

"What do you need?" he asked.

The question was simple and it took me apart.

I didn't have an answer that wasn't primal. *Contact. Pressure. You.*

So I reached out instead. Slowly. Giving him every chance to pull back. My fingertips brushed the back of his hand where it rested on the ledge.

The contact was electric. A jolt that straightened my spine. His skin was cool. My whole world narrowed to that point.

He turned his hand over. Palm up. An invitation, or a surrender.

I slid my fingers into his. His grip was firm. Steadying.

And the floodgate opened - all the frantic scattered energy in me coalesced and ran toward that anchor. The painful edge of it blunted. Not gone. Channeled.

We stood there in the dark, hand in hand, not speaking. The city noise went to a murmur. My breathing slowed to match his. My ears, which had been flicking at everything, relaxed and rotated gently toward the rhythm of his heartbeat. My tail stilled and curled around my own ankle.

It wasn't passion. Not yet. It was something more fundamental. A quieting. A recognition.

The thing in my blood, frantic and howling, saw him and settled.

He lifted our joined hands, thumb stroking a slow line across my knuckles. Unbearably gentle. Human.

"Is this okay?" he whispered.

I leaned my forehead against his shoulder and hid my face in the clean linen smell of his shirt. I nodded.

For the first time since it started, the cage wasn't pressing in. The door wasn't open, but the lock felt less heavy.

He held my hand in the dark, and for a little while I wasn't a subject, or an applicant, or a monster.

I was a girl on a rooftop holding onto something warm, waiting for the heat to break.

---

I went inside.

The ghost of his touch felt different under a ceiling. Not like a connection. Like a smudge.

The pill kit sat on the counter. A stark, clinical answer to the mess I'd just made. *Non-negotiable for field readiness.* What a joke. I couldn't manage a hand on a roof.

I punched a pill through the foil.

A small white admission of failure. I'd gone up there to escape the frenzy and what had I done - dragged a stranger into it. Practically glued myself to some poor guy trying to get some air. He'd been kind, which made it worse. He saw a woman in obvious distress and he was decent about it, and he had no idea the distress was an engineered biological command, that I'd honed in on his scent like a predator picks a line.

My skin crawled with it.

The pill wasn't about control going forward. It was an eraser for tonight.

I swallowed it dry. The bitter chalk was a fitting punishment.

---

That night the heat didn't vanish, but its meaning changed. The need was still a thrum in my blood; the frantic compulsive story around it was gone. And the memory of leaning on him, of twining my fingers with his, replayed in my head not as solace but as a cringing lapse in judgment.

*What was I thinking?*

I hadn't been. That was the whole problem.

I fell asleep wrapped in a cold blanket of regret, the arousal reduced to a dull physiological echo, stripped of its dangerous illusion.

---

Morning brought a brutal clarity. The fog was gone, burned off by the regulator. With it went the last of the heat's romantic lie.

In the crisp quiet light I saw the rooftop for what it was: a vulnerable, potentially catastrophic mistake. Leo wasn't a point of quiet humanity. He was a variable. An unknown. A random man who now had a very strange story to tell, if he chose to.

My ears flattened as I replayed it. The neediness. The trembling. Offering my name like a gift, when it was just another piece of data I'd handed over for free.

A cold sharp peace cut through me. This was the clarity I needed. No more messing with strangers. No more letting the animal dictate my interactions. The pill gave me the silence to hear my own priorities and they were singular: survive, get control, get out. Anything else was a liability.

I've had a long time to look at that morning, and I want to set down what I've since worked out about it, because I couldn't see it then and it was the whole thing.

Consider what the pill actually did.

It did not make me see the night correctly. It made me see the night *coldly*, and I have spent my entire life treating those as the same word. Cold is my instrument. Cold is how I know something's true. So when I woke up and the memory had gone from *a quieting* to *a lapse*, I didn't experience that as a chemical rewriting my testimony. I experienced it as waking up.

But the man on the roof asked me what I needed and then gave it to me and did not ask for one thing back. That happened. It happened whether or not there was a drug in my blood when I remembered it. The pill did not add any information. It only changed the verdict, and it changed the verdict in exactly the direction the company that couriered it would prefer - toward *variable*, toward *liability*, toward a closed system that doesn't reach for anybody and can be deployed anywhere.

They didn't have to tell me to think that. They just had to make thinking it feel like sobriety.

I never took a second one. I want that on the record, because it matters to what comes after and because I have never told anyone.

One pill. That's all it took. The bottle sat on that counter for two weeks and I didn't touch it again - I didn't need to. The heat broke on its own schedule, the way it always does, and by then the thing the pill had done to my memory of the roof was permanent. You don't have to keep taking something that only had to work once.

The placid, emotionless days that followed were a relief, and they were mine. No chemistry in them. That's the part I couldn't see: I'd swallowed one tablet as an eraser and then spent a fortnight doing the erasing myself, for free, and calling it clarity.

Then the phone rang.
