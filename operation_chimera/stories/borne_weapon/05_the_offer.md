# Chapter 5 - The Offer

The manufactured quiet was broken by a single phone call. It didn't come from a blocked number. It came with a title.

"This is Lieutenant Colonel Daniel Thorne, Joint Special Operations Personnel Division." A voice carved out of granite, with the ghost of a southern drawl smoothing the edges. No warmth in it. Only authority. "I am contacting you on behalf of our private-sector liaison, Titan Reach Consulting."

My grip tightened on the phone. I was sitting on the fire escape, which was two floors and one week from the rooftop and felt like another country. My tail lay inert.

"They've forwarded your clinical evaluation package, Ms. Osyra. It's… thorough. Dr. Vasquez's notes indicate you possess a… unique aptitude profile."

I heard the subtext. *A controlled asset. Or a loose cannon.*

Both of those are things you buy. Nobody makes a phone call about a third option.

"Titan Reach has a proposal. They field operatives. You've passed the medical. Now you face the practical."

The sun felt interrogative.

"They have a dedicated training curriculum at a secure facility. A final evaluation under simulated high-stress conditions. Think of it as a combine for people who hunt things that shouldn't exist. If you pass, a formal job offer is guaranteed. Full benefits, structured pay, a defined role. A purpose."

A purpose. A structure. Walls to replace the cage. Rules to prevent rooftop mistakes.

"And if I fail?" Flat.

A pause. "Failure in this line of work has permanent consequences. But in the context of the evaluation, it means you walk away. No offer. Your file is closed. Titan Reach considers the matter concluded."

*Concluded.* Either they'd forget me, or they'd make certain I stopped being a variable. He left the gap on purpose and let me furnish it myself, which is cheaper than a threat and does the same work.

"What does it involve?"

"Need-to-know, delivered on-site. It will assess tactical acumen, threat response, team interoperability, and stress resilience under controlled exposures. All necessary equipment provided. You need only bring yourself."

*And the discipline not to compromise yourself.* A stranger's hand, sharp in my mind.

"You have forty-eight hours to decide. An NDA will be transmitted. Sign it, and coordinates for transport will follow. Do not sign it, and this conversation never happened."

The line died.

---

I sat in the fading light and did the arithmetic, and the arithmetic was clean, and I want to walk through it exactly as I had it then, because I have never been able to find the error and I have looked.

The pills on my counter were proof I couldn't trust my own biology.

The rooftop was proof I couldn't trust my own judgment once the biology had hold of it.

That's two of the three legs gone. You cannot stand on one. So the only move left was to stop trying to be my own authority and hand the job to something bigger with clearer rules - outsource the trust. Titan Reach doesn't care what I regret. It cares what I can do. There's a kind of mercy in a system that only grades your output, and I had been looking for that mercy my whole life without a name for it.

The recruiter's call was a lifeline thrown off the same ship that designed my storm. It was the only play on the board.

Here's what I couldn't see, sitting on that fire escape with the phone going cold in my hand.

Every piece of evidence in that proof was manufactured by the people making the offer. The clinical established the liability. The courier delivered the fix. The fix wrote the verdict on the roof. And forty-eight hours later a colonel called to tell me the solution to my unreliability was to become theirs.

I had one data point that didn't come from them - a man asking what I needed and then just giving it, on a roof, for free, at no profit to anyone. That one point disproved the whole model. I had already thrown it out. I'd thrown it out the morning after, in a bathroom, and called the throwing-out *clarity*.

I went inside. The NDA glowed on the screen.

I didn't hesitate. The shame of what I'd been with a stranger was a sharper spur than any ambition. It moved me faster than money would have. That's worth noticing, if anyone's keeping score: they didn't have to offer me enough. They only had to make me ashamed of the one hour of my life that hadn't cost anybody anything.

I typed my name.

The screen refreshed. Coordinates. A time: 04:30. **Pack Light.**

I looked at the pills on the counter. Two weeks they'd sat there. I hadn't needed a second one and I wasn't going to - whatever that tablet was for, it had done its work in a single night and the work had held.

I left them where they were and went to find a bag.

The only thing I really needed to pack was the resolve not to be a mistake again.
