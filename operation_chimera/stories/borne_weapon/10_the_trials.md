# Chapter 10 - The Trials

The gas chamber was a small concrete-block building that smelled, even from outside, like dread and old chemicals.

We lined up in our protective masks, the tight rubber seals alien against my face, pressing my ears flat. Sergeant Hale stood by the door, his own mask making him look like a giant unfeeling insect.

"This is CS gas. Tear gas. It will not kill you. It will make you wish you were dead. The point is to trust your gear, and to learn how to clear and seal your mask under stress. You will enter, form a line, and on my command, you will break the seal, remove your mask, state your name and rank, then reseal. Anyone who runs for the door before I say 'go' repeats. Anyone who doesn't complete the exercise repeats. Understood?"

"YES, SERGEANT!"

One by one we filed into the thick hazy air. Even masked, my eyes started to water. The air had a sharp metallic taste that seeped through.

Then my turn.

"RENAYA! BREAK SEAL AND REMOVE!"

I pulled the mask off my face.

The world dissolved into instant agonizing fire. Like inhaling crushed glass and hornets. My lungs seized, a raw scraping cough tearing out of me. My eyes flooded, vision smearing into grey walls and green uniforms.

And it was exactly as bad as it was for everyone else in that room.

No worse. No special weakness the lab had built in. Just the same chemical hell, democratically applied. It sat in my sinuses and the back of my throat a little longer - sharper senses, slower to stop screaming - but that's a rounding error.

The great equalizer. In there I was one more body drowning in its own mucus.

"NAME AND RANK!" Hale bellowed from somewhere in the burning fog.

I tried to suck air to speak and got a mouthful of fire. "RENAYA-!" A strangled rasp before a coughing fit doubled me over. Snot pouring from my nose, thick and uncontrollable.

All around me the sounds were the same. Retching, gagging, the wet desperate hacking of fifty people drowning in their own mucus. The respiratory plague that had haunted the bay for a week was instantly, violently cured. Nobody was congested anymore. Every passage ruthlessly, painfully clear.

I'd spent a week being the only healthy body in a sick room. It took a concrete shed full of poison gas to get me admitted.

"SEAL YOUR MASK!"

I fumbled the rubber back over my face, fingers slick with tears and snot, and sucked in the sweet filtered air. Instantaneous relief, though the burning lingered another hour, the way it did for everyone.

We stumbled out into the clean cold daylight, faces raw and shining, all of us united in a new profound understanding of misery.

For a few minutes, nobody looked at me. They were too busy trying to remember how to breathe.

***

Night ops came a week later.

They issued night-vision goggles - heavy bulky units that turned the world into a ghostly green landscape of vague shapes and halos. We practiced moving in squads, communicating in whispers, the gear hissing and whining in our ears.

I tried to use them. I did try.

The amplified light was overwhelming, washing out detail, and the limited field of view was like looking through two foggy soda bottles. It was harder to see with them than without.

During a navigation exercise, moving through dense woods, I finally lifted them onto my forehead.

The world didn't just brighten. It clarified.

The darkness wasn't an obstacle. It was a different palette. Deep shadows held texture and depth. I could tell the grey of a rock from the grey of a root, see the path of crushed leaves, track the heat-shimmer of a recruit ten yards off as a faint rippling disturbance in the cool air. My pupils gathered every stray photon. I could hear the lieutenant's whispered commands from two squads over with perfect clarity.

I read the real night like a cloudy twilight, and fifty people around me stumbled through an electronic haze.

"Renaya, why are your NODs stowed?" A hiss. Corporal Vance, training cadre.

"They degrade my natural sight, Corporal. My night vision is sufficient."

A long pause. I could feel his stare in the dark, and he couldn't see my face at all, and there was something in that worth having.

"Noted. Keep up. And don't make me regret this."

I didn't make him regret it.

By the end of the exercise I was quietly directing my squad around obstacles I could see and they couldn't, my whispers keeping them out of ditches and off low branches. Nobody thanked me. But when we reassembled, ours was the only squad without a sprained ankle or a face full of spiderwebs.

The looks weren't fear or disgust anymore. They were grim practical acknowledgment.

I'll take that trade too. I was starting to notice how many trades I'd taken.

***

The PT tests were where it shifted, almost imperceptibly.

Straightforward: two-mile run, max push-ups in two minutes, max sit-ups in two, pull-ups. For me they were laughable. My body performed with a cold efficiency that unsettled even me.

But standing there panting lightly after the run, watching others stagger across the line with faces purple from strain, something strange happened.

I didn't feel smug. I felt impatient.

"Come on, Miller! You've got fifty yards! Dig! It's just pain, leave it on the road!"

The words were out of my mouth before I thought about them. A full sentence, at volume, in a yard, at a man who'd called me Fido.

Miller shot me a look of pure shock. Then he grimaced and found a last desperate sprint.

On the pull-up bar I watched Carter fail his seventh rep, arms shaking. "Don't let your knees drift, Carter! Lock your core! You've got three more in you, I know you do!"

He grunted, heaved, got an eighth.

It wasn't kindness. I checked. I checked immediately, standing right there, because if it was kindness then something in me had come unlatched without permission and I needed to know.

It wasn't. It was pragmatism, and brutal. We were cells in an organism. If the cell next to me is weak, the whole body is weaker. My own performance is irrelevant if the platoon fails. The DIs had beaten that into us for weeks.

For the first time I internalized it - not as a threat. As a mechanic of survival.

That's what I told myself in the yard and I was satisfied with it, and I would like to now point out the flaw, since nobody else was going to.

I wasn't part of the organism. I never had been. That was the whole design - red tape on a seat, ten feet off the formation, *you are not of this platoon.* If the platoon failed, the attachment did not fail with it. I had checked that months ago, the way I check everything.

So the pragmatic argument for shouting at Miller was worth exactly nothing. It was a false proof I built at speed to cover the fact that I wanted him to make it.

I didn't see that for years.

---

Slowly, tentatively, I became able to lead. Not by appointment. By default.

On ruck marches, when somebody's pack strap was digging in, I'd bark the adjustment drill Hale taught us. During the endless mind-numbing parade drills, when the rank lost its dressing, my height and voice from the flank called the alignment back. "Dress right, DRESS! Hold your interval, Garcia!"

And the most profound comfort in that entire forest was the silence from the DIs.

They never blamed me for being different. Not once, in a month. They only ever blamed the others for noticing.

"YOU STARING AT HER FEET TO LEARN THE CADENCE, JENKINS? EYES FRONT!"

That simple consistent reinforcement was a balm I hadn't known I needed. My existence was not the problem. Their lack of focus was.

Twelve years. That's how long I'd waited for someone in authority to look at a room and locate the problem somewhere other than my body. It took a defense contractor with a training schedule to do it, and they did it for the wrong reasons, and it worked anyway. I don't know what to do with that. I still don't.

We did ruck marches that felt like they'd never end, the Mk 13 an old familiar enemy on my shoulder. Hand-to-hand in the gritty pits, where my reach and speed became assets I learned to control instead of flail. Parade drills until the movements were autonomic, tail held in a tight still curl against my leg. A form of camping they called bivouacking, which was mostly lying in a cold damp hole trying not to shiver, listening to a forest with ears that catch every rustle and snap.

I was not becoming one of them. That was never the goal. The red-taped seat still existed.

But the space around me had stopped being a vacuum of hostility. It had become a space of function. I was a unique tool in the kit and the other tools were slowly, grudgingly, learning to work alongside me.

And I was starting to care whether they could do their jobs, which surprised me more than anything that happened in that forest.

The withering internal narration - *freak, monster, mistake* - was getting drowned out by the immediate demands of the task. *Move faster. Shoot straighter. Carry more. Help them.*

The thing in my blood, the one that drove me across a rooftop to a stranger, was being channeled. Given a direction. Given a pack, however dysfunctional.

I noticed the shape of that at the time and let it go by, and I'd like to pick it up now.

On the roof, that thing wanted a person, and I called it a malfunction and swallowed something to make it stop. Here it wanted fifty people, and they gave it a rifle and a rank and told me it was leadership.

It isn't that they cured me. Nobody cured anything, and nobody medicated me either - I'd taken exactly one tablet in my life and that was two months and two hundred miles ago. They found the appetite the lab installed and pointed it at a mission, and that was all it took. And it *worked*. That's the part I can't get around even now. I was less alone in that hole in the ground than I'd been since I was six, and every bit of it was issued to me.

A different kind of cage. But for the first time in my life the walls weren't only there to contain me.

They were there to shape me into something that could, perhaps, eventually break out.

I believed that. I want it in the record that I believed it, standing in a forest at twenty, holding a rifle that fit.
