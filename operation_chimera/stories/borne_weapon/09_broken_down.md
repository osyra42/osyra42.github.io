# Chapter 9 - Broken Down

The first week wasn't about being different. It was about being broken down.

The humiliation was total and it was democratic. It wasn't personal, because nothing there was personal. You were a body in uniform and your body was weak, slow, and stupid. One recruit fumbled a drill step and the whole platoon paid in push-ups on the gravel, the DI's voice shredding the air above us. Somebody's footlocker sat a millimeter out of alignment at inspection and we all stood at attention for an hour in the biting cold while he methodically dumped every locker in the bay onto the floor.

The lesson was a blunt instrument: you are a cell in an organism. One cell fails, the whole body suffers.

I understood the architecture of it within about two days, and I want to be fair to them - it's good work. It is the most efficient machine for making a group out of strangers that I have ever been inside of. You don't build loyalty by asking for it. You build it by charging everyone for one person's mistake until the arithmetic makes them each other's problem.

I could see it working. I watched it work on fifty people.

It did not work on me, and not because I resisted it. Because you can't be made into a cell of an organism from ten feet outside the organism.

My status as *the attachment* meant I stood apart during formations but suffered alongside them for the consequences. I did the push-ups. I stood in the cold. I scrubbed. And I did all of it a full head taller than almost everyone.

At six-four my height had always been a fact. In ranks of recruits averaging five-ten it became a permanent feature of the landscape. I had to consciously slump during some drills to not break the line - a habit the DIs beat out of me fast.

"STAND AT YOUR GOD-GIVEN ATTENTION, RENAYA! DON'T APOLOGIZE FOR THE BLUEPRINT!"

*God-given.* That's the joke, and he'll never know he made it.

There was so much cleaning. Floors had to gleam like obsidian. Latrines had to smell of nothing but bleach. We scrubbed with toothbrushes, with rags, with our own fingernails. Industrial cleaner became a permanent note in the back of my throat. My height was an advantage there - no shelf out of reach, so I got the high dusting and the light fixtures while the others grumbled at baseboards.

Physical training was a relentless grind. Calisthenics at 0400 in the dark. Long grueling runs in formation, the pack's rhythm a punishing collective heartbeat. My physiology handled the cardio better than most - my lungs burned but they didn't scream - and my legs gave me a stride that made the runs feel almost natural, though I had to check my pace constantly to keep from overtaking the file.

Constantly. Every run. That's the detail I'd give anyone who asked what that month was actually like: I spent it running slower than I can run, so that a line of men could stay in front of me.

The volume of it wore on everyone. My muscles ached with a new deep fatigue and my tail often felt like dead weight, a clumsy appendage I had to consciously tuck and control.

By the third day a plague of respiratory illness swept the bay. Wet hacking coughs and congested sniffling, a constant chorus. Sinus pain made faces puffy and eyes watery. The DIs showed no mercy. "I DON'T CARE IF YOU'RE COUGHING UP A LUNG, MAGGOT! YOU MOVE!"

I watched recruits stumble through drills shivering with fever and collapse afterward.

I stayed healthy. Another side-effect - the lab's logs called it a *robust adaptive immune response*, and there's a man somewhere who was proud of that sentence.

So I moved through a bay full of sick people at full strength, looking down at them from a height nobody asked me to have, and there is no version of that where you come out clean. I couldn't be sick with them. It's the only thing they all had in common that week and I couldn't have it. Every morning I was fine and fifty people weren't, and the fine was doing more damage to my standing in that room than the ears ever did.

---

Then came the weapons.

The armory was a temple of oil and cold metal. We filed past and the armorer, a grizzled specialist with sergeant's stripes, called assignments. "Smith, M4. Gonzalez, M4. Carter, M4."

He reached me. His eyes flicked from the manifest up to my face - a longer journey than for most - then to a different, heavier rack.

"Renaya. Mk 13 Mod 7. .300 Winchester Magnum. Bolt-action precision rifle. With this."

He handed it over.

It was a beast. Over four feet long, thick fluted barrel, massive muzzle brake, chunky telescopic sight. The weight settled into my hands - a dense profound thirteen pounds of steel and polymer and glass. Less a weapon than a piece of industrial machinery meant to be mounted on something.

Compared to the M4s the others cradled like toys, it was from another world.

In my hands, its scale made sense. It didn't look oversized. It looked correct.

I stood there in a cold room holding the first object I have ever been handed that was the right size, and I want to say I felt nothing about that, and I did feel something about it, and I have never liked what.

A stunned silence, then a sharp collective intake of breath. Heads turned. A recruit named Finch, two people ahead, craned his neck, eyes wide as saucers.

Before he could blink, the armorer's voice cracked like a whip. "FINCH! YOU SEE SOMETHING PRETTY? YOU ON A TOUR? YOUR WEAPON IS IN YOUR HANDS! EYES ON YOUR OWN GEAR UNLESS YOU WANT TO CLEAN EVERY WEAPON IN THIS ARMORY WITH A TOOTHBRUSH!"

Finch's head snapped forward so fast I heard his neck pop.

The lesson was immediate: staring at the anomaly is a failure of focus.

It became a pattern. Zeroing on the range, settling in behind the rifle, I'd feel eyes. A flicker in my peripheral vision.

"HILL! YOU TRYING TO LEARN BY OSMOSIS? WISHING WON'T MAKE YOUR GROUPING TIGHTER! FOCUS ON YOUR FRONT SIGHT POST OR I'LL HAVE YOU WEARING IT AS AN EARRING!"

On a march, someone stumbling behind me would gawk at the rifle on my shoulder or the tip of my tail keeping time with the walk.

"VALDEZ! THE ROAD IS IN FRONT OF YOU, NOT ON HER BACK! YOU LOST? NEED A MAP? PICK UP THE PACE AND WATCH YOUR SECTOR!"

The DIs never mocked me for being looked at. They mocked the others for looking.

It took me a while to see it whole, and when I did I had to admire it, the way you admire a trap that's already closed on you. They never once had to say *don't call her a dog.* They never issued a rule about it. They simply made looking at me expensive, and the men stopped looking, and the discipline they were building got built out of the material I was standing there being.

My difference wasn't a source of personal humiliation anymore. It was a test for everyone else. A distraction to be overcome. A temptation to fail. Every head that turned my way was an excuse for a DI to pounce, punish, reinforce the only thing that mattered: the task.

My presence became a metric of their discipline. I was equipment they were being graded on ignoring.

I couldn't decide then whether that was kindness. I've had years and I still can't. It worked. It got me quiet. It cost them nothing and it cost me the last thing I had, which was the possibility that one of those fifty people might have looked at me on purpose, for a reason of his own.

---

The drawback of the Mk 13 was constant and grinding. So was the advantage, and it showed up during a tactical drill.

We were simulating perimeter guard. The DI pointed at me. "Renaya! That cannon is a liability inside ten meters. Simulate a security hand-off. Briggs! Secure her primary!"

Briggs had muscles on his muscles and stood a good eight inches shorter. He stepped forward. His eyes darted from my face to the rifle - that same flicker of awe and apprehension.

"BRIGGS! YOU TAKING A CENSUS OF HER FEATURES OR SECURING A WEAPON? MOVE!"

He snapped to, but the damage was done. He was already off-balance.

I unslung the Mk 13 and passed it over. The moment the full weight transferred his body jerked, eyes widening as his posture sank, arms tightening instinctively to wrestle the unexpected heft.

"Looks like a struggle, Briggs! You admiring the craftsmanship or fighting a bear? Renaya, you're weaponless! Demonstrate a hostile take-down. Non-lethal. Go."

Unburdened by thirteen pounds of anchor, my body became pure potential.

I was across the gap in one long stride before he could reset. I used his rooted unbalanced posture against him, my longer arms bypassing his guard, swept his legs, drove him to the mat with a controlled thud.

Controlled. I want that word to stand, because I put it there deliberately and it is doing a lot of work.

I did not decide any of that. Not one piece of it. The word *go* left the man's mouth and something in me that is faster than I am read Briggs's stance, found the weight on his heels, and took him down, and by the time I arrived the job was finished and my hands were empty and a man was on his back looking up at me.

The *controlled* was the only part I contributed. Somewhere in the middle of it, whatever runs that machinery let me put a hand on the throttle, and I have thought about that a great deal since. It let me. It could have not.

I stood there on a mat in a forest with fifty people watching and understood, in a way the clinical never managed, what they actually put in me.

The DI didn't praise. He narrated, voice cutting through the damp air while the platoon stood rigid with their eyes fixed on nothing.

"Observe. The hammer is heavy. The arm is built to carry it. A tool for every task." He let the silence hang. "Briggs, firewatch. The rest of you, look alive - at your own damn jobs."

*The arm is built to carry it.*

It was a visceral, undeniable lesson. My *different* was a tactical specification, and noticing it was a punishable lapse.

The recruits learned. The stares didn't stop; they went covert, fleeting, followed by an immediate paranoid snap back to whatever they were supposed to be doing.

I wasn't a spectacle anymore. I was a hazard. A distraction hazard.

It was the most anonymity I'd had since I got there. I'll take it. That's the sentence I keep arriving at and I've stopped fighting it: they turned me into a piece of equipment that fifty men were punished for perceiving, and I'll take it, because the alternative was Fido.

---

The only respite was food.

The chow hall was a haven of shocking greasy abundance. The food was, against all odds, awesome. Giant portions of pasta, real eggs, mountains of potatoes. Calories as fuel, and we were furnaces. My metabolism and my size demanded a shocking amount of it. I ate with mechanical focus, often going back for seconds while others picked at their plates. Even the MREs were acceptable in their weird vacuum-sealed way.

At the table, for fifteen minutes at a time, we weren't maggots. We were hungry animals refueling in silence.

*We.* Fifteen minutes a day, three times, and it's the only place in this account where I get to use that word about a room with other people in it.

I sat alone, as always, at the end of a table. But the space around me had stopped feeling like exile and started feeling like a cleared range. Nobody looked too long. Nobody sat too close. I could eat in peace, the monstrous rifle leaning against the table like a silent guardian.

I was the one who carried the thirteen-pound answer, and I was built on a scale that made it look logical. And if you were smart, you kept your eyes on your own tray.

I took a bite of gravy-soaked meatloaf. It was fuel. For now that was enough.

The rest was just physics, and I was finally starting to feel like I was built for the equation - which is a sentence I would have found horrifying two months earlier, and which I thought that evening with something I'd have to call contentment.

The silence of their averted eyes was the closest thing to respect I was likely to get. I did the arithmetic on that too, at that table, with my mouth full.

It came out yes.
