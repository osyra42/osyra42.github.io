# Chapter 1 - The Application

"Hey. My name is Osyra. This is my video application."

I held up the piece of paper. It bleached the screen - a stark white rectangle in the dark of my room, and the monitor threw it back at me off the inside of my glasses, a bright bar sitting across everything I looked at. The polish on the lenses catches everything in a room this dark. I'd noticed it while setting up and left it. Let the light do what it wants. My hood was up. Between the hood and the glare there was nearly nothing of my face left to see, which was the arrangement I'd landed on after an hour of trying arrangements.

"I'm told someone put my name forward," I said. "That you already know the broad strokes, and you'd rather hear the rest from me. Fine."

"My parents had nothing-I was one mouth too many. They kept me anyway until I was six, and then a school nurse wrote down what that had cost me, and the state took it from there. Foster care, one house after another. Until I was sixteen. Then the man who was supposed to be keeping me sold me to a facility."

I stopped there. Longer than I meant to. I'd written that sentence down and said it out loud maybe forty times in the two hours before I hit record, sanding it flat, taking the feeling out of it word by word until it was just a series of events that had happened to a person. It still cost something to say into a lens. I looked down, and my hair swept past my glasses, and I put my eyes back into the camera and made my face do nothing.

"They ran illegal experiments. Hybridization-splicing animal into human and seeing what lived. I was a line item. A body nobody who mattered would come looking for."

"They had charts of my development, my reflexes, my cellular response. Most of the others didn't survive what they were given. I did. When the place burned down I walked out, and I lived like an animal for the better part of two years-started in the woods, ended up on the street-taking what I needed, answering to no one. Long enough to learn I could."

I took a breath and held it a second before I let it go.

Here was the problem I'd been working the whole time, and I want to be honest that I never solved it, I only ran out of night. They already knew. Somebody in the smoking circle had put my name in and that name came with the rest of it attached, so the ears were not information. They were theater. The only question on the table was who got to do the reveal - them, in a file, in an office, with my photograph on a table between two people who'd never met me. Or me. Once. On my own terms, in my own room, with my own hands.

That's the entire reason I made the video. Not to tell them anything. To be the one holding the hood.

My hands came up off the desk and took the sides of it.

"This is what they made."

I pulled it back in one motion. My hair came down. My ears came up into the cool air of the room and twitched once, on their own, the way they do when they get the temperature of a place.

"Their serum. Chimera 9."

I smiled. There wasn't anything in it.

"I believe this covers the 'unique biological modifications' bullet point from your email."

Then I stopped recording, and watched the whole thing back, because I wasn't going to send a thing I hadn't inspected.

The final frame held on the screen a moment before it let go: red eyes staring out of the dark, black fur stark against black hair, a face I have spent eighteen years making sure nobody sees. It looked exactly like what it was. I made myself sit through it twice.

Then: UPLOAD COMPLETE. Two words on a black background.

I sat back in my chair. The room was quiet except for the hum of the computer. The monitor was the only light in it, laying a soft edge along my hood, the desk, the empty mug.

Five minutes. I didn't move. I'd like to report I was thinking. I wasn't. I was sitting in a dark room having handed strangers the one thing I've never handed anybody, and my body was doing what it does when there's nothing to fight - which is nothing at all, very tightly.

Then a chime. A notification slid into the corner of the screen.

I clicked it, and the dark of the room went out under the white of a new email.

FROM: Miranda Vasquez, MD <m.vasquez@titanreach.com>
SUBJECT: RE: Video Application - Verification Required
SENT: Tonight, 21:17

Applicant Osyra,

Your submission has been received and reviewed.

The nature of the presented physiological claims necessitates a preliminary verification to rule out digital or practical artifice before formal consideration can proceed.

You are required to attend a brief, live video conference at 09:00 tomorrow (your local time). You must present yourself exactly as you appeared in your submitted recording-same environment, same attire. You will be asked to perform simple visual confirmations at my direction.

Confirmation of your attendance is required within the hour.

If verification is satisfactory, you will be scheduled for a comprehensive clinical evaluation at our facility. This on-site evaluation serves as the final stage of the interview process.

This is a procedural necessity.

Miranda Vasquez, MD
Medical Screening Director
Titan Reach Consulting

Below the signature: a secured link and an alphanumeric passcode. The characters looked sharp. Final. A set of keys to a door I had just asked someone to open.

I read *procedural necessity* twice. My tail was curled around the chair leg and it gave one slow twitch, which is the only part of me that has never learned to lie.

No greeting. No reaction. I'd taken the hood off in front of them and they had come back with a scheduling requirement. Somewhere in that building a woman had watched a Chimeran pull her hood back and had thought: *rule out practical artifice.* I'd braced for revulsion. I'd rehearsed for it - I had answers ready for disgust, for fear, for the polite version of both. I had nothing ready for a form letter.

I turned it over and found, underneath, that I preferred it. Nobody had ever looked at what I am and been *bored* before. Bored means the room isn't about my face. Bored means there's a job.

My fingers were over the keyboard. The cursor blinked in the reply field. Out the window the city lights were doing what they always do, which is not care.

I could have not answered. That was still available. Close the laptop, be in the woods by morning, and nothing in my life would have to change, and I sat there long enough to know I was going to say yes and was just charging myself a minute for it first.

I hit reply. Typed one word.

Confirmed.

Sent it.

The screen dimmed to black, and there was only the green pulse of the power light, slow and steady in the dark, like something with a heartbeat waiting in the room with me.
