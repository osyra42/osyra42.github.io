# Chapter 3 - The Clinical

The lobby was all muted steel and frosted glass. It smelled of industrial cleaner and recycled air. The woman at the desk scanned my pass with a bored expression, handed me a stack of forms and a clear plastic cup, and pointed down a hall. "First door on the left. Fill the cup halfway, leave it in the wall cabinet. Then change into the gown in the waiting area. Open in back."

The first room was small and brutally functional. A toilet, a sink, a metal cabinet with a numbered slot. The instructions were laminated.

I did as I was told. The whole ritual humiliating in its banality, which I noted at the time and have thought about since - that they'd found a way to make it worse by making it *boring*. The lab at least had the decency to be a horror. This was a Tuesday.

In the alcove, a thin cotton gown. I shed my clothes, ears flattening at the exposure. The gown did nothing for my tail, which I had to thread through the special opening.

The special opening. Someone designed that. Someone at this company sat down and drew a gown with a hole in it, on a Tuesday, and sent it to a manufacturer.

The main waiting room had a few others in gowns. Two guys, maybe early twenties, slouched in plastic chairs - close-cropped hair, the wary stillness of military recruits.

"…they say it's just depth perception," one muttered, not looking at the other. "But my cousin went through, he said they're checking for latent psychic resonance. The 'third eye' test, he called it."

"Bull," the other grunted, picking at his thumbnail. "It's micro-expressions. They're weeding out sociopaths. Friend of a friend failed it, got flagged for 'empathy deficiency.'"

I sat as far from them as I could and pulled the gown tighter.

Nervous chatter. Nothing more. But I'll say this for them: they were both certain the test was secretly about something else, and they were both wrong about what, and they were closer than they knew. Whatever they were afraid of finding, they were afraid of finding it *in themselves*. Nobody in this building was going to look at either of them and write down *fully functional mechanism.*

I kept my head down. My hair made a poor curtain.

A nurse with a tablet called my name. She didn't blink at the ears. "Osyra. This way."

A long, bright hallway with multiple doors. The processing line. First a tiny booth where a technician took my blood pressure, then drew vial after vial, glass filling with dark liquid. "Hydration scan next," she said, handing me a towel for the crook of my arm.

The scan room was cold. I stood on a platform while a ring of light passed over me. "Arms out, please. Hold still." A digital hum, a flicker on a monitor showing a wireframe of my body. The technician's eyes went from the screen to my head, my tail, back to the screen.

He said nothing. He was very careful to say nothing. That's training - somebody taught this man not to react, and he was working at it, and I could see him working at it, which is worse than staring.

Then the coordination room. A different nurse, younger, with Vasquez observing from the corner, arms crossed. "Please walk heel-to-toe along this line." I did. "Now, on your toes only." I rose, tail extending for balance without being asked. "Now your heels." A shuffle. "Good. Now, touch your finger to your nose. Eyes closed." My finger found its mark. My hearing tracked the shift of Vasquez's weight on the floor behind me, because it does that, because it never stops doing that.

"Now," Vasquez said. "Jump. From a standing start. As high as you can."

I bent my knees and pushed. The ceiling tiles came at my face. I came down heavy, knees folding to take the load - a solid, audible *thud* you felt through the platform. Not graceful. A big frame coming back to earth and the floor knowing about it.

"Ground reaction force must be exceptional," the young nurse breathed.

"Proceed," Vasquez snapped.

A blur of small rooms and specific, bizarre commands. A hearing booth where I raised a hand at tones so high the technician kept checking his equipment. A vision test - lines of letters, then shapes on a depth-perception panel. "Now track the light without moving your head." The penlight moved. My eyes followed. My ears angled toward it on their own.

I heard Vasquez make a note.

Then a larger examination room. An exam table, an ultrasound machine, a tray of instruments. Vasquez came in, followed by a younger doctor, Chen, who couldn't stop staring.

"Ms. Osyra," Vasquez said. "We'll begin the musculoskeletal and specific integument assessment."

An hour of directed movement. "Full spinal extension. Hold." I arched back. "Plantar flexion to maximum." I rose onto my toes. "Now, rapid directional changes. Follow the laser." Ten minutes of lunging and pivoting after a red dot, the paper on the floor shredding under my bare feet.

My body performed with a cruel efficiency.

Here's the thing I'd been trying not to arrive at all day, and it arrived anyway, somewhere around minute six of chasing a dot across a floor for strangers.

I know this cadence. *Subject demonstrates exceptional lumbar pliability.* Page forty-two. I have read the sentence. I read all of them, later, when I was in a position to, and I can tell you the rhythm of it the way you'd know a song.

Vasquez was writing the same sentences.

Not similar ones. The same ones, in the same order, for the same reasons, because there is only one way to write down what a body can do and everyone who does it converges on it eventually. The men who did this to me at sixteen and the woman doing it to me at eighteen were not different kinds of people having different kinds of thoughts. They were the same profession.

The difference is the lab strapped me down. Titan Reach mailed me a pass and I drove myself here and I parked.

I chased the dot. I'm aware of what that means. I chased it well.

Chen approached with a handheld scanner. "May I?" he asked, hovering near my ears. I nodded stiffly. He scanned. "Dense cartilage, fully integrated." He produced a clicker. *Click.* Behind me. My left ear swiveled back instantly. *Click.* To the side. Both ears pinned the location.

That is not something I can stop. Understand that, because it's the only part of the afternoon I'd take back. Everything else I did on command. That one I did because a man clicked a clicker in a room and my body answered him.

"Auditory range is significantly extended," Chen reported.

"Eyes," Vasquez said. The ophthalmoscope light hit me. I stared into it and let the red burn. "Pigmentation is structural. Probable superior low-light vision."

"Tail mobility and strength."

This was the worst. Lift a small weight with it. Swish it through specific arcs. Demonstrate its counterbalance during a one-legged stance. The muscle at the base of my spine burned, and under that a shame I've never found the bottom of.

"Fascinating. A fully functional mechanism," Chen murmured to Vasquez.

*Mechanism.* He said it to her. Not to me. I was six feet away holding a weight off the ground with my spine.

After an eternity of pokes and scans and measurements, Vasquez gave a curt nod. "The physical is concluded. We will now move to the final portion of the evaluation." A glance at Chen. "Doctor, you may go. Send in Dr. Ellen."

Chen left. A moment later, a woman in her forties entered - calm, competent, carrying a new tablet. "Hello, Osyra. I'm Dr. Ellen. This part is standard for all applicants, but given your unique physiology, we need to establish a complete reproductive baseline."

The dread came in cold and immediate.

This is the part their notes were most feverish about. *Cyclical stability. Hormonal integration.* Whatever else the lab wanted, it wanted this, and it wanted it in a way that came off the page.

She started standard - heart rate, palpation of the abdomen. Her hands were professional. When she pressed near my ovaries a jolt went through me, deep, resonant, and she paused, gaze sharpening. "Interesting."

The internal ultrasound was next. Cold gel, intrusive probe. I stared at a crack in the ceiling tile. On the screen, grey shapes swirled. My own insides, looking alien.

"Uterine structure appears normal," she said quietly. "Ovarian activity is… significant. Follicular development is advanced."

She removed the probe and sat on a stool, facing me. Expression neutral, open. "Osyra, I need you to describe your reproductive cycle for me. In your own words. What do you experience?"

The question was a blade.

I had never said it out loud. Not once, to anyone, in two years of living with it. I had said nothing about it to anyone at all, ever, and here was a stranger with a tablet asking me to narrate it in a cold room with gel drying on my stomach, and the terrible part - the part I have never gotten past - is that she was the first person to *ask*. The lab measured it. She asked me what it was like.

"It's not a period," I started, the distinction clawing out of me. "It runs on a different clock. Once a year, in the fall - same stretch every time. I can mark it on a calendar. And it comes in stages."

"Walk me through them."

I kept my voice flat. Read it off like a report. "First I can't settle. Don't sleep. Everything gets too loud, too bright, too much - my own skin feels like it's listening. I get short with people. There's some bleeding, early on, a few days of it." A muscle moved in my jaw. "Then it sharpens into a pull. Toward-" I stopped. Made myself finish. "Toward closeness. Contact. For a few days I want it badly enough that I don't trust myself with it. Then it breaks and I crash - wrung out for a week. After that it's gone for another year."

She nodded, typing. "A seasonal estrous cycle. Photoperiod-triggered - your body reads the shortening daylight and runs the program on schedule. Textbook canid, grafted onto a human frame. The serum rewired your hypothalamic-pituitary axis." She leaned forward slightly. "Predictable, at least. That helps. But that drive window is a profound liability in the field if it isn't managed."

She wasn't judging. That's what she was doing instead: assessing a flawed asset.

"Given Titan Reach's operational parameters, we will provide a regimen of hormonal regulators. Non-negotiable for field readiness. The prescription and a monitoring kit will be couriered to you."

A chemical leash for the biological one.

And I sat there and turned it over and could not find the objection. That's what I want on the record. It *is* a liability. She's right. There are three days a year I would not trust myself with a weapon and a door, and I have known that since the first fall, and I have never had a solution for it, and a stranger solved it in ninety seconds with a courier.

The help and the leash were the same object. There was no version where I got one without the other. There has never been a version where I got one without the other - that's the whole architecture of my life, and Titan Reach didn't build it, they just read the schematic and priced it out.

"Understood," I said. The word was ash.

She stood. "Your body is operating on their schedule, Osyra. We're just helping you keep time."

A professional nod, and she left. I don't think she knew what she'd said. I think she meant it kindly.

Vasquez came back in, looking from her clipboard to me. The clinical detachment had hardened into something else. Verification. Conquest. "The Chimera 9 expression is valid and fully integrated. You have passed the final interview."

I dressed in the empty alcove, yanking my hoodie on, hood up the second it cleared my head. The clinic's smell was in my nose and on my skin. No relief. Just a hollow echo where relief should have been.

Passed.

I'd graduated from the lab to a bigger, better-funded cage. The tests were over. Somebody had looked at every part of me and written *valid* next to it.

Somehow that was the worst possible outcome, and it took me a long time to be able to say why: because I wanted it. Because when she said *you have passed* something in me sat up. Twelve years of being a line item - foster paperwork, then lab charts - and I still went warm at a stranger telling me I'd measured up.

They didn't have to break me. They just had to grade me.
