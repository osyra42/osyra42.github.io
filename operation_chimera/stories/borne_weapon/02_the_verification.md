# Chapter 2 - The Verification

I didn't sleep much. The kind of tired that sits behind the eyes and hums in the bones. My ears kept tuning to the wrong things - the drip of a tap, something scuttling in the walls, the low thrum of a city that never stops. When I did drift, it wasn't dreams. It was a replay of sensations: the cold press of a stethoscope, the bite of a needle, a scientist's voice noting observations in a flat, delighted tone.

That's what the delight does. It stays. A man can hurt you and the hurt will fade on schedule, but a man who is *pleased* while he does it puts something in the recording that doesn't come out.

I woke before my alarm, grey dawn seeping around the edges of the blinds. My hood was still up.

At 08:55 I was back in the chair. Dark room. The computer's glow. I'd cleared the desk, leaving only the monitor and the camera.

I clicked the link.

The window opened. Just me in a small square, and a larger, empty black one labeled *Dr. Vasquez - Titan Reach*. No picture. No voice. A full minute of nothing, which I understood was not an accident. You don't keep a person waiting by accident at 08:55 when you sent the invitation.

Then her square lit up.

She wasn't what I expected. Younger. Sharp features, dark hair in a severe bun, glasses reflecting her own screen so her eyes were invisible. White lab coat over a black turtleneck. Her background a blur of sleek grey office.

"Osyra," she said. Same voice as the email. Cool. Precise. A diagnostic tool. "Confirm your environment is identical to that of your submission."

I panned the camera slowly - dark walls, empty desk. "It is."

"Good. Posture, please. Sit up straight. Remove your hood."

My hand stopped for a fraction of a second at the edge of the fabric.

Then I pulled it back. My hair fell loose. My ears came up and pivoted forward, tensing toward the laptop speaker, because that's where the voice was and they do that without consulting me. In my own square in the corner of the screen I watched them do it. I watched her gaze lock onto them.

She didn't comment.

"Shoulders," she said. "I need to observe the integration at the trapezius and cervical spine. Rotate them. Forward, then back in a full, slow circle."

It felt absurd. Like a warm-up for a workout video. I did it - rolled them forward, feeling the muscle move under the skin, then back in a wide circle. My tail, out of frame, flicked once.

"Again. Slower."

I complied. Total silence on her end except the faint tap of a keyboard.

"Now, touch your right ear. The outer cartilage. Pinch it gently and pull it forward. Hold it."

Something went hot in my chest.

This was worse than the shoulders, and it took me until my hand was already moving to work out why, because the shoulders were also nonsense and the shoulders hadn't done this. It's that a shoulder is a shoulder. Everyone has one. She was asking me to reach up and take hold of the part of me that is *not human* and present it to a camera with my own hand. Not to be examined. To do the examining to myself, on command, while she watched.

The fur was soft. The cartilage springy under my fingers. I pulled it forward. It felt like holding open a wound so someone can see in.

"Hold for five seconds. Release. Now the left."

I did it. My ears flattened back against my skull the moment I let go, on their own, and I let them, because I had run out of things I was willing to override for her.

"Eyes. Look directly into the camera. Without moving your head, track my finger."

A slender finger entered her frame, moving slowly left, then right, then up, then diagonal. My eyes followed. In my own square the red was blazing out of a dark room, which is what it does, which is the entire reason I don't own a front-facing camera.

"Pupillary response appears normal. Non-standard pigmentation confirmed," she murmured, more to herself than to me. "Final check. Bare your teeth for the camera. Canines specifically."

I pulled my lips back. A snarl I didn't feel. The points caught the light.

A pause. More typing.

"Satisfactory," she said. The word wasn't praise. It was a stamp. "The preliminary verification is complete. We are authorizing the in-person clinical."

She leaned slightly closer to her camera. "Report to the Titan Reach biomedical facility on 12th Avenue. Can you be there for a full-body physical evaluation at 14:00 today?"

Two o'clock. Today. No time to think. No time to back out.

Which was the design. I'd worked that out by then. You don't schedule a person five hours out because five hours is convenient; you do it because five hours is not enough time for the animal to talk itself down off the ledge it just climbed onto. She'd had my file since last night. She knew exactly what she was buying and exactly how long I'd hold still for it.

"It's a comprehensive panel. Blood work, advanced imaging, stress-testing of the modified physiognomy. Consider it a freshening up of your baseline data before field consideration."

*Baseline data.* There is a filing cabinet somewhere with my baseline data in it, taken when I was sixteen, by men who used that exact phrase.

*Field consideration.* My ticket out. My leash. Both of those words were sitting on the table and she'd put them there in the same sentence, and I had to decide right then which one I was going to look at.

"Is 14:00 acceptable?" A hint of steel under the calm.

My throat was dry. I managed one stiff nod.

"Verbal confirmation, please."

Of course. Of course verbal. A nod isn't a record.

"Yes," I said, and it scraped on the way out. "Two is fine."

"Good. An appointment confirmation and pass for the front desk will be sent to this email. Do not be late."

Her square went black. The meeting ended.

I sat there looking at my own reflection in the dark monitor. Ears still pinned. Shoulders aching from the pointless rotations.

The rotations were not pointless. That's the thing I keep coming back to. There's no clinical value in a shoulder circle over a video call; she couldn't have measured anything from that feed and she isn't the kind of person who does work with no value in it. She wanted to see whether I'd do it. Whether the thing on the other end of the line would take an absurd instruction from a voice in a box and comply, twice, and then reach up and hold its own ear out for inspection when told.

I passed.

That's what I keep coming back to. I passed, and the test was obedience, and the reward for passing is a building on 12th Avenue at two o'clock.

The clock read 09:07. Less than five hours until I reported to my next examination.
