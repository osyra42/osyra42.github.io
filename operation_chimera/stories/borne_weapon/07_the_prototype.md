# Chapter 7 - The Prototype

That was when a new voice cut through. Quiet, but carrying a strange deliberate intensity.

"Shut up, Evers."

It came from a recruit leaning against the lockers. Tall, leanly built, sharp watchful features, dark hair cropped close. His eyes - a pale crystalline grey - were fixed not on me but on Stumps. No anger in his face. Just cool assessment.

Stumps puffed up. "What'd you say, pretty boy?"

The lean one pushed off the lockers. He didn't move aggressively. He moved with a relaxed economy that was somehow worse. He glanced at me, gaze sweeping from my hidden ears to my posture and back to Stumps, and his eyes widened a fraction, like he was connecting dots only he could see.

"I said, shut up. You have no idea what you're looking at."

"I'm looking at a circus act," Stumps sneered.

The lean one shook his head slowly, a faint almost awed smile touching his mouth. He looked back at me, and this time the stare wasn't disgust or mockery.

It was fascination. Unvarnished. Awe.

"No," he said, loud enough for the others. A hush fell, eager for new drama. "You're looking at a prototype. I've heard whispers about this. Deep-bio black ops stuff. They don't mod for looks. They mod for function."

He took a step closer, eyes locked on me like I was a fascinating blueprint.

"Look at the base of the spine. Perfect for balance and kinetic transfer. The auricular mobility? 360-degree audio surveillance, no tech required. The ocular pigmentation? That's for low-light and thermal tracking."

He was making it up. I could hear him making it up - the cadence of a man assembling a theory out loud and falling in love with it as it comes. His conviction was magnetic and there was not one sourced fact in it.

He was also right. That's the joke. Everything he invented, they did on purpose. Not for the reasons he said and not for the war he imagined, but a room full of men did sit down and decide what my spine was going to be for. He got the answer right off a wrong proof, which is the most common way anyone has ever been right about me.

"They didn't make a pet. They built a super-soldier. And you're calling it names."

The silence in our corner was absolute. Stumps's bluster deflated into a dawning uncertain fear. The others stared at me with new eyes - not a monster now. A weapon. The mockery gone, replaced by wary calculating distance.

The lean one held my gaze a moment longer.

No pity in it. No kindness either. Just the cool approving gleam of a fanatic recognizing a superior piece of engineering. He gave me a slight, almost imperceptible nod.

Then he turned and walked away, leaving a void of tense quiet behind him.

---

I sat back down on my cot with his words going around in my head. *Super-soldier. Prototype. Built.*

He hadn't defended me. I want to be precise, because for about four seconds I thought he had, and those four seconds were embarrassing. He didn't say *she's a person, leave her alone.* He said *you're too stupid to see what that is.* He moved me from one shelf to another. Both shelves are in a display case.

But the stares had changed, and that was worth something. Not mocking now. Wary. Calculating. Stumps wouldn't meet my eyes at all.

Fear is a cleaner barrier than contempt. It's simpler. It gives you room.

I pulled my hood tighter. And somewhere under the flat surface I keep over everything, something pragmatic came together.

Here's the trade, laid out honestly, the way I laid it out then.

They will never let me be a person in this building. That option is not on the table and I did not put it there and I cannot put it there. What's on the table is *pet* or *weapon*. Those are the two shelves. Fido, or prototype.

Pet costs me everything and buys nothing. Weapon costs me the same thing - it costs being seen, it costs any chance that one of these two dozen people ever looks at me and thinks *her* - and it buys distance. Distance is the only currency in a room like that. I have spent my life shopping in stores that only carry two items and I have gotten very good at knowing which one is cheaper.

So: let them have the weapon. Lean into the rumor. Wear their awed fear like a cloak. They want to see a thing that was built? Fine. It's better than being a pet, a freak, a Fido. Inside these walls, performance is everything. I could get respect out of this, or at least a safe radius, by pretending to be exactly the monster they'd already decided I was.

It's a good trade. It's the best available trade. I made it in about thirty seconds on a cot in a gymnasium and I'd make it again.

I noticed, distantly, the way you notice weather through a window, that I was agreeing to be the thing the lab built. That the men who put their hands on me at sixteen would have found this outcome entirely satisfactory. That somewhere there's a file with my name on it and a column marked *disposition*, and I had just, voluntarily, at no cost to anybody, filled it in.

I noticed it and nothing moved. That's mine. Nobody drugged me into that. I have been doing it to myself for years and I am better at it than any chemist.

Boot camp hadn't officially started, and I was already alone in a room full of people. But now, at least, I had a role to play.
