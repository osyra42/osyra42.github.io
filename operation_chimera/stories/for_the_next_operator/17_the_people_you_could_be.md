# Chapter 17 - The People You Could Be

*Hand a person the better version of themselves and some will reach for it. Most will look at it, and set it down, and pick up the knife instead.*

The town was called Tidegate, a river crossing gone to rust, and the thing killing its people came up out of the water at night. A gillheart - long, pale, hooked jaw, eyes gone milk-white from living under the ice. It took four before we got there. It dragged them under and it fed slow. We found the fourth still tangled in the reeds with most of her ribs opened out like a book. That is the plain fact of it. Her name was Ada Voss and she had been a schoolteacher and she was thirty-one and there was not enough of her chest left to close her coat over.

The town had a foreman by then. Man named Coll. He had organized them, and I want you to understand I was glad of it when I walked in, because organization is rare and it is the thing that keeps people alive. He had them rationed and watched and he had a plan for the crossing. He was frightened and mean with it, and he had started deciding which of them were worth the food.

Here is the principle. It is the last one I have to give you, so hold it.

When you want a person to be better than they are, tell them they already are. Not flattery. You name the finer self out loud, you hand it to them like it is theirs, and you make them ashamed to be less than the thing you just called them. It works. It works on the worst nights on the worst people. I told Coll he was the reason this town still had a count to take. I told him a lesser man would have already run the ferry and left the slow ones on the bank, and that everyone here could see he was not that man. I said it flat. I said it like it was already true.

And he reached for it. I watched him do it. He straightened. He put the two he had written off back on the ration list. He waded out with me to the reeds and helped me carry Ada Voss up the bank in a tarp when his own people would not touch her, and his hands shook the whole way and he did not stop. That is what the principle buys you. For one night I gave a mean, scared man a better man to be, and he stood in it.

You will get that too. Do not let it lie to you.

Because the gillheart came back the second night, and it came up under the ferry where the children slept warm, and there was a moment - one - where the plan needed a body in the water to hold the winch line while the rest hauled. Cold black water, the milk-eyed thing somewhere in it. I told Coll to hold the line. I told him the man I had met would hold it.

He looked at me. He looked at the water. And he took a girl - a foster girl, no people to raise a noise, twelve maybe, the kind nobody counts - and he put the line in her hands and he pushed her off the boards himself.

I got her out. I got the gillheart too, both barrels through that pale hooked skull, and it thrashed and voided black in the shallows and went still and the town was saved. The girl lived. She has a scar around both wrists now like she wore the rope forever.

And Coll - I did not touch him. I want you to know that. I stood over him dripping and I gave him the last clean chance. I said, *that is not who you are.* One more time. I held out the better man.

He said, *she was nobody. You'd have done the same.*

He would rather be the other thing. That is the turn, recruit, and it is the same turn every chapter of this book has walked you to. It is not that they cannot be better. It is that better costs them something and cruelty is free and safe and warm, and offered the choice, again and again and again, in town after town, they set down the finer self I handed them and they pick up the knife, because the knife asks nothing of them but someone else's blood.

I was sold to a lab at sixteen by people who had every chance to be better and found it too expensive. So I know the arithmetic from the inside. Nobody is coming to be good for free. The better self is real, it is possible, it is right there in reach - and they will not pay for it.

So here is the whole book in one place, and then I am done.

You will learn to make them like you. To listen, to leave them their dignity, to hand them the person they could be. It all works, and none of it saves them from what they would rather do to each other, and it will hollow you out to watch.

There is one thing that would work. I have thought about it every mission of my life. If I simply stood aside - if I let the gillheart come, let the milk-eyed things rise in every town, one shared horror with teeth for all of them - they would hold each other's lines. They would be decent. Nothing unites the small and cruel like a monster at the door. I could give them that. I could give them each other, by giving them me stepping back.

I will not.

That is not our job.

I do not do this because it means anything. It does not. There is no meaning waiting out there to be found; there is only what a person decides to carry. Everyone else at that table set their meaning down because it was heavy. This is the one I picked up and would not put down: I stand in front. No one goes past me into the dark. Not the town that calls me monster, not the girl with rope scars, not the foreman who would drown her twice.

They will not be better. I go anyway.

That is the last thing I have to give you, and it is the only thing that was ever mine to assign.

Hold the line. It is yours now.

- Osyra
