# Chapter 5 - Glad to See You

*Warmth disarms. A real, unforced, glad-to-see-you softens people faster than any order you can give. Keep your face open and your voice easy and they'll follow you out of a burning building. Just know that on you, warmth reads differently, and there's nothing you can do about that but keep offering it.*

Fear makes people stupid and slow and dangerous, to you and to themselves, and the fastest tool against fear is warmth. Not authority - authority makes a frightened person dig in. Warmth. An open face, an easy voice, the animal signal that says *I am glad to see you and you are safe with me.* People read it below the level of thinking. It gets under the panic and turns it down. You'll use it more than your rifle.

I'll tell you where I learned its limit.

A shifter had been wearing people in a suburb - one of the ones that takes a face and the memories that come with the face, so by the time you find it, it's been three neighbors and a mailman and it knows everyone's kids' names. Nasty work, the kind where the town's real horror isn't the thing, it's that they can no longer trust a single face on the street. I cleared it. Ugly, but I cleared it, and the last body it had been wearing came apart when it died the way they do, so there was a thing on the lawn that had been a man that morning and was now mostly not shaped like one, and a street full of people who'd watched their neighbor's face slough off a thing I'd shot.

There was a woman on that street, older, who had gone somewhere behind her eyes - shock, the flat kind, the kind that doesn't come back on its own. She was standing in the open with the sprinklers still going, soaked, not feeling it. You don't grab a person like that; grabbing tips them the wrong way. You warm them out. So I went to her slow and I put everything I had into my face, the whole glad-to-see-you, *there you are, I've got you, we're okay now,* and I kept my voice like something you'd use on a spooked horse, and I reached out easy.

And she looked up into the open, warm, glad face of the six-and-a-half-foot thing that had just torn her neighbor into wet pieces on the grass, and what my warmth did to her was confirm it. Because here is what warmth is, if you're built like me: it's a predator that isn't hungry right now. That's all a frightened primate can read on a face like mine when the ground is covered in what I'm capable of. The soft voice didn't say *safe* to her. It said *this one plays with its food.* She started screaming in a way she hadn't managed for the actual monster, screaming and scrambling backward through the sprinklers and she went down on the wet concrete of her own driveway and cracked her head doing it, trying to get away from the warmest thing I know how to be.

They trusted the other operator that came in behind me. He was human-shaped and armed and he never softened his face once, all business, barking orders, and they *loved* him for it, they did everything he said, because a hard human face in a crisis reads as competence and a soft inhuman one reads as appetite. I stood at the edge of that and did the math I'm giving you now: the warmth works. It works on everyone. It just doesn't work coming from me, not when they can see what I am, and there is no version of my face soft enough to outrun what they know my hands do.

So here's the lesson, and it splits two ways depending on what you are. If you're built to look like help, use the warmth without a thought - it's the best de-escalator there is and it'll save lives that force would lose. But if you're built like me, if you're the kind of operator they'll call a monster, then know that your warmth is going to land as threat as often as not, and offer it *anyway.* Not because it'll work. Because the one time in ten it reaches someone - the child too young to have learned yet what your face is supposed to mean, the person too far gone to be afraid - that one is worth the nine who flinch. You keep your face open into a world that reads it as a weapon. That's not weakness. That's the most stubborn thing you'll ever do, and you do it for strangers who'll thank the man who yelled at them and run screaming from the one who was gentle.
