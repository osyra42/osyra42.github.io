# Chapter 6 - I Know Your Names

*Learn the person. Ask after the bad knee, the dog, the son the water took. People open for anyone who bothers to remember them. The trouble isn't that it fails. The trouble is that it works, and then it's yours to carry, and it doesn't come back off.*

Nine days in Saltmarrow, which is long for me. You don't interrogate a frightened town; you ask them about themselves, and they hand you everything. I asked the harbormaster, Toll, about his rope-burned hands and let him talk for an hour. I learned the widow Serafine's drowned boy was named Eddan. I learned the innkeeper's wife was pregnant and hiding it from a husband who drank. I keep things - the lab tested my memory the way you test a rope, with load, until they knew what it held, and now it holds whether I want it to or not.

It worked. It always works. They gave me the docks and the tide tables and the honest count of the missing, and I took the eel-thing they called the Drownfather in the shallows and held its jaw open until the charge in its throat went.

Here is what no one told me, and what this chapter is actually about, and it is not that they turned on me - they didn't, Saltmarrow was grateful, they waved me off the dock. It's smaller and it's worse.

You cannot learn a person for the length of a mission and then set them down. I have tried. The interest is a tool when you pick it up and it is not a tool you get to put back, because to learn someone is to decide they matter, and that decision doesn't have an off. I meant to learn Toll's hands the way you learn a lock - functionally, to get the door open. But you can't spend an hour inside a man's forty years and come out with only the parts you needed. I came out with Toll. I came out with Eddan, a boy I never met, drowned before I got there, whose name I will have until I die because I learned it to comfort his mother for one afternoon and there is no procedure for giving a name back. I have a dead child in my head that I picked up as a lever and cannot put down.

Multiply that. Every town, nine days, a lock's worth of people learned and none of them returnable. I am carrying hundreds. The baker's girl afraid of the church. The innkeeper's wife's secret, which was never mine and is mine forever now. A man in a valley three years ago who fed stray cats and is certainly dead and lives in me because I asked him one kind question to get a map. I did not keep these people because I loved them. I kept them because the job made me learn them and learning is a door that only opens.

That's the cost, and it's the whole lesson, and it's got nothing to do with anyone being cruel to me. The principle works perfectly. It saves the town every time. And every time it works it puts another handful of people into a house that only I live in - a house you'll build too, if you're any good at this, room after room of the saved and the incidental and the already-dead, all of them learned on purpose, none of them coming to visit, because to them you were a monster who was briefly useful and the last thing they want is to be kept in a wolf's head. You'll stand in that house at the end, saying the names over. It is the loneliest thing this job makes, and it is made entirely of your own kindness, and there is no one to blame for it, which is the part that will get you.

So learn them. It's how you save them; there's no other way in. Just know going in that you are not borrowing these people. You are adopting every one, permanently, unilaterally, and most of them will never know your name the way you'll know theirs, and you will be the sole custodian of a crowd that would flinch to learn it was being kept.

Eddan. Six years old, drowned in Saltmarrow before I ever arrived. I never saw his face. I'll have him the rest of my life. Nobody did that to me. I did it myself, reaching for a lever, and the lever turned out to be a person, and that is what interest is - and you're going to do it too, and I can't teach you how not to, only that it's coming.
