# Chapter 13 - Braver Than You Are

*Tell a crowd they are brave and they will be brave - for exactly as long as you are watching them. Then the lights go, and so does the bravery.*

The town was called Emberthroat, a mining strip built into the throat of a dead volcano, and the thing living in the vent shafts was a burrower - a Chimeran the file called a Delving Maw, four meters of segmented muscle with a mouth that opened in four directions like a flower and teeth set in rings all the way down. It came up through the floors. It took a man out of his bed on the third night and left the wife screaming into a hole in her own bedroom, and by the time I arrived the town had done what frightened towns do. They had stopped being a town. They were three hundred people in a cannery, packed shoulder to shoulder, and they had already started sorting themselves into who ate and who watched others eat.

I want to teach you the trick I used, because it works, and because I need you to understand exactly how far it works and no further.

You do not tell frightened people to behave. You tell them they already are. You give them a name too big for them and you make them ashamed to be smaller than it. I stood on a crate in that cannery and I looked at three hundred faces that had decided they were animals, and I told them they were the bravest town on the ridge. I told them I had seen barricades break and I had never seen a barricade like theirs. I told them the man the Maw took had held the line so the rest could run, and that his neighbors would not dishonor that by clawing each other over water rations. None of it was true. He had died in his sleep. There was no line. I authored it standing on that crate, out loud, in front of all of them, and I watched it take.

And it took. That is the part I need you to feel before I ruin it. A woman gave her ration to a child that was not hers. Two men who had been at each other's throats an hour before carried the wounded together, gently, competing now to be seen being gentle. An old miner stood at the shaft mouth with a pry bar he could barely lift and told me he would hold it so the doctor could work. They rose. I called them brave and they climbed up into the word to meet me, and for six hours Emberthroat was the town I had invented for it. I have made buildings burn and I have never made anything as fast or as clean as I made those people good with a lie.

I killed the Maw at dawn. Drew it up the main vent with a heat lure and put forty rounds down its ringed throat as the mouth bloomed open for me, and it came apart in wet segments across the cannery floor, each ring still chewing at the air on its own for a minute after, teeth grinding on nothing. It reeked. The old miner cheered with his pry bar over his head. The woman with the borrowed child wept. I stood in the gore of the thing I had killed and I let myself, for one hour, believe the crowd was what I had called it.

Then the relief convoy was two days out and the radio went quiet and the eyes came off them.

Here is what happens when no one is watching, and you should write it in your hand so you do not forget it in mine. The gentle men went into the dead man's house in the dark and stripped it, and when the widow tried to stop them one of them broke her jaw with the flat of a shovel and left her in the same room with the same hole in the floor. The woman who gave away her ration took it back that night and three more with it, from a family too sick to hold them, and the child she had fed watched her do it. The old miner with the pry bar - the one I would have died beside - I found him behind the cannery holding it over a girl who had said no to him, and he was not brave anymore because there was no one left to see him be it, and bravery had only ever been the thing he wore in front of me.

I broke his arm at the elbow and took the bar. He looked up at me like I had betrayed him. Like I had changed the rules. And the thing is, from inside his skull, I had. I put the noble man in him. I built him, out loud, on a crate, because I needed him for an hour. I never asked whether there was anything underneath to hold the shape once I stopped looking. There was not. There is not, in most of them. The virtue you appeal to is a costume you cut and hand them, and they will wear it beautifully while the audience holds, and they will fold it up and set it down the second the room goes dark, because it was never theirs. It was yours. You wrote it. You are the only one at that table who assigns a person more worth than they can carry, and worth does not transfer just because you said it loud.

Vanity logged it as a clean extraction. Monster killed, town saved, casualties acceptable. I did not correct her.

So use it. It is one of the best tools you will ever hold. Stand on the crate, name them braver than they are, and watch them climb up to be it while it matters. Just do not stay for the part where they come down. Do not let yourself believe the costume was the man. And when one of them looks at you, betrayed, because you broke the noble thing you swore they were -

remember you were the one who dressed them in it. Then keep walking.
