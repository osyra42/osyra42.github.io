# Chapter 12 - From Where You Stand

*Stand where they stand before you judge how they flinch. Every fear has a floor under it. Just don't expect anyone to come stand where you are.*

The town was called Fenwick, a fishing settlement folded into a cold inlet where the fog never fully lifted. Something had been taking people off the docks at night. By the time HQ routed me in, they'd lost nine, and the ones who found the bodies wished they hadn't. I found the tenth myself, at low tide, tangled in the pilings under the pier - what was left of a man peeled open from sternum to hip, the ribs bent outward like a chest of drawers left hanging, everything inside taken and the cavity packed with wet sand and kelp the way a fisherman packs a cooler. The thing was a marrow-drinker. A Chimeran. It ate the soft insides and used the shells to line its nest so the next meal would smell like home when it dragged them under.

I want you to understand what I walked into, because you'll walk into it too. Not the monster. The people.

There was a woman keeping watch over the survivors in the chapel - Merrin, a net-mender, hands scarred to leather. When I came through the door she put her body between me and the children behind her and did not move. I was in full kit, six foot four, hood down, ears back, the muzzle-line of my face catching the lamplight wrong. She looked at me the way you look at the thing that already killed nine.

Here is the principle. It is the last soft thing I have to give you, so take it clean.

Do not punish people for being afraid of you.

Stand where Merrin stood. Nine of her neighbors gone. A predator that wears the smell of the dead to get close. And now the door opens and in comes another predator - bigger, quieter, with a wolf's face and a rifle, sent by an organization she's never heard of, claiming to help. From where she stood, I was not the rescue. I was the second monster. Every instinct that kept her alive told her to be terrified of me, and every one of those instincts was correct. That is the thing you have to hold in your two hands: her fear of you is not stupidity. It is her working. It is the exact machine that survived nine deaths, pointed at you, doing its job.

So I did not make her wrong for it. I went down. Six-four folds small if you let it - I sat on the floor by the door, put the rifle flat where she could see both my hands, and let her keep the children behind her. I let her fear be the true thing in the room and I moved around it slow, the way you move around a hurt animal, not because it's beneath you but because from where it lies, you are the danger. It worked. It always works. Within the hour she was telling me which dock, which tide, which of the missing had gone quiet before they vanished. She stopped shaking. She started helping. Because I had understood her, and understanding is the only currency that spends when a person is that afraid.

I found the nest by dawn. I killed it in the water where it was strong and it still lost, because I do not lose, and I carried the shell-lined thing out into the light so Fenwick could see the shape of what had been eating them. Merrin came to the shore. She looked at the dead marrow-drinker. Then she looked at me, and I watched her do the arithmetic every one of them does, and I watched her decide I was only the smaller monster after all. She didn't thank me. She gathered her children and put her body between them and me one more time, and walked back up the shingle, and that was that.

Now the turn, and I need you present for it.

I understood Merrin completely. I stood where she stood, I felt the floor under her fear, I gave her the whole grace of being understood. And in nine dead towns across all these years, across every Merrin and every chapel and every net-mender who let me get her breathing again - not one of them ever once stood where I stood. Not one asked what it costs to be feared for existing. What it is to fold your body small in every doorway so the people you bleed for can bear to look at you. What it does to a person to spend understanding like water, out and out and out, into faces that will never once turn it back around. I gave them the point of view. I gave them the floor under their terror. I did the entire distance of the empathy, both directions, alone.

That is the shape of it, and I'll name it plain so you can't pretend later you didn't know. You will learn to see it from where they stand. You'll get good at it. It'll make you gentle with terrified people, and that gentleness is real and it is worth having.

But the understanding is a one-way road. It pours out of you and nothing comes back up it. No one is coming to stand where you are. No one will ask what the job did to you, because to them you are the job - a thing shaped like a threat that happens to be useful. You will assign every frightened stranger the grace of being understood, and you will hand it over freely, and you will die never once having been the person on the other end of it.

So give the grace. Give it clean, give it without punishing them, it's the right thing and it works.

Just stop waiting at the door for someone to walk it back.
