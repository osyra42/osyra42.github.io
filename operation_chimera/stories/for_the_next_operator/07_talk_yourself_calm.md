# Chapter 7 - Talk Yourself Calm

*Let them empty the whole magazine of it. Full attention, no interruption, and a frightened person will talk themselves back into a person. Just understand what you become the moment they do - you become the one who knows.*

Cinderslope was a mining strip on a dead mountain, and the thing coming down the shafts folded its jaw outward like a hand opening and took nine men off the lower galleries before we got the call. I went down to seal the last one and found a survivor at the bottom, a shift foreman named Harl, wedged in a supply nook behind the blasting mats, and my lamp found the rest of the gallery in the same sweep that found him.

It hadn't finished. It had been interrupted mine after mine, so the gallery was a half-eaten meal held in the cold - a man still in his harness with everything below the ribs drawn out down the incline in a long wet rope, another folded backward over an ore cart the wrong way, a third who'd crawled a distance on what still worked and wrote the distance on the floor behind him. Harl had been down there with that for eleven hours. Lamp for the first three, then the dark, then just the sounds, because a mountain won't let a body cool quietly.

You'll meet him, some version. The survivor gone past screaming into a flat low shaking. You can't pull them out; pull and they lock. So you sit in the muck at their level, you put the rifle down where they can watch you do it, and you shut up and let the silence get heavy enough that they fill it. That's the tool. It's real and it's the best of what I've got.

Harl filled it. And here's the part you have to understand about *how* the calming actually works, because the trap is inside the mechanism, not bolted on after. A person doesn't talk themselves calm by reciting facts. They calm by confessing - by finding the one thing they can't hold alone and handing it to another mind so they're not the only one carrying it. The steadying *is* the unburdening; there's no version without it. And the thing Harl handed me, because it was the heaviest thing and so the thing that had to come out for his heart to come down, was this: when the jaw opened the first time, he put his hand flat on the back of a young miner named Ost and shoved, and bought the nook with him. He watched Ost go into the opening hand of that mouth. He told me because he had to tell someone or stay a held breath forever, and I was the someone, and it worked - his pulse came down off the ledge, hundred-ten, ninety, seventy, and he walked out of that mountain on his own feet a person again.

I did that. The listening did that. It was kindness and it was competent and it saved him.

And it damned me, and here is why it was never going to go any other way, so you learn the actual lesson and not a complaint about people. Harl walked out with his confession *outside of himself now*. He'd survived the eleven hours by putting the hand-on-Ost's-back into another mind - mine. But a man cannot go home and build a life on top of a murder that is sitting, fully known, in the head of a creature the whole county already calls a monster and would believe anything of. I was the only witness to the worst thing he ever did, and I'd become that witness *by* comforting him. He didn't calm down and then decide to betray me. The calming handed him a new unbearable thing - that his guilt now lived in me - and there was exactly one way to make that survivable, which was to make me unbelievable. So when the inquest came, he stood up and described the monster in the dark, built me out of the same images he'd handed me in the nook, arranged so the jury couldn't tell the thing that ate the gallery from the thing that sat in it with him. Not out of malice. Out of the same instinct that made his pulse come down - get the unbearable thing out of yourself and into a form the world will discard. First he put it in me by confessing. Then he had to put me somewhere it could be thrown away.

The kindness built the knife and then required its use. I couldn't have listened him calm without receiving the confession, and he couldn't have lived with the confession received while the receiver was still credible. Every good thing I did in that nook was load-bearing for the thing he did on that stand. There's no clean version. If I'd stayed silent and let him lock up, he'd have died down there and taken Ost's death with him, and eleven hours of a man screaming would've been the only justice Ost got. I chose to save Harl. Saving him meant learning what he did. Learning what he did meant I had to be destroyed, because a confessor with my face cannot be left standing.

So do it anyway. Sit in the muck, put the rifle out of your hands, let them talk themselves calm. You'll save people who cannot afford to have been saved by you - and the better you are at it, the more of them will need you gone afterward, not because they're ungrateful, but because you'll be the one who knows. That's the tool. It heals them by making you the keeper of the thing they can't keep, and no one gets to un-know a thing, so the keeping is permanent and the keeper is a liability the moment they're calm enough to notice.

Ost is in me now too, of course. Harl handed him over and went home. I got the mountain, the gallery, the boy shoved into the mouth, and the man who needed me erased for having caught him. That's what listening costs. Learn it before you sit down, because you're going to sit down anyway.
