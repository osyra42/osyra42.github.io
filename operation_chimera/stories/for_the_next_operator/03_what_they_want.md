# Chapter 3 - What They Want

*You can't move people by telling them what you need. You move them by finding what they want and opening the door to it. Speak to their desire, never yours. Just be sure you know what they actually want before you open it - because you will not always like what's on the other side, and you'll have used everything you have to get it open.*

You'll spend a lot of nights trying to make people do the sensible thing, and you'll learn the sensible thing has almost no pull on anyone. "Evacuate" is a word about what *you* need; it slides right off. People move toward what they want. So when a place has to empty and someone won't go, stop naming the danger and find the thing they're staying for, then show them that leaving is how they keep it. Everyone who won't leave is staying for something. It's never the reason they give you first. Find it.

This is the one I'd erase from myself if I could keep the lesson without the memory. I can't, so you get both.

A stalker had moved into the treeline outside a farm road - the slow kind, works a family for weeks before it takes anyone. The whole road cleared but one house. A man in the doorway, shotgun that would've annoyed the thing and nothing more, telling me he wouldn't leave his land. Land wasn't it; his eyes kept cutting back into the house while his mouth said land. So I did the thing this chapter is about. I asked who was inside. His daughter, he said, and something in how he said it should have stopped me and didn't - grown, but *delicate*, that was his word, didn't go out, hadn't in years, couldn't be moved easy. He wasn't defending acreage. He was refusing to leave her. I read a father who loved his child too much to run, and I built my whole approach on it. Told him I'd carry her out myself, he'd be beside her the entire way, that leaving was the only way he got to keep her. Gun down inside a minute. Both of them out before midnight. I felt the clean thing you'll learn to chase, the feeling of a door opened exactly right.

Here's what I hadn't read. There was a bolt on her door. On the outside. I saw it going in and my mind filed it under *she wanders, he worries* because that was the story I'd already decided to be true, and you will do this, you will fit every detail to the story you picked in the first thirty seconds unless you fight yourself, and I didn't fight myself, and there was a bolt on the outside of a grown woman's door.

She told me in the staging area, once there were lights and other bodies and walls that weren't his. First walls that weren't his she'd stood inside since she was twelve. She told me flat, no shake in it, because the size of a thing wears the inflection off it after enough years - the way you'd report a road closure. She said her father had been coming into that room at night since she was a girl. That "delicate" meant he'd told her since she was small that she was too fragile for the world so she'd never think to try the door, and that the door had a bolt for the nights she stopped believing him. She said there had been a baby once, early, and that it had not been born in a hospital and was not spoken of again, and she said the word she used for what he did to her body was the plain clinical word, said it evenly, and then said the part that I still carry: that when she'd heard there was a monster in the trees, she had gotten down on the floor of that bolted room and prayed - really prayed, hands and everything - that it would come to the house. That it would come through her window first. She had wanted the thing in the treeline to reach her before he did one more time, because the thing in the treeline would at least be finished with her when it was done.

And I had carried her out of that. Personally. Gently. I had put my body between her and the only thing on that property that might have ended it, and I had spoken so beautifully to her father's *want* that he'd walked wherever I pointed, and the want I'd found and used as my handle - the love, the won't-leave-my-child - was the machinery of the exact thing she'd been on her knees begging a monster to interrupt. The stalker never touched her. It circled for weeks and the worst thing on that farm had a key to her door and my full, competent, well-spoken cooperation.

I put it right after. As right as it goes, which with a thing like that is not much and is not fast, and "right" mostly means he does not get to be anywhere near her again and she gets people whose job is to help her carry it. I made sure of that part with my own hands. It doesn't unbolt the years. Nothing does.

So here is the lesson with its teeth in. Speaking to what people want *works* - it is the strongest handle there is, because want is the truest thing in a person, and I moved that man like water because I found his. But the truest thing in a person is not always a thing that should be fed, and love is not a safe word - love is just a want that's learned to call itself something noble, and some of the worst things a person will ever do, they do in its name and believe every word. You will open the door because you found the want and the want was real. Sometimes the want is a horror that has been wearing the face of a father for twenty years, and you will serve it flawlessly before you understand what you served.

Read deeper than the surface want. Ask *why* the thing they're staying for keeps them, not just that it does. Look at the door and notice which side the bolt is on. And when you get it wrong - you will get it wrong - go back, fix what can be fixed, and do not let the guilt talk you out of the tool. The tool is still right. You just have to be more afraid of what people want than you were before. Be afraid of it. That fear is the only thing that will make you read them all the way down to where they actually live - which, the night it counts, is the difference between the door you should open and the one you should burn.
