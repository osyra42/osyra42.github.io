# Red Light District

*A thought experiment: what a Chimera partner would even look like. Different variant, so the math doesn't work - that's the point of running it. First person. Unpublished seed for the romance mini-book.*

The bar didn't have a name on the outside, which was the point. Inside, the lights were low and red, and that was the point too - under red light, red eyes don't show. It's the only room in the city where I don't wear the contacts. I found it the way I find everything: by following the smell of people hiding the same thing I am.

He was already at the back when I came in. I knew before I saw him, because the place went quiet in the particular way a room goes quiet around a predator the other predators respect. A cup of something untouched in front of him. A stillness I recognized, because it was mine.

When he looked up his eyes were the same impossible red, and slit down the middle like a cat's.

"Chimera-Four," he said. Not a question. "I can smell the wolf on you from here."

"Nine." I sat without being asked.

Neither of us pretended not to look. He didn't flinch at my size. He didn't measure himself against it either, which is the other thing men do, and the one I've never found a way to answer. He registered me and went back to being still, and something in my chest I'd kept clamped for a decade came loose a quarter-turn.

We didn't talk about the labs. That's the etiquette. You don't ask which facility, which guardian sold you, how many of your cohort came out the other side. You talk around it, in the shorthand of people who already know the shape of the hole.

"You hide the eyes," he said eventually. "Out there."

"Contacts."

"I wear sunglasses. People think I'm an asshole." A flicker at the corner of his mouth - not quite a smile, but the place one would go. "Better than the alternative."

I found, to my own irritation, that I'd leaned in.

Up close he smelled of nothing I had to be afraid of. No fear-sweat. No adrenaline. Nobody bracing to run from me or to win against me. Just a tired person who'd survived the same machine and come out still able to make a joke about sunglasses.

It was the absence of a thing. That's what got me - not what he was doing, what he wasn't. No performance. I have spent my whole life in rooms where everybody is performing at me, and I've gotten so good at reading the performance that I forgot there was a version without one underneath.

"You're staring," he said.

"You're not running," I said.

"No," he agreed. "Why would I."

That was the whole of it.

Not a confession. Not a promise. I'm not built for those and I don't think he is either, though I want to be careful there - I don't know that. I know I'm not, and I know he didn't reach for one, and I have spent an hour deciding those are the same fact and they aren't.

The math I do know. Red eyes met red eyes, and a cat and a wolf make nothing between them but company. The rewrite made us different species twice over. There's no future here of the kind the world keeps score by, and I did that arithmetic before I sat down, the way I do all of it, and I sat down anyway.

Just this. A red room. Two survivors. The rare and ordinary relief of being a person somebody looked at and didn't look away from.

I slid the contacts case across the table and let him see me do it. Let him watch me decide, in front of him, not to hide.

That was the only thing I did all night that I'd call a choice. Everything else I could explain - the bar, the sitting, the leaning in. Not that. That one I made on purpose and I still couldn't tell you what I was buying.

He picked up his cup.

Outside, the city kept insisting on its rules, on its breeding and its belonging and its lists of what counts. In here, under the red light, none of it could reach us, and for one held breath that was enough.

I'd leave first, later. I always do.

But not yet.
