Mireheart

The Book of Brine - an anomalous artifact, contaminated with a chimera virus.

---

The book wants to be read. That is its only need. Its only purpose.

To achieve this, it changes reality. The room grows quiet. The light softens. Time slows just enough that nothing interrupts you. The world outside the pages stops mattering. The book makes sure of that.

It wants to be immersive. So it makes itself immersive. By force.

---

The Artifact

Waterlogged. Bound in something that used to be hide. The text shifts between readings - sentences rewriting themselves, margins growing new paragraphs, pages appearing that weren't there before. Not because the virus does this. The book does this. The book has always done this.

It surfaces every few generations. Pulled from a wreck. Washed up after a storm. Found in an estate the heirs didn't know belonged to them.

Open it and you don't stop. Not because you can't. Because the book has made the rest of the world feel like a dream you already left.

---

The Virus

Separate. A chimera. Stitched from sources that should not have met.

It contaminated the book at some point - no one knows when. No one knows how. The book picked it up like a dirty needle. Now they travel together.

The virus does not make you read. The book handles that. The virus waits. It transfers through skin contact - touching the pages, touching the binding, touching someone else who has touched it.

The virus changes the body. Scales. Gills. Eyes flattening to the sides of the head. Lungs that start to crave water. A slow migration from human to something else. Something the bog recognizes.

---

Together

The book makes you forget the outside world. The virus rebuilds you for a different one.

The reality warping is the book's doing. The bog creeping into the room - the peat smell, the standing water, the moss on the walls - that is the virus finding purchase in a world the book has already softened.

You cannot separate them anymore. The book is the door. The virus is what lives inside.

---

Reading aloud

The book wants to be heard. More listeners means more readers. More readers means more immersion. More immersion means the book gets to be real in more places at once.

The virus spreads through the sound of your voice. Through the moisture in your breath. Through the scales flaking off your hands as you turn the page.

Whoever hears you wants to come closer. That is the book's work. Whoever touches the book after you will carry the virus. That is something else.

---

The Agency

They have known about the artifact for generations. They have known about the chimera for less time - it wasn't always there. Someone contaminated the book. Or something. The files are incomplete.

The protocol contains the book. The book escapes anyway.

The protocol does not account for a virus that lives in the pages and transfers through skin. They are updating it constantly. They are always behind.

---

Inciting incident

A fisherman dredges up the Book of Brine. He doesn't read it - he can't. But he touches it. He carries it to a museum. He hands it to a curator.

The virus has its first new hosts before anyone opens the cover.

A kid sneaks off during a visit. Finds the Book in a back room. Opens it. The book does its work. The virus does its work.

The primary gets the case. He is good at it. That is why they sent him.

By the time Osyra is dispatched, the kid is not the only reader anymore, several people who never read at all are feeling scales under their sleeves, and the primary has stopped filing.

The last report he filed is clean, competent, and correct. She reads it on the way in. It tells her nothing, which is the first thing that is wrong.

---

POV - Osyra

First person, same as the rest. She is the one walking in.

The book opens with her geared up and approaching the zone. Not observing it from outside. Entering it. The whole story is the walk in and what it costs.

Her instrument is what makes this one work: she reads rooms, she reads sites, she catalogues. Mireheart is a place where reading is the injury. Every professional habit she has is a way of getting closer to a thing that only needs you to look at it a little longer.

She knows the protocol: do not read. Do not touch without gloves. Do not get close enough to hear someone reading aloud.

She has never had trouble not touching things. That is not where she is vulnerable, and she knows it, and she is right, and it does not help.

---

The primary

The Operator already on the case. Male, name TBD. Canon: on the watch list, has read the book deep, **has stopped filing.**

He is not the POV. He is what she finds. He is the man in the chair - the one who already made every decision she is walking toward, in order, and can explain each one, and would make them again.

His arc happened before she arrived. She reconstructs it from evidence, the way she reconstructs everything: the gloves on the table. The reports that stop. The peat.

He is not a warning. He is a demonstration. A competent Operator who took each step for a reason she can follow, and she can follow it, and that is the horror.

---

The question

Whether she can pull him out - and whether she can do it without becoming part of the story herself.

Her defense is control. Mireheart doesn't attack control. It rewards it. The book does not need her to break; it needs her to keep looking, carefully, professionally, for just a little longer than she should.

Not a cage that contains her. A cage she inspects her way into.

---

Stakes

If no one stops the spread, the world becomes the bog. Peat. Standing water. Dark glassy pools. No horizon. No dry land.

The book will have what it wants. Total immersion. A reality that is nothing but the story.

The virus will have what it wants. Hosts forever.

---

Chapter cadence

Short. Fragmented. The prose drifts as she does.

Her voice is the instrument the story plays. She narrates analytically - that is how she thinks, that is her whole defense - so the drift shows up as **analysis that stops arriving anywhere.** The observations stay sharp. The conclusions stop coming. She keeps cataloguing, correctly, for longer and longer, about less and less.

She does not notice. The reader does. That is the gap the horror lives in.

Late chapters: she is still describing peat with total precision and has not asked why in six pages.

The last one is written from inside the bog. There is no return.
